From the Cascades to Hawaii to a supervolcano hiding under a national park, the
United States has volcanic landscapes that rival anywhere on Earth. These ten
are the ones to know.

## 1. Kīlauea, Hawaii

One of the most active volcanoes in the world, where you can often watch lava
within a national park — volcanism made accessible.

## 2. Mauna Loa, Hawaii

The largest active volcano on the planet by volume, a shield so broad its scale
is hard to perceive from its own slopes.

## 3. Mount St. Helens, Washington

The 1980 lateral blast rewrote volcanology. The crater and recovering blast
zone are now an outdoor classroom.

## 4. Mount Rainier, Washington

A glacier-clad stratovolcano looming over Seattle — beautiful, and among the
most hazardous US volcanoes due to lahar risk.

## 5. Yellowstone, Wyoming

A caldera supervolcano whose geysers and hot springs are the surface signs of
an enormous magma system.

## 6. Mount Shasta, California

A massive, isolated Cascade cone, a mountaineering objective and a cultural
landmark visible for a hundred miles.

## 7. Lassen Peak, California

The other Cascade eruptor of the 20th century, ringed by mudpots and fumaroles
in its own national park.

## 8. Crater Lake (Mount Mazama), Oregon

A collapsed volcano holding the deepest, bluest lake in the US — a caldera you
can stand inside.

## 9. Mount Hood, Oregon

Portland's iconic stratovolcano, glaciated and steadily climbed year-round.

## 10. Haleakalā, Hawaii

A vast shield volcano whose summit crater is one of the great sunrise
destinations on Earth.

## How to visit US volcanoes safely

Most headline volcanoes sit inside national parks or monuments with visitor
centres, ranger programmes, and clear hazard information. Always check the USGS
Volcano Hazards Program status and the park alert page before you go —
eruption activity, gas, and trail closures change fast at Kīlauea in
particular.

## Timing and altitude

Cascade volcanoes are best mid-summer when snow has cleared lower trails;
Hawaii is year-round. Several summits (Shasta, Rainier, Mauna Loa) are serious
high-altitude climbs needing acclimatisation, permits, and mountaineering
skills — the viewpoints below them are spectacular without the climb.

## Respect the hazard and the place

Stay on marked trails near thermal areas — Yellowstone's ground is literally
thin crust over boiling water. Never approach active lava off designated
viewing areas. Volcanic sites are often sacred to Native Hawaiian and tribal
communities; treat them accordingly and take nothing, including rocks.
