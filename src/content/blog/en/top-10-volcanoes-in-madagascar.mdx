Madagascar sits on the eastern edge of the African plate, well away from the Rift Valley that powers Tanzania's Kilimanjaro or Ethiopia's calderas. Yet the Great Red Island harbours a surprising volcanic legacy. The Ankaratra massif rises to 2,644 m just south of Antananarivo, its summit plateau blanketed in high-altitude heath and frost, while the Itasy region to its west holds a dense field of young basalt cones and glittering crater lakes that geologists estimate erupted as recently as 7,000 years ago. This is volcanology in an unexpected setting.

## Tsiafajavona — Roof of the Ankaratra

At 2,644 m, Tsiafajavona is the highest point of the Ankaratra massif and Madagascar's third-highest summit. The mountain is a dormant shield built from successive basalt and trachyte flows. Mornings often bring mist and temperatures near 5°C even in summer, giving the plateau an austere, otherworldly atmosphere. Hikers approach from Ambatolampy along a 14 km trail through tapia woodland and montane grassland. The summit views on clear days extend across the central highlands.

## Itasy Volcanic Field

West of the capital and roughly 100 km from Antananarivo, the Itasy district contains more than 150 scoria cones, maars and lava flows spread across rolling farmland. Eruptions here are thought to have continued into the Holocene. The cones rise 30–80 m above their surroundings and are largely unforested, making them easy to identify from the road. Local guides lead walks to several vents; the terrain is gentle and the views across Lake Itasy reward the effort.

## Lake Itasy

Lake Itasy is the largest lake in the volcanic field, covering about 35 km². It formed in a broad depression partly controlled by lava flows blocking earlier drainage. Hot springs bubble at the lake's edge near Ampefy, reaching 40–50°C, evidence that the volcanic system below remains thermally active. Fishermen in pirogue canoes work the lake at dawn, and the surrounding hills harbour several heron rookeries.

## Lac Kavitaha and the Ampefy Maars

A cluster of circular maar lakes north of Ampefy represents some of the youngest volcanism on the island. Lac Kavitaha is the most visited, a near-perfect circle roughly 400 m across and 30 m deep, its walls of dark tuff rising steeply from the water. A second maar nearby, known locally as the Cascade de la Lily source maar, fed an unusual waterfall where the overflow from a crater pool drops over a basalt ledge. The youngest basalt flows in this cluster may be only 7,000–10,000 years old.

## Ankaratra Northern Vents

Along the northern escarpment of the Ankaratra massif, several smaller vents erupted long after the shield's main construction phase. These produced thin lava flows that overran river valleys and left columnar-jointed cliffs now exposed by erosion. Villages along the RN7 south of Antsirabe use these columns as building stone. Some flows dammed streams to create ponds still visible on satellite imagery.

## Tsaratanana Massif Influence

Although the Tsaratanana massif in northern Madagascar, which hosts Maromokotro (2,876 m), is broadly granitic rather than volcanic, parts of its flanks show ancient basaltic intrusion. Ambre-Bobaomby, the northernmost tip of the island, sits near the end of a geological line connecting the northern highlands to submarine volcanic seamounts in the Mozambique Channel. The connection points to a plume-influenced tectonic history extending offshore.

## Lac Tritriva

About 15 km south of Antsirabe lies Lac Tritriva, a maar of crystalline depth. Its water is an intense indigo, and local Malagasy tradition holds that it is bottomless. Scientific soundings have measured it at about 160 m. The crater rim reaches 50 m above the water surface on three sides, and the access trail drops steeply through eucalyptus forest. It is a popular excursion and one of the island's most photographed natural sites.

## Volcanic Soils and Agriculture

Across the Ankaratra and Itasy, weathered basalt has produced highly fertile andosol soils that support intensive rice cultivation on terraced hillsides. Antsirabe, situated at 1,500 m on the southern Ankaratra plateau, is surrounded by market gardens growing strawberries, garlic and vegetables that supply Antananarivo. The volcanic heritage is thus not merely geological curiosity but an agricultural foundation for millions of highland Malagasy.

## Geothermal Potential

Madagascar's geothermal resources remain largely untapped. Thermal springs at Antsirabe, Ampefy and several rural sites indicate subsurface heat flows of potential energy value. The government has explored geothermal surveys in the Itasy field, where temperature gradients in shallow wells exceed typical continental norms. Development would reduce the country's near-total dependence on hydropower, which is vulnerable to drought.
