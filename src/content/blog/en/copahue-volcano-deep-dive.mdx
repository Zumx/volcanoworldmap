Few volcanoes on Earth manage to be simultaneously dangerous and therapeutic. Copahue, rising to 2,997 m along the Chile-Argentina border in Neuquén Province, carries a permanent sulfuric-acid lake inside its active crater and a renowned geothermal spa town called Caviahue-Copahue at its base. The smell of rotten eggs permeates the air for kilometres downwind, and the crater rim is periodically dusted with new sulfur deposits — yet thousands of visitors arrive each year to soak in the hot pools, inhale medicated steam, and gaze across a landscape that resembles nothing so much as another planet.

## A Volcano on Two Countries

Copahue sits directly on the international border, with its crater shared — at least nominally — between Argentina and Chile. The Chilean flank receives the brunt of westerly winds, while the Argentine side hosts the only accessible base camp. Most expeditions approach from Caviahue, a small ski-and-spa town nestled beside a turquoise crater lake that formed when glacial melt filled an ancient caldera depression. The volcano's name comes from the Mapuche word for "place of sulfur waters," which gives some indication of how long the local Indigenous people have recognised its extraordinary geothermal character.

## The Crater Lake: pH Near Zero

What makes Copahue globally noteworthy is its crater lake, informally called El Agrio. Scientists have measured the pH at values as low as 0.2, making it one of the most acidic natural water bodies ever recorded — rivalling Rio Tinto in Spain and Kawah Ijen in Indonesia. The acidity comes from volcanic gases dissolving directly into the water, particularly sulfur dioxide and hydrogen chloride. The lake changes colour through the year from milky white to vivid green depending on the concentration of suspended sulfur particles and the volume of condensed steam being fed in. Standing at the crater rim on a calm morning, you can watch geysers of steam burst from the lake surface and feel the metal fittings on your backpack beginning to tarnish.

## Eruptive History and Recent Activity

Copahue has been in a near-permanent state of low-level unrest since at least the 1990s. Major eruptions were documented in 1992 and 2000, both accompanied by ash plumes that drifted east over the Argentine pampas. The period from 2012 to the present has seen persistent Strombolian activity punctuated by occasional paroxysmal episodes. Argentina's OAVV (Observatorio de Aplicaciones Volcánicas) and Chile's SERNAGEOMIN monitor the volcano jointly, and an alert-level system closes the summit approach whenever emissions rise above threshold values. In 2016, an escalation forced temporary evacuation of Caviahue, though residents returned within days.

## Geothermal Wonders at the Base

The same heat source that powers the crater lake also drives an extensive geothermal field across the northern and western flanks of the volcano. Boiling mud pools, fumaroles, and hot streams crosscut the terrain between 1,800 m and 2,400 m elevation. The town of Caviahue-Copahue has channelled this energy into a cluster of therapeutic bathing facilities: warm mineral baths, inhalation rooms filled with sulfurous steam, and mud-therapy cabins. Argentine balneotherapy tradition considers the waters of Copahue beneficial for respiratory and rheumatic conditions, and the site has been operating as a health resort since the early twentieth century.

## Surrounding Volcanic Province

Copahue is far from isolated. The broader southern Andes of Neuquén Province form one of the densest volcanic fields in the Americas. Within a few hundred kilometres you can reach Volcán Domuyo (3,709 m, the highest summit of Patagonia), Volcán Lanín on the Argentine-Chilean border to the south, Payún Matrú with its vast basalt fields, and Payún Liso. Cerro Auca Mahuida marks the easternmost limit of the volcanic arc, rising from the flat Patagonian steppe in an almost surreal manner. Volcán Batea Mahuida near Villa Pehuenia hosts a ski area inside a caldera — possibly the most unusual ski resort in South America. Cerro Nevado, Volcán El Tromen, and Volcán Campanario complete a regional catalogue that rewards any dedicated volcanic traveller.

## Trekking and Safety on the Slopes

Experienced climbers can reach the rim of Copahue's active crater in a long day hike from the resort town, covering roughly 1,200 m of vertical gain over 10 km of loose volcanic rock and snow. The route is unmarked above 2,400 m, and navigation requires care in cloud. Crampons and ice axes are necessary when snow covers the upper flanks, typically from June through October. All visitors must check the current alert level with park rangers before setting out: an alert level above Yellow means the crater approach is prohibited. Outside the exclusion zone, lower slopes offer rewarding walks through forests of lenga beech and pehuén araucaria pine, with geothermal features viewable from marked paths.

## Indigenous Heritage and Mapuche Territory

The Mapuche people have inhabited the Andean foothills around Copahue for centuries, regarding the volcano as a sacred site. Their oral traditions include accounts of eruptions that forced seasonal migrations across the cordillera. Several Mapuche communities still hold land rights in the immediate vicinity, and community-run guided tours offer a cultural dimension to the geothermal experience. Local artisans sell hand-woven textiles and carved wood in Caviahue, and regional cuisine includes traditional dishes prepared with wild herbs and native grains from the mountain environment.
