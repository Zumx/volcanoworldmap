Shortly after 17:00 UTC on 15 January 2022, the submarine volcano Hunga Tonga-Hunga Ha'apai erupted with an energy release that scientists later estimated at between 15 and 20 megatons of TNT equivalent — roughly 1,000 times the yield of the nuclear bomb dropped on Hiroshima. The explosion was heard in Alaska, more than 9,000 km away. A pressure wave propagated outward at the speed of sound and was recorded on barometers and weather stations across every continent, completing at least two full circuits of the globe. The eruption generated tsunamis that struck Tonga, Fiji, American Samoa, Japan, and the Pacific coast of South America within hours. And it injected an unprecedented volume of water vapour into the stratosphere, triggering a global atmospheric signal that persisted for more than a year.

## The Volcano's Unusual Setting

Hunga Tonga-Hunga Ha'apai is a submarine caldera in the Tonga archipelago, southwest Pacific Ocean, about 65 km north-northwest of the Tongan capital Nuku'alofa. Before the 2022 eruption, the only parts visible above water were two small islands — Hunga Tonga and Hunga Ha'apai — that represented the remnant rim of a large submerged caldera. The caldera itself lies at depths of 150 to 200 m below sea level, and its summit region was periodically breaking the surface through eruptions that built ephemeral cones of tephra. A major eruption in 2014-2015 had built a new island — named Hunga Tonga-Hunga Ha'apai — that bridged the two older islands and stood about 100 m above sea level. By 2022, this new land had been largely eroded, leaving only a narrow connection between the old islands.

## The Eruption Sequence

The January 2022 eruption was preceded by weeks of elevated seismicity and minor explosive activity beginning in December 2021. On 13 January 2022, activity intensified significantly, and Tonga Geological Services issued advisories. The main paroxysmal explosion occurred on 15 January. Satellite imagery captured the eruption column rising to an estimated 55-58 km, penetrating well into the mesosphere — the highest observed eruption column in the modern satellite record. The plume spread laterally at multiple altitudes, with ash fall reported across Tonga and detectable on satellite imagery across an area exceeding 500,000 km².

## The Pressure Wave

The most globally remarkable feature of the eruption was the atmospheric pressure wave it generated. Detected on barometers worldwide within hours of the explosion, the wave propagated at approximately 315 metres per second — the speed of sound at stratospheric temperatures — and was clearly identifiable on instruments in London, New York, Tokyo, and every other major atmospheric monitoring station. Critically, the wave circled the globe not once but multiple times, each circuit lasting roughly 36 hours. This type of globally propagating atmospheric wave had not been clearly observed since the 1883 eruption of Krakatau, and the 2022 Tonga event provided the first opportunity to study it with the density of modern instrumentation.

## The Tsunami

The eruption-generated tsunami was exceptionally unusual in origin. While most tsunamis are caused by seafloor displacement from earthquakes, the Tonga tsunami was primarily generated by the atmospheric pressure wave — a so-called meteotsunami mechanism never previously observed on a planetary scale. This meant that standard tsunami warning systems, which rely on seismic triggering, did not catch the event promptly: the tsunami waves arrived faster than the warning network expected based on earthquake-only models. Wave heights of 10-15 m were recorded near the eruption site. Japan reported waves of 1.2 m, significant enough to damage infrastructure at several ports. The Pacific coast of Peru was also struck, with waves causing flooding and two fatalities.

## Stratospheric Water Vapour Injection

Perhaps the most scientifically significant long-term effect of the Tonga eruption was the injection of water vapour into the stratosphere. Whereas most major volcanic eruptions inject sulfur dioxide that cools the climate by reflecting sunlight, Tonga was a submarine eruption in relatively shallow water, and the water-vapour injection far exceeded any previous event in the satellite record. Scientists estimated that the eruption increased the total water vapour content of the stratosphere by as much as 10%. This excess moisture contributed to accelerated ozone depletion and a measurable, if modest, temporary warming of the lower stratosphere — the opposite of the cooling typically associated with volcanic eruptions.

## Consequences for Tonga

For Tonga itself, the eruption was catastrophic. The capital Nuku'alofa was coated in several centimetres of ash, contaminating water supplies, damaging crops, and closing the airport. The island of Mango was almost completely destroyed, with all its residents forced to evacuate. The Ha'apai island group suffered widespread damage to infrastructure and housing. The deep-sea communication cable linking Tonga to Fiji was severed by the eruption, cutting the archipelago off from internet access for weeks while a repair vessel was deployed. Total economic losses were estimated at over 90% of Tonga's gross domestic product — one of the highest ratios of disaster damage to national income ever recorded.

## Scientific Legacy

The Hunga Tonga eruption has generated an extraordinary volume of scientific research. Every major atmospheric, oceanographic, volcanological, and seismological agency in the world dedicated resources to studying it in 2022 and subsequent years. Among the specific findings: the eruption demonstrated conclusively that submarine caldera explosions can produce atmospheric waves comparable to those of the largest subaerial events; that meteotsunami propagation on a global scale is possible and insufficiently covered by existing warning systems; and that water vapour injection, not sulfur dioxide, can be the dominant stratospheric loading from submarine eruptions. These findings have direct implications for future hazard assessment wherever submarine calderas exist.
