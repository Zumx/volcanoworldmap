Volcan de Fuego, the Volcano of Fire, lives up to its name. Towering over the
highlands of southern Guatemala, this steep stratovolcano is almost perpetually
active, regularly punctuating the skyline with explosions of ash and glowing
rock. For most of its life it has been a spectacular but manageable neighbour. In
2018, however, it produced one of the deadliest volcanic disasters of the
twenty-first century, a stark reminder of the danger hidden in its near-constant
activity.

## A near-perpetual eruption

Fuego reaches about 3,763 metres and is one of the most consistently active
volcanoes in Central America. For years it has maintained an almost continuous low
level of activity, with frequent small explosions sending ash and incandescent
material above the summit. This persistent unrest makes it a familiar presence for
the communities and coffee plantations on its slopes, who have long lived with its
rumblings and ashfall.

## A volcano near a colonial capital

Fuego rises close to the historic city of Antigua Guatemala, a former colonial
capital and UNESCO World Heritage Site. Together with its dormant neighbour
Acatenango, with which it forms a twin volcanic massif, Fuego dominates the
landscape around Antigua. Its near-constant plume is a defining feature of the
view from the city, drawing photographers and travellers from around the world.

## The 2018 catastrophe

On 3 June 2018, Fuego escalated dramatically from its usual activity into a violent
paroxysm. Powerful explosions generated fast-moving pyroclastic flows that swept
down the volcano's flanks, overwhelming communities including the village of San
Miguel Los Lotes with little warning. The disaster killed hundreds of people and
left many more missing, making it one of the worst volcanic tragedies of the
century and a profound shock to Guatemala.

## Why the disaster was so severe

The 2018 event was lethal because pyroclastic flows — searing avalanches of gas,
ash, and rock — reached populated areas extremely fast, giving residents almost no
time to escape. Heavy ash and subsequent rains also generated lahars that
compounded the destruction. The tragedy raised hard questions about monitoring,
warning systems, and evacuation in a region where many people live close to an
active volcano.

## Living with constant activity

For the people on Fuego's slopes, the volcano is a constant companion. Its fertile
volcanic soils support agriculture, particularly coffee, while its frequent
explosions are a normal part of life. This familiarity, however, can blur the line
between routine activity and the rare, dangerous escalations, a challenge that the
2018 disaster brought into sharp focus for both residents and authorities.

## Monitoring and warning

Guatemala's national disaster and volcanology agencies monitor Fuego closely,
tracking its explosions, tremor, and ash emissions. Since 2018, efforts have
focused on improving early warning, evacuation planning, and communication with the
communities most at risk. The challenge is to distinguish the volcano's routine
activity from the signs of a dangerous paroxysm in time to act.

## A draw for adventurous travellers

Despite its dangers, the Fuego-Acatenango massif has become a major destination
for adventurous hikers. Many climb the dormant Acatenango to camp on its slopes and
watch Fuego's nighttime eruptions from a safe distance, an unforgettable spectacle
of glowing explosions against the dark sky. Such trips underscore the dual nature
of the volcano as both a hazard and a wonder.
