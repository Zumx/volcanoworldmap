Curving southward from the Virgin Islands to Trinidad, the Lesser Antilles form a volcanic arc born from the subduction of the North and South American plates beneath the Caribbean plate. The outer arc consists of older, eroded limestone islands like Anguilla and Barbados, while the inner arc — the one that matters to volcanologists — is a chain of young, active stratovolcanoes building on the ocean floor. Seven of these have erupted since 1900, including two — La Soufrière Hills on Montserrat and La Soufrière on St Vincent — with eruptions after 2000 that rank among the Caribbean's most disruptive in centuries.

## La Soufrière Hills, Montserrat

When Montserrat's Soufrière Hills volcano erupted in 1995, it began an eruptive episode that has continued intermittently ever since. Pyroclastic flows buried Plymouth, the former capital, under metres of ash and debris, leaving it a ghost town visible from viewing platforms in the exclusion zone. The volcano has expelled a sequence of lava domes that have grown, collapsed and partially rebuilt multiple times. The current dome rises above 1,050 m. About 60% of the island remains in a permanent exclusion zone administered by the Montserrat Volcano Observatory.

## La Soufrière, St Vincent

La Soufrière (1,234 m) lies dormant for long intervals between explosive episodes. Its most recent eruption began in December 2020 and became fully explosive in April 2021, generating a Plinian column that reached 8 km and depositing ash across neighbouring islands including Barbados, 150 km to the east. Some 16,000 people were evacuated from the island's northern red zone. The eruption was the most powerful in the Caribbean since Montserrat's ongoing activity.

## Soufrière Hills — Context and Science

The Soufrière Hills eruption has become one of the best-studied andesitic eruptions in history because of the continuous monitoring established at the Montserrat Volcano Observatory. Seismic data, GPS ground deformation, SO₂ flux measurements and dome geometry surveys together give scientists an unprecedented real-time picture of how a dome-building volcano evolves over decades.

## Pelée, Martinique

Mount Pelée's 1902 eruption killed an estimated 29,000 people in the town of Saint-Pierre, making it the deadliest volcanic disaster of the 20th century. A pyroclastic density current, later named a nuée ardente, devastated the town in minutes. Pelée has since returned to dormancy. The ruins of Saint-Pierre, often called the Pompeii of the Caribbean, are a UNESCO Tentative List site. The volcano's summit at 1,397 m is reached by trail from the Aileron car park in roughly three hours.

## Soufrière de Guadeloupe

Grande-Terre and Basse-Terre together make up Guadeloupe. The volcanic activity is confined to Basse-Terre, where La Soufrière (1,467 m) — the highest peak in the Lesser Antilles — continues to emit sulphurous gases from fumaroles. Its last magmatic eruption was in 1530, but hydrothermal unrest in 1976 forced the temporary evacuation of 70,000 people. A 2018 increase in seismicity and new fumarolic activity prompted upgraded monitoring. The summit trail is one of Guadeloupe's most popular hikes.

## St Lucia — Sulphur Springs and Qualibou Caldera

St Lucia contains the Qualibou caldera, an 11-km-wide depression that hosts the Sulphur Springs geothermal area near Soufrière town — often marketed as the world's only drive-in volcano. The site exposes boiling mud pools, fumaroles with temperatures exceeding 170°C and vivid mineral staining. The twin Piton peaks — Gros Piton (798 m) and Petit Piton (743 m) — are the eroded necks of ancient volcanic conduits and are UNESCO World Heritage listed.

## Kick 'em Jenny, Grenada

Kick 'em Jenny is a submarine volcano 8 km north of Grenada whose summit sits about 185 m below sea level. It is one of the most active volcanoes in the Eastern Caribbean, with at least 12 confirmed eruptions since 1939. Each eruption raises a temporary exclusion zone of 1.5 km radius around the summit. The volcano poses a distant tsunami risk for the low-lying coasts of nearby islands, and monitoring buoys track its activity continuously.

## Nevis Peak

Nevis Peak (985 m) is the central edifice of the island of Nevis, a dormant stratovolcano that last erupted several thousand years ago. Its summit is often hidden in orographic cloud. The ascent from Gingerland takes four hours through rainforest. Hot springs at Oualie Beach and Bath Hotel are the visible geothermal expression of the underlying magmatic system.

## Chances Peak, Montserrat — Historical Dome

Before the 1995 eruption of Soufrière Hills, Chances Peak (915 m) was Montserrat's highest point, topped by a 1930s-era lava dome. The 1995 eruption was focused further south and Chances Peak was largely unaffected, but it now lies within the exclusion zone. Historical records document fumarolic activity there in the 18th and 19th centuries.

## Morne Diablotins and Watt Mountain, Dominica

Dominica is the most volcanically active island in the Lesser Antilles in terms of geothermal energy and fumarolic output, despite no historical eruptions. Its Boiling Lake — the world's second-largest active flooded fumarole — sits in a hydrothermal crater and was measured at 92°C. Morne Diablotins (1,447 m) is Dominica's highest peak and a dormant volcanic edifice. The island's geothermal resources are being developed for electricity export to Martinique and Guadeloupe.
