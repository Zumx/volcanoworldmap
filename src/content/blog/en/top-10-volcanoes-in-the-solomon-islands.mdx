The Solomon Islands archipelago stretches for over 1,500 kilometres through the southwestern
Pacific, draped across a convergent plate boundary where the Australian and Pacific plates
collide. This collision has produced one of the most volcanically active island chains in
Melanesia, yet it remains among the least-visited volcanic regions on Earth. Logistical
challenges, limited infrastructure, and sheer remoteness have kept Tinakula, Savo,
Kolombangara, and their neighbours off the volcano tourism circuit, even as these peaks
erupt, steam, and reshape their landscapes with a frequency rivalling far more famous
volcanoes elsewhere in the Pacific.

## Tinakula: The Island That Stays Uninhabited

Tinakula is a stratovolcano rising to about 851 metres above the Santa Cruz Islands at the
eastern end of the Solomon Islands chain. It has been almost continuously active for
centuries, and its eruptions — characterised by lava flows that cascade directly into the
sea, strombolian explosions, and periodic pyroclastic activity — forced the permanent
evacuation of its resident population in 1971. Attempts to resettle the island have
repeatedly been abandoned due to renewed eruptions. Satellite imagery regularly shows active
lava and thermal anomalies at Tinakula's summit and flanks. It is among the most remotely
located persistently active volcanoes on Earth; the nearest significant town, Lata in Santa
Cruz, lies roughly 50 kilometres to the southeast.

## Savo: Fumaroles Beneath the Capital's Horizon

Just 35 kilometres northwest of Honiara, the capital of the Solomon Islands, Savo Island
is an almost perfectly circular volcanic edifice rising about 485 metres from the sea. It is
a potentially dangerous volcano hidden in plain sight of the national capital. Savo's caldera
is partially submerged by the sea on the island's western side, and the interior hosts
vigorous hot springs and fumarole fields that have long been exploited by local communities
for cooking. The last significant eruption occurred around 1840, leaving young lava flows
and ash deposits still visible on the island. Volcanologists monitoring the Solomon Islands
arc consider Savo a priority observation target given its proximity to Honiara's population
of roughly 85,000 people.

## Kolombangara: The Sleeping Caldera

Kolombangara in the New Georgia Group is a nearly symmetrical volcanic cone rising to
1,770 metres, its summit occupied by an ancient caldera roughly 3 kilometres in diameter.
The peak is perpetually cloud-shrouded and receives extraordinary rainfall — over 7,000
millimetres per year in places — that has sculpted the flanks into dramatic ridgelines and
deep ravines. Kolombangara's forests are recognised as one of the most biologically intact
in Melanesia and are protected under an internationally certified forest management scheme
that covers the entire island. The volcano is considered dormant but not extinct; its flanks
host hot springs and the summit caldera shows persistent weak fumarolic activity.

## Vangunu and the New Georgia Volcanic Complex

The broader New Georgia Volcanic Complex includes Vangunu, Gatukai, and several smaller
islands, all part of a single interconnected volcanic system. Vangunu reaches about 1,000
metres and is covered by dense rainforest. Its geology is less well documented than
Kolombangara's, partly because access requires multiple boat transfers through the island
lagoon system. New Georgia Sound — known historically as "The Slot" for its strategic
importance during the Second World War naval battles of 1942–1943 — runs between these
volcanic islands, and several shipwrecks from that conflict now rest in waters warmed by
submarine volcanic activity.

## Nondo and the Central Chain

Nondo, a small island in the central Solomon chain, is geologically part of the same
volcanic arc that produced Tinakula and Savo but is considerably quieter. Its modest
elevation and sparse population have meant that it receives almost no scientific attention.
However, regional volcanic arc surveys have identified it as having structural features
consistent with past caldera formation, and its hot springs and discoloured coastal waters
suggest ongoing hydrothermal activity at shallow depths. Nondo exemplifies the dozens of
geologically significant but scientifically neglected volcanic islands scattered throughout
Melanesia.

## Gallego and the Makira Volcanic Zone

The island of Makira (formerly San Cristóbal) in the southern Solomons is associated with
the Gallego volcanic zone, a belt of scattered volcanic centres and intrusions along the
island's northern margin. Gallego itself is not a discrete volcanic edifice but rather a
zone of elevated geothermal heat flow, submarine venting, and onshore fumaroles. Makira's
volcanic rocks are dominated by older basalts and andesites, indicating that volcanic
activity here is on the wane compared to the more actively building islands to the north.
The ocean around Makira shows patches of discoloured water — yellow and green from
sulphurous submarine venting — visible from small aircraft.

## Kavachi: The Submarine Volcano

Perhaps the most dramatic volcanic feature of the entire Solomon Islands is Kavachi, a
shallow submarine volcano in the New Georgia Sound. Kavachi erupts frequently enough that
fishermen and sailors regularly encounter steam plumes, debris fields, and temporarily
emergent shoals where the volcano has briefly built itself above the sea surface before
wave erosion reclaims it. Since its first documented eruption in 1939, Kavachi has erupted
dozens of times and has created several temporary islands, the largest reaching about
1 kilometre in length before subsiding. Despite being one of the most active submarine
volcanoes in the Pacific, Kavachi is rarely studied due to access difficulties.

## Simbo: The Volcano of Skulls

Simbo Island in the Western Province of the Solomon Islands is a small active volcano whose
name became associated in the colonial era with the skull shrines that local communities
maintained at fumarole sites, reflecting the spiritual significance attributed to volcanic
venting. Simbo reaches only about 260 metres in elevation but exhibits vigorous surface
geothermal activity, including boiling springs, fumarole fields, and areas of hydrothermally
altered ground. The island's megapode birds famously exploit the geothermal heat to incubate
their eggs, burying them in volcanically warmed soils rather than sitting on them.

## Monitoring and Scientific Challenges

Monitoring volcanoes in the Solomon Islands presents extraordinary logistical challenges.
The Vanuatu Meteorology and Geo-Hazards Department provides some regional oversight, and
the Darwin Volcanic Ash Advisory Centre in Australia tracks major eruptive plumes for
aviation safety. The Smithsonian Institution's Global Volcanism Program maintains eruption
records for Solomon Islands volcanoes, but ground-based monitoring networks are thin — Savo
has had a seismometer installed only intermittently, and Tinakula has almost no permanent
ground instrumentation. Satellite-based infrared monitoring by institutions including the
Hawaii Institute of Geophysics has become the primary tool for tracking activity.
