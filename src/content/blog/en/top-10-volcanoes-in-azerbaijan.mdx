Drive south from Baku along the Caspian coast on a calm autumn morning and the horizon begins to look wrong. Low rounded mounds punctuate the steppe, some venting wisps of gas, others caked white with mineral salt. Occasional rumbles shake the ground, followed by bursts of cold grey mud that can reach ten metres into the air. This is Azerbaijan's extraordinary mud volcano country — a landscape unlike anywhere else on Earth, where overpressured sediment chambers push ancient marine mud to the surface through networks of conduits that have been active for millions of years.

## Gobustan — Petroglyphs and Erupting Mud

Gobustan National Park, 60 kilometres south-west of Baku, is better known for its 6,000-year-old rock carvings but is equally remarkable for its mud volcano field. The Qobustan Daşgil Palçıq Vulkanı is one of the most photogenic of the reserve's volcanoes, a low dome that periodically erupts with enough force to litter the surrounding steppe with fresh grey mud. The park also protects the Palçıq Vulkanı site on the plateau above the rock carvings, where a cluster of smaller vents creates an alien, grey-moonscape panorama just steps from bronze-age inscriptions.

## Lökbatan — The Fiery Mud Mountain

The Lökbatan palçıq vulkanı, 15 kilometres south-west of Baku, erupted violently in 2001, sending a column of burning gas 300 metres into the sky and leaving a lava-like field of cooled mud that can still be walked. Eruptions at Lökbatan are recorded back to the 1840s; each episode dramatically reshapes the summit cone. The site is freely accessible from the main road and the scorched crater rim offers a view over the oil derricks of the Absheron Peninsula, connecting Azerbaijan's two great geological exports — petroleum and mud volcanism.

## Axtarma Paşalı — A Boiling Plateau

The Axtarma Paşalı palçıq vulkanı sits on open steppe south of Baku and is notable for the size and vigour of its central pool, which churns and heaves with trapped methane throughout the year. The surrounding plain is glazed with dried mud flows that preserve the shapes of previous eruptions like geological growth rings. Local guides sometimes arrange night visits, when the phosphorescent sheen of the wet mud under starlight creates an eerie spectacle.

## Böyük Kənizədağ — The Great Lady Mountain

The Böyük Kənizədağ Palçıq vulkanı stands out for its relative height — the dome rises several tens of metres above the surrounding plain, forming a genuine hill rather than the low mounds typical of most Azerbaijani mud volcanoes. The cone is topped by multiple pools venting warm, saline mud rich in hydrocarbons. Geologists use cores from Böyük Kənizədağ to study the pressure evolution of deep Caspian sedimentary basins.

## Torağay Dağı — The Qaradağ Cluster

The Torağay Dağı Palçıq Vulkanı in the Qaradağ district is part of a dense cluster of volcanic features that extend along a tectonic lineament linked to the fold-and-thrust belt of the Greater Caucasus. Neighbouring vents include Palçıq Boz vulkanı Hökməli and the Bozdağ Palçıq Vulkanı, which erupted with particular force in the early 2000s. The Qaradağ landscape is stark and beautiful, combining salt flats, dried mud rivers, and the blue line of the Caspian on the eastern horizon.

## Keyrəki — Coastal Mud Volcanism

The Keyrəki palçıq vulkanı sits close enough to the Caspian shore to have been partially flooded during periods of rising lake level. Subaqueous mud volcano activity in the Caspian — detected by sonar surveys — suggests that many onshore volcanoes are the emerged tips of much larger submarine structures. The Keyrəki site is accessible from the coastal road south of Baku and is often visited alongside the nearby Palçıq Vulkanı Xıdırlı site.

## Şarınca — A Hillside of Vents

The Şarınca Palçıq vulkanı is unusual for its hillside setting, where multiple vents on a sloping terrain channel mud flows that travel considerable distances downslope before hardening. The site preserves an active archive of eruption history in the layered sequences of dried flows, with colours ranging from pale grey through ochre to dark charcoal depending on mineralogy and oxidation state.

## Gargar — The Western Fields

Moving west away from the Absheron Peninsula, the Gargar Volcan sits in a transitional zone where the geology shifts from the oil-rich Caspian basin to the metamorphic rocks of the Lesser Caucasus foothills. This remoter setting means that Gargar sees far fewer visitors than the Baku-area volcanoes, rewarding those who make the journey with solitude and the sight of vents that have changed little since European travellers first documented them in the nineteenth century.

## Çeyildağ — Methane Flames by Night

The Çeyildağ Palçıq vulkanı is one of the features most likely to reward an evening visit in the cooler months, when methane seeping from the vent sometimes ignites spontaneously at the surface. These brief, flickering flames at the edge of the main pool are a reminder that Azerbaijan's traditional name — the Land of Fire — refers to natural gas seeps rather than exclusively to magmatic activity.

## Diləngəz — The Gobustan Outlier

The Diləngəz Palçıq Vulkanı in the outer Gobustan district completes a circuit of the main mud volcano province. Like many of the Qobustan outliers, the Diləngəz vent is best visited in spring, when winter rains thin the mud to a more fluid consistency and eruptions become more frequent and spectacular. Combined with the nearby Ayrantəkan palçıq vulkanı and the Qoturdağ palçıq vulkanı, it forms part of a half-day loop through some of the most concentrated mud-volcano terrain in the world.
