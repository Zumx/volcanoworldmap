On the night of 21 August 1986, the inhabitants of the villages of Nyos, Kam, Cha, and Subum in the northwest of Cameroon went to sleep as they did on any other evening. By morning, approximately 1,700 of them were dead. The cause was not an earthquake, not a landslide, and not a conventional volcanic eruption. Instead, the trigger was a limnic eruption — a sudden, catastrophic overturn of Lake Nyos, a volcanic crater lake perched in the highlands of Cameroon's Oku volcanic field. Within minutes of the overturn, an invisible cloud of carbon dioxide rolled across the landscape and suffocated everything it touched.

## The Geology of a Killer Lake

Lake Nyos sits in a maar — a broad, low-relief volcanic crater formed when magma contacts groundwater and explodes violently. The maar that holds Nyos is part of the Oku volcanic field, which includes Mount Oku, one of the highest peaks in Cameroon at 3,011 metres, and is linked to the broader Cameroon Volcanic Line, the same geological feature that produced Mont Cameroun, the highest peak in west-central Africa at 4,040 metres. The Oku field has been active within the last 10,000 years, and the maar beneath Lake Nyos is underlain by magma that slowly diffuses CO2 into the lake's deepest waters. Over decades or centuries, the bottom of the lake accumulates dissolved gas under the pressure of the water column above it, turning the deep layer into a kind of pressurised carbonated system.

## How a Limnic Eruption Works

Under normal conditions, the stratified temperature layers of the lake prevent the gas-saturated deep water from mixing with the surface. But any disturbance — a landslide, an earthquake, even a wind storm strong enough to disturb the water column — can trigger rapid de-stratification. When this happens, the released gas causes surrounding water to expand and rise, which releases more gas, which causes more rise, in a runaway chain reaction. At Lake Nyos, the process appears to have been triggered by a small landslide or, possibly, a minor subsurface volcanic event. The overturn produced a wave that was estimated at 25 metres high and a gas cloud that escaped the crater in seconds.

## The Night of the Disaster

Survivors described hearing a rumbling sound, then seeing a mist rising from the lake. Those who were awake felt a warm sensation before losing consciousness. The CO2 cloud — being denser than air — hugged the ground and flowed along the valleys, travelling up to 25 kilometres from the lake before dispersing. At its highest concentrations near the lake, the gas asphyxiated animals, people, and insects alike. Cattle were found standing dead in their fields. In the village of Nyos itself, only a handful of people survived. The air remained dangerous for hours, and rescue workers who arrived the next day found scenes of mass death.

## Scientific Response and Global Significance

Lake Nyos was not the first limnic eruption; Lake Monoun, 100 kilometres to the southeast in the same Oku field, had experienced a smaller event in 1984 that killed 37 people. But the scale of the Nyos disaster drew immediate international scientific attention. Volcanologists rapidly identified that Lake Nyos, Lake Monoun, and neighbouring Lake Kivu on the Congo-Rwanda border all shared the same dangerous combination of volcanic CO2 input and stratified deep water. The term "limnic eruption" entered the scientific lexicon, and governments and researchers began debating how to prevent future events.

## Degassing Solutions

The chosen technical solution was controlled degassing — running pipes down into the deep water layer so that the gas-rich water continuously vents upward in a controlled plume, preventing catastrophic accumulation. The first degassing pipe at Lake Nyos became operational in 2001. By the early 2010s, three pipes were installed, and gas concentrations in the deep water had been measurably reduced. However, the pipes drain CO2 faster than it accumulates only if all three are operating properly, and funding and maintenance have been intermittent. The natural dam at the northern end of Lake Nyos — an unstable volcanic rock formation — also poses a separate risk: its collapse would cause catastrophic flooding of the valleys below, independent of any gas release.

## Communities Displaced and Ongoing Risk

The Cameroonian government forbade villagers from returning to the vicinity of Lake Nyos after 1986, creating a displaced community that has lived in resettlement camps for nearly four decades. As degassing has reduced gas concentrations, pressure has mounted to allow some return. By the 2020s, limited resettlement had been authorised in some areas, but the dam instability and the long-term adequacy of the degassing infrastructure remain contested. Pico Basilé, on the island of Bioko in Equatorial Guinea, and Pico Biaó on Príncipe Island are other volcanic centres in the wider Gulf of Guinea region that scientists continue to monitor for similar hazards.

## What Lake Nyos Taught Volcanology

Before 1986, the concept of a volcano killing thousands of people with an odourless, colourless gas cloud — without any lava, ash, or explosion — was largely theoretical. Lake Nyos forced a fundamental expansion of volcanic hazard categories. It demonstrated that lakes overlying volcanic systems can be weapons that arm themselves silently over decades, then discharge in minutes. It also highlighted the particular vulnerability of rural African communities to geological hazards that receive little international monitoring. The Nyos disaster prompted a global inventory of potentially hazardous volcanic crater lakes.
