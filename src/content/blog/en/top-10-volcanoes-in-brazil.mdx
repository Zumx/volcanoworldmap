Brazil is not a name that leaps to mind in conversations about volcanism, yet the country
harbours one of the most geologically varied volcanic records in the Southern Hemisphere.
From the vast Paraná-Etendeka flood basalt province — which blanketed an area larger than
Western Europe roughly 132 million years ago — to the remote trachyte plugs of Trindade
Island far out in the South Atlantic, Brazil's volcanic inheritance is ancient, enormous,
and still shaping the landscape today through lava tube systems that snake beneath the
cerrado scrubland of Goiás and Bahia.

## The Paraná-Etendeka Flood Basalt Province

The most spectacular chapter of Brazilian volcanism unfolded during the Early Cretaceous,
when continental rifting tore apart what would become South America and Africa. Over a
geologically brief pulse of perhaps one to two million years, enormous volumes of low-
viscosity basaltic lava poured across what is now southern Brazil, Uruguay, Argentina, and
Paraguay. The resulting Paraná Large Igneous Province covers roughly 1.2 million square
kilometres, with individual lava flows stacked up to 1,700 metres thick in places around
the Paraná Basin. Erosion has since sculpted these horizontal basalt sheets into the
spectacular red-earth plateaux of Paraná and Santa Catarina states, and the dark basalt
columns are visible in cliff faces along the Iguaçu River. The paired Etendeka province
on the Namibian coast confirms this was once a single eruptive system, neatly documenting
the Gondwana breakup.

## Trindade Island: Trachyte Plugs in the Open Ocean

About 1,140 kilometres east of Vitória in Espírito Santo state, the volcanic island of
Trindade rises steeply from the South Atlantic. It is one of Brazil's most remote
inhabited outposts, maintained by a small Brazilian Navy garrison. Geologically, Trindade
is a hotspot-related oceanic island, and its lavas are unusually alkaline — dominated by
phonolites and trachytes rather than the basalts typical of mid-ocean islands. The
island's coastline is fringed with towering plug remnants and eroded necks, their pale
grey rock contrasting sharply with the deep-blue ocean. The highest peak, Pico Desejado,
reaches 620 metres. Access is tightly restricted; visits require navy permission, which
makes Trindade one of the least-studied Atlantic volcanic islands of comparable geological
interest.

## Pico do Cabugi: A Lone Neck Above the Sertão

In Rio Grande do Norte state in northeastern Brazil, the basaltic neck known as Pico do
Cabugi rises abruptly about 220 metres above the flat, semi-arid caatinga scrubland.
It is among the most photogenic volcanic remnants in South America — a dark, steep-sided
monolith of solidified magma that once occupied the conduit of an ancient volcano. Isotopic
dating places its last activity at around 12 million years ago, during a Cenozoic period
of intraplate volcanism that peppered northeastern Brazil with isolated alkaline plugs.
Cabugi is protected as part of a state environmental protection area, and a trail to the
summit offers wide views across the thorny caatinga.

## Morro São João and the Poços de Caldas Alkaline Complex

The Poços de Caldas Plateau in southern Minas Gerais state is the eroded remnant of a
circular alkaline intrusion roughly 33 kilometres in diameter — one of the best-preserved
ring structures of its type in South America. The complex formed about 75 million years ago
and now sustains the geothermal springs that give the spa city of Poços de Caldas its name
and economy. Morro São João, rising to about 1,678 metres, is the highest point within the
complex and offers hiking trails through cerrado vegetation to wide panoramic views. The
soils derived from the alkaline rocks — rich in niobium and rare-earth elements — have
attracted mining interest, and the region around Araxá, another alkaline complex 200
kilometres to the north, hosts one of the world's largest niobium deposits.

## Vulcão Amazonas and Intraplate Hotspot Tracks

Although the name "Vulcão Amazonas" conjures dramatic imagery, it refers to a submarine
volcano on the Fernando de Noronha Chain, a linear track of seamounts and islands that
records the passage of the South American plate over a hotspot, analogous to the Hawaiian
chain. Fernando de Noronha Island itself, a UNESCO-listed marine park, is the most
accessible expression of this hotspot track. The island's dramatic sea stacks, such as
Morro do Pico at 321 metres, are eroded phonolite plugs. The seamounts stretching east
toward the Mid-Atlantic Ridge trace the plate's motion over roughly 70 million years and
serve as natural laboratories for studying mantle dynamics.

## Brazil's Lava Tube Systems

Perhaps the most unexpected of Brazil's volcanic features are its lava tubes — subterranean
tunnels created when the outer skin of a flowing basaltic lava hardens while molten lava
continues to drain through the interior. Several important tube systems occur in the states
of Goiás and Bahia, associated with Cretaceous basaltic volcanism. The Igatu lava tubes in
Bahia's Chapada Diamantina region include galleries hundreds of metres long, some housing
unique cave-adapted fauna. These tubes represent some of the best-preserved lava tube
environments in South America and are increasingly recognised for their speleological and
biological value.

## Cenozoic Alkaline Volcanism in the Northeast

Northeastern Brazil experienced a pulse of alkaline intraplate volcanism between roughly 30
and 7 million years ago, leaving behind a constellation of basaltic plugs, dikes, and
small intrusions scattered across Ceará, Rio Grande do Norte, Paraíba, and Pernambuco
states. These features are typically isolated hills rising 100 to 300 metres above the
surrounding tableland, many forming the cores of municipal parks or ecological reserves.
The Seridó Geopark in Rio Grande do Norte encompasses several of these volcanic remnants
alongside its granite inselbergs, offering visitors a compressed lesson in the deep
geological history of South America's passive margin.

## Geothermal Springs and Subsurface Heat

Brazil's volcanic heritage manifests at the surface in dozens of hot-spring resorts, most
famously in the Caldas Novas region of Goiás state, where artesian groundwater percolates
through deep crustal rock heated by residual magmatic and radiogenic sources. Caldas Novas
hosts the largest concentration of thermal pools in the world by visitor number, drawing
over two million tourists annually. Though the heat source is diffuse rather than tied to a
discrete magma body, the region's warmth is a direct expression of the same geothermal
energy that drove Mesozoic volcanism across the Brazilian interior.

## Scientific and Conservation Value

Brazil's volcanic sites are increasingly recognised in national and international
geopark frameworks. The Chapada dos Guimarães National Park in Mato Grosso exposes
tilted basalt sequences from the Paraná province. The geomorphological diversity created
by ancient volcanism — mesa plateaux, inselbergs, cave systems, and hydrothermal springs —
underpins much of Brazil's extraordinary biodiversity in the cerrado, caatinga, and
Atlantic Forest biomes. Ongoing geological surveys continue to refine the age and
chemistry of intraplate volcanic features, filling in the picture of how heat from the
deep mantle has shaped the South American continent.
