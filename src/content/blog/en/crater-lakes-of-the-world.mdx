Standing on the rim of Kawah Ijen in East Java, Indonesia, the turquoise colour of the lake below is almost impossibly beautiful. The light hits the acid surface differently from the light on any ordinary body of water, producing an iridescent shimmer that painters would struggle to reproduce honestly. But the water in that lake is sulphuric acid at pH 0.5 — about as acidic as battery acid — and the yellowish crust surrounding its margins is native sulphur deposited directly from the volcanic gases bubbling through the lake floor. Below, if you arrive at night, you will see blue flames: molten sulphur gas igniting as it contacts the air, burning in an eerie electric blue that seems incompatible with the ordinary laws of fire. Crater lakes are among the most chemically extreme environments on Earth, and they are also among the most beautiful.

## Kawah Ijen, Indonesia: The Blue Fire Lake

The Ijen volcanic complex in East Java hosts what is scientifically the largest hyperacid crater lake in the world by volume — approximately 27 million cubic metres of sulphuric acid solution. The lake sits at 2,145 metres above sea level and maintains a temperature of about 33°C from geothermal input. Beneath the lake surface, volcanic gases — primarily SO2 and H2S — dissolve into the water and react to form the sulphuric acid that gives the lake its extreme acidity. At the lake margin, native sulphur deposited from the gas streams is harvested by local miners who descend into the crater in the dark hours to chip sulphur blocks weighing up to 80 kilograms, which they carry up 200-metre crater walls and then 3 kilometres to the road on their backs.

The blue fire visible at night is molten sulphur gas igniting at temperatures above 360°C as it meets the oxygen in the ambient air. The flames are real and burn at the lake margin in an almost supernatural display.

## Crater Lake, Oregon, USA: Clarity and Depth

At the opposite extreme of the chemical spectrum sits Crater Lake in Oregon, one of the purest bodies of water in the world. Formed roughly 7,700 years ago when the stratovolcano Mount Mazama collapsed following a catastrophic eruption, the caldera lake is 592 metres deep — the deepest in the United States and ninth deepest globally — and holds approximately 17 cubic kilometres of water. The lake has no inlet rivers and no outlet, receiving all its water from snowfall and losing it through evaporation and seepage. The absence of any significant river input keeps the water extraordinarily clear: visibility extends to around 30 metres below the surface on still days, giving the lake its characteristic deep Prussian blue colour. A small cinder cone, Wizard Island, rises 230 metres above the lake surface within the caldera.

## Lake Nyos, Cameroon: The Killer Lake

On August 21, 1986, Lake Nyos in Cameroon silently killed approximately 1,800 people and 3,500 livestock in a few hours. The cause was a limnic eruption — a sudden overturn of the lake's water column that released a massive cloud of CO2 from the bottom of the lake, where volcanic gases had been accumulating for years under the pressure of the overlying water. The CO2 cloud, heavier than air, flowed downhill into nearby valleys and suffocated everything in its path for distances up to 25 kilometres. Lake Nyos is a maar lake occupying a crater in the Cameroonian highlands; beneath it, volcanic activity continuously charges the deep water with CO2. After the disaster, a degassing pipe was installed to slowly release the gas from the deep water, preventing further dangerous accumulation. Lake Monoun, a smaller maar lake 95 kilometres from Nyos, had experienced a smaller limnic eruption two years earlier, killing 37 people.

## Kelimutu, Indonesia: Three Colours, Three Spirits

The summit of Kelimutu volcano on Flores island in Indonesia hosts three crater lakes whose colours shift dramatically and unpredictably over time — a result of changing volcanic gas compositions, temperature fluctuations, and chemical reactions at the lake floor. At different periods the lakes have been turquoise, black, dark green, red, and white. Local tradition holds that the lakes are the resting places of the souls of the dead: one lake for young souls, one for old souls, and one for evil souls. The lakes are separated by narrow crater walls only a few metres wide. From the summit car park, all three lakes are visible simultaneously, their different colours creating an otherworldly tableau that has made Kelimutu one of the most visited volcanic sites in eastern Indonesia.

## Ruapehu Crater Lake, New Zealand: A Monitored Hazard

The active Crater Lake on top of Ruapehu in New Zealand's North Island is one of the most carefully monitored bodies of water in the Southern Hemisphere. The lake — sitting at approximately 2,530 metres within the summit caldera — receives heat directly from the active vent beneath it. Its temperature fluctuates between about 10°C during cold periods and above 60°C during volcanic unrest. The lake chemistry is acidic (pH typically 0.5–0.8 during warm phases), and the lake level fluctuates with eruptive cycles. The critical hazard from the Crater Lake is not eruption itself but lahar: the lake can drain catastrophically when eruptions breach the ice dam that normally retains the water, sending a hot acidic lahar down Ruapehu's flanks into ski areas and river valleys. The Tangiwai disaster of 1953, which killed 151 people when a railway bridge was destroyed by a Ruapehu lahar, remains New Zealand's worst rail accident.

## Laguna Caliente, Costa Rica: Extreme Acidity at High Altitude

Poás volcano in Costa Rica hosts Laguna Caliente, another candidate for the world's most acidic lake. During active periods, the pH can drop below 0 — measured values of pH -1 have been reported, meaning the fluid is essentially concentrated sulphuric acid. The lake temperature during unrest reaches approximately 70–90°C, and the lake frequently erupts in phreatic explosions that hurl acid spray and rock fragments. The crater has been closed to visitors multiple times due to unrest, most recently following a major phreatic eruption in April 2017 that produced eruption columns reaching 3,000 metres. The lake's extreme acidity makes it one of the most inhospitable environments on Earth — yet even here, evidence of extremophile microorganisms adapted to the acid has been found.

## Lake Taal, Philippines: A Lake on an Island in a Lake on an Island

The Taal volcanic system in the Philippines presents a nested geography: Taal Volcano Island sits in the middle of Taal Lake, which in turn occupies a caldera surrounded by the larger main island of Luzon. The volcano island has its own active main crater lake, and Taal has been one of the Philippines' most persistently active volcanoes throughout recorded history. The January 2020 eruption of Taal produced a phreatomagmatic column reaching 14 kilometres, deposited ash across Metro Manila, and triggered lava fountaining from fissures on the island. The nested caldera system means that any major eruption of Taal Volcano Island can generate tsunamis within Taal Lake, adding another layer to an already complex hazard environment.

## The Science of Crater Lake Chemistry

Volcanic crater lakes are monitored by researchers using a combination of remote sensing, buoy-mounted sensors, and direct water sampling. The key geochemical signatures include: pH and total acidity (which indicate the flux of volcanic gases through the lake), Cl/SO4 ratios (which can distinguish between different volcanic gas sources), temperature gradients (which map heat flow from the volcanic conduit), and stable isotope compositions (which distinguish magmatic from hydrothermal water). Changes in these parameters, especially rapid shifts in temperature or acidity, are important precursory signals that can warn of increasing volcanic unrest before surface deformation or seismicity becomes obvious.
