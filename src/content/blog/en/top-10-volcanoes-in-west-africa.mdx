The Cameroon Volcanic Line is one of Africa's least celebrated geological features, yet it ranks among the continent's most impressive volcanic chains. Running roughly northeast from the island of Annobón in the Gulf of Guinea, through Bioko, Príncipe and São Tomé, to Mount Cameroon on the mainland, and then inland past the Biu Plateau and the Mandara Mountains toward Lake Chad, this chain of oceanic and continental volcanoes has shaped landscape, climate and human culture across West and Central Africa for tens of millions of years.

## Mount Cameroon — Fako

At 4,040 m, Mount Cameroon is the highest peak in West and Central Africa and one of the continent's most active volcanoes. Historical eruptions are documented from 1922, 1954, 1959, 1982, 1999, 2000 and 2012. The most recent flows descended toward the fishing village of Bakingili on the southwest coast, halting short of the sea. Buea is the main trailhead for the ascent, and the annual Cameroon Mountain Race draws trail runners from across the continent up the 4,040 m in under four hours.

## Bioko — Pico Basile

The island of Bioko belongs to Equatorial Guinea and rises from the Gulf of Guinea as the emergent summit of a submarine volcano. Pico Basile reaches 3,011 m, making it the highest point in Equatorial Guinea. The island is one of the last refuges for the drill monkey and several endemic bird species. The summit is often cloud-capped; trails from Malabo penetrate cloud forest with tree ferns and African violets.

## São Tomé Peak

São Tomé is the larger of the two islands in the Gulf of Guinea nation of Sao Tome and Principe. Its central massif, Pico de São Tomé, reaches 2,024 m and supports primary Atlantic rainforest on its upper slopes — one of the world's most biodiverse island forest ecosystems. The island has been geologically quiet for thousands of years, but its volcanic origin is evident in black-sand beaches, columnar basalt outcrops and mineral-rich soils supporting cocoa and coffee.

## Príncipe and Pico do Príncipe

The smaller island of Príncipe rises to 948 m at its highest point and is even more biodiverse relative to its size. A UNESCO Biosphere Reserve since 2012, Príncipe's jungle-covered caldera remnants and basalt sea stacks make it a striking landscape. Volcanic soils and high humidity support forests with 700 endemic plant species.

## Annobón

Annobón, the southernmost island of Equatorial Guinea at latitude 1.4°S, is the oceanic terminus of the Cameroon Volcanic Line and sits atop a hotspot track. Its highest point, Pico do Fogo, reaches 655 m. The island's 5,000 inhabitants fish tuna and subsist on volcanic soils. A crater lake, Lago A Po, fills the summit caldera and is rimmed by tree ferns. Annobón is one of the most isolated and least visited islands in the Atlantic.

## Manengouba Massif, Cameroon

Rising to 2,396 m near the town of Nkongsamba in Cameroon's West Region, the Manengouba Massif is a dormant shield volcano. Its twin summit calderas contain two lakes — Lac Femme and Lac Homme (Lake Female and Lake Male) — about 1 km apart and of contrasting chemistry. Lac Femme is turbid and nutrient-rich; Lac Homme is clear and cold. The caldera rim is walkable and offers panoramic views over the Mungo valley and toward Mount Cameroon.

## Oku Volcanic Field and Lake Nyos

The Oku volcanic field in the Cameroon highlands contains one of the world's most dangerous natural hazards: Lake Nyos, a crater lake that in August 1986 released a massive pulse of dissolved CO₂, asphyxiating approximately 1,800 people in surrounding valleys overnight. A limnic eruption on this scale had never been witnessed before. Today a degassing pipe installed in 2001 continuously vents CO₂ from the lake floor to prevent pressure accumulation.

## Ngaoundéré Plateau

The Ngaoundéré Plateau in Cameroon's Adamawa Region is an elevated plateau of Precambrian basement overlain by Cenozoic basalt flows. The town of Ngaoundéré sits at 1,100 m amid a landscape of gently sloping shield vents and scoria cones. This is the northeastern extent of the Cameroon Volcanic Line before it transitions into the Biu Plateau of Nigeria.

## Biu Plateau, Nigeria

The Biu Plateau in Borno State, northeastern Nigeria, is a dissected basalt plateau formed by Cenozoic volcanism on the Cameroon Volcanic Line's continental extension. It rises to about 700 m above the surrounding plains and is covered with grassland and scattered shrub. At least seven scoria cones are visible on satellite imagery; none has erupted in historical time. The plateau is of scientific interest as one of the few volcanic terrains in Nigeria.

## Tibesti Massif — Emi Koussi

Although geographically in Chad and technically in the Sahara, the Tibesti Massif shares the deep-mantle context of West African intraplate volcanism. Emi Koussi, at 3,415 m, is the highest point in both the Sahara and in Chad. Its caldera, roughly 15 km across, holds fumaroles and salt deposits. The massif last erupted in historical time at Tarso Voon, where black lava flows are fresh enough to show glassy surfaces. The Tibesti remains geopolitically difficult to access.
