Roughly 800 of the world's 1,500 potentially active volcanoes have never been monitored by a single ground-based instrument. They sit in remote jungles, on uninhabited ocean islands, or in conflict zones where fieldwork is impossible. For most of human history, eruptions at these volcanoes arrived without warning. That has begun to change radically since the 1990s as an expanding constellation of Earth-observation satellites — measuring ground deformation, gas emissions, thermal signatures, and ash clouds simultaneously — has brought every volcano on the planet within reach of scientific observation.

## InSAR: Measuring Ground Deformation From 700 Kilometres Up

Interferometric Synthetic Aperture Radar (InSAR) is perhaps the single most transformative technology in modern volcanology. Radar satellites such as the European Space Agency's Sentinel-1 pair, the Italian COSMO-SkyMed constellation, and Japan's ALOS-2 transmit microwave pulses to the ground and measure the phase of the returning signal to sub-centimetre precision. By comparing radar images acquired weeks or months apart, scientists can construct deformation maps showing exactly where the ground has moved — up, down, or sideways — across an entire volcanic field. Magma intruding at depth, hydrothermal fluids migrating, or fault systems adjusting can all leave distinctive patterns. In 2018, InSAR data from Sentinel-1 showed that Sierra Negra volcano on Isabela Island in the Galápagos was inflating at nearly 80 centimetres per year. The volcano erupted in June 2018 — its biggest eruption since 1979 — with the inflation signature having been visible from orbit for over a year beforehand.

## Sentinel-1 and the Global Volcano Monitoring Revolution

The Sentinel-1 mission, operated by ESA under the Copernicus programme, provides repeat-pass InSAR coverage of the entire globe every 6 to 12 days. This cadence is fast enough to detect the onset of volcanic inflation and track its evolution in near real time. Researchers at the Centre for Observation and Modelling of Earthquakes, Volcanoes and Tectonics (COMET) in the United Kingdom have used Sentinel-1 data to build a global catalogue of volcanic unrest detected from space, identifying several previously unknown or undermonitored systems showing measurable ground deformation. Among the most striking findings was the detection of ongoing uplift at Campi Flegrei, the caldera underlying the western suburbs of Naples, Italy, which has been slowly inflating since 2013 at rates reaching 2 centimetres per month during peak activity.

## Thermal Infrared: Watching Heat From Orbit

Volcanic activity produces heat, and heat can be detected from space. The MODVOLC system, operated by the University of Hawaii, uses thermal data from NASA's MODIS instrument aboard the Terra and Aqua satellites to monitor volcanic hot spots globally, generating near real-time alerts when anomalous thermal emissions are detected. Kīlauea, Etna, Stromboli, and Nyiragongo are among the dozens of volcanoes with nearly continuous thermal anomalies visible from orbit. More detailed thermal mapping is available from Landsat 8 and 9, which carry a Thermal Infrared Sensor (TIRS) capable of resolving temperature variations across individual lava flows and fumarole fields at a resolution of 100 metres. In 2020, thermal data from Landsat 8 and Sentinel-2 helped track the progression of the Kīlauea rift zone eruption in near real time.

## SO₂ Monitoring: The Plume From Space

Sulphur dioxide is one of the clearest chemical signatures of magmatic degassing. Unlike CO₂ or water vapour, SO₂ absorbs ultraviolet light in a distinctive way that allows it to be detected from orbit by instruments measuring the scattering of sunlight. The Ozone Monitoring Instrument (OMI) aboard NASA's Aura satellite and the TROPOspheric Monitoring Instrument (TROPOMI) on Sentinel-5P have produced global SO₂ maps with unprecedented sensitivity. TROPOMI, launched in 2017, can detect SO₂ plumes from even moderate volcanic emissions, tracking gas clouds thousands of kilometres downwind. In 2019, TROPOMI revealed that Ambae Volcano in Vanuatu was emitting over 10,000 tonnes of SO₂ per day during a period of heightened unrest — a rate comparable to large industrial complexes and detectable across the entire Pacific. These measurements have also been used retrospectively: SO₂ flux data from OMI and TROPOMI have revealed that some volcanoes previously thought quiescent had been degassing at high rates for years.

## Ash Detection and Aviation Safety

Volcanic ash is a critical hazard for aviation: particles of glass and crystalline silica as fine as 0.1 millimetres can destroy jet engine turbine blades in seconds and sand aircraft windshields opaque. The 2010 Eyjafjallajökull eruption in Iceland grounded more than 100,000 flights across European airspace, at an economic cost estimated at 1.3 billion euros. Geostationary satellites such as the Meteosat series operated by EUMETSAT and the GOES series operated by NOAA now provide continuous, high-temporal-resolution monitoring of ash clouds, feeding data into volcanic ash advisory centres (VAACs) that issue warnings to airlines. The Spinning Enhanced Visible and Infrared Imager (SEVIRI) on Meteosat can detect and track ash clouds every 15 minutes across the North Atlantic, Europe, and Africa.

## Detecting Unrest at Remote and Poorly Monitored Volcanoes

One of the most consequential applications of satellite volcanology is the systematic scanning of volcanoes that have never had ground instruments installed. The Global Volcano Monitoring programme, drawing on data from Sentinel-1, TROPOMI, and MODIS, has identified previously undetected unrest at volcanoes on remote islands in the South Atlantic, the South Pacific, and the Arctic. Sarychev Peak in the Kuril Islands of Russia, which produced a major eruption in 2009 photographed dramatically from the International Space Station, had shown measurable thermal anomalies from orbit months before the event. Veniaminof Volcano on the Alaska Peninsula and Shishaldin Volcano on Unimak Island — both in remote, sparsely monitored stretches of the Aleutian Arc — have been tracked primarily via satellite since the 1990s, with ground networks supplementing rather than replacing the space-based view.

## Multi-Sensor Integration and Machine Learning

Modern volcanic monitoring increasingly combines data streams from multiple satellite sensors simultaneously: InSAR deformation, TROPOMI SO₂, MODIS thermal, and Sentinel-2 optical imagery processed together provide a richer picture of volcanic state than any single sensor can offer. Machine learning algorithms trained on historical eruption data are now being applied to these multi-sensor data streams to generate automated unrest alerts. Google's DeepMind and several university groups have published experimental systems capable of detecting the onset of eruptive episodes at global scale within hours of satellite overpasses. The Global Volcano Monitor initiative, led by a consortium of European universities and ESA, aims to deliver automated eruption alerts for all monitored volcanoes within 12 hours of detection.

## Limitations and Ground Truth

Satellite remote sensing is powerful but not omniscient. Cloud cover — ubiquitous over tropical volcanoes — can obscure optical and thermal signals for days to weeks. InSAR coherence breaks down in densely vegetated areas and over snow and ice. Atmospheric water vapour introduces artefacts in InSAR deformation maps that can mimic volcanic signals. Ground-based instruments — seismometers, tiltmeters, gas sensors, and GPS receivers installed on the volcano itself — remain essential for confirming satellite-detected anomalies and for the rapid-response monitoring that precedes an eruption by hours. The most robust monitoring systems combine both approaches, with satellite data providing the broad picture and ground sensors delivering the detail.
