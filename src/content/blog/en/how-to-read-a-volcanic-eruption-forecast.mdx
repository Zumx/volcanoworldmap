Standing on the rim of Bromo in East Java or hiking the slopes of Etna above Rifugio Sapienza, most travelers have no idea that a suite of real-time monitoring data is being collected, processed, and published within metres of where they walk. Volcano observatories around the world translate seismic signals, satellite radar measurements, gas emission rates, and visual observations into alert levels and color codes that anyone can learn to read with a little practice. Knowing what these forecasts actually say — and what they deliberately do not say — is one of the most practical skills a volcano traveler can develop.

## The Volcano Alert Level System

Most countries with active volcanoes use a tiered alert system, typically numbered 1 through 4 or 5, where higher numbers indicate greater unrest or eruption intensity. These levels are set by national volcano observatories and guide civil protection responses. In Indonesia, the national body PVMBG (Pusat Vulkanologi dan Mitigasi Bencana Geologi) uses Levels 1 to 4: Level 1 (Normal) means background activity; Level 2 (Waspada) means elevated seismicity or thermal anomaly; Level 3 (Siaga) means significant unrest with eruption possible in weeks; Level 4 (Awas) means eruption imminent or under way, and evacuation exclusion zones are enforced. In New Zealand, GeoNet uses a Volcanic Alert Level from 0 to 5. In the United States, the USGS uses a separate but parallel Volcano Alert Level (Normal, Advisory, Watch, Warning) for the ground alongside the Aviation Color Code for flight safety.

## Aviation Color Codes: What They Mean for Travelers

The International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) coordinates a four-colour system — Green, Yellow, Orange, Red — applied to volcanoes by their respective observatories, primarily for aircraft safety but useful as a quick proxy for overall unrest. Green indicates background, non-eruptive activity. Yellow means the volcano is exhibiting signs of elevated unrest above known background levels. Orange means heightened unrest with increased potential for eruption, or a minor eruption under way producing limited ash. Red signals a major eruption is imminent or occurring, with significant ash emission into the atmosphere. Crucially, a Red code at a remote volcano like Shishaldin on the Alaska Peninsula may mean a steam-and-ash plume 10 km above the crater — a severe hazard for aircraft transiting the North Pacific — but near-zero hazard for a tourist 200 km away. Context is everything.

## Seismic Tremor: Reading the Shaking

Volcanic earthquakes differ from tectonic earthquakes in character. Observatories distinguish high-frequency (HF) events, caused by brittle fracture of rock as magma forces its way through it, from long-period (LP) events, caused by fluid resonance in cracks and conduits. A swarm of LP events accompanied by harmonic tremor — a sustained, rhythmic shaking — often signals magma or gas moving through the plumbing system. At Kilauea in 2018, hours before the eruption broke out in Leilani Estates, scientists observed an intensifying tremor signal and felt earthquakes exceeding magnitude 5. Public bulletins from the USGS Hawaiian Volcano Observatory (HVO) described these precursors in plain language, updated hourly. Travelers following these updates had clear advance warning to leave threatened areas.

## Ground Deformation: GPS and InSAR

Magma inflating a reservoir beneath a volcano pushes the ground upward and outward. GPS stations permanently installed on volcano flanks measure this displacement in millimetres per day. Interferometric Synthetic Aperture Radar (InSAR), using satellite pairs like Sentinel-1 from the European Space Agency, generates deformation maps updated every few days for hundreds of volcanoes simultaneously. At Campi Flegrei in Italy — the calderic volcanic field underlying the western suburbs of Naples — ground uplift of over 20 cm per year in 2023 and 2024 prompted scientists at the Vesuvius Observatory (INGV–OV) to raise the alert level to Yellow in December 2012, where it has broadly remained. Travelers visiting Pozzuoli, at the heart of the field, can find weekly deformation bulletins on the INGV website summarising how many centimetres the ground has moved in the past seven days.

## Exclusion Zones and Access Restrictions

Alert levels translate directly into exclusion zones — areas where access is forbidden or restricted by civil protection authorities. Etna's summit area above roughly 3,000 m is periodically closed when activity increases; the exclusion radius around the summit craters can reach 9 km during paroxysmal episodes. Merapi in Central Java maintains permanent restricted zones, and after the 2010 eruption that killed over 350 people, a 20-km exclusion zone was imposed for weeks. Sakurajima in Japan issues daily advisories; the volcano erupts multiple times per year, and Kagoshima city, just 8 km away, conducts regular evacuation drills. Always check with the national volcano observatory and local civil protection before hiking near any active volcano.

## Volcanic Hazard Maps

Many observatories publish static hazard maps showing which areas would be affected by different scenarios — lava flows, pyroclastic density currents, lahars (volcanic mudflows), and tephra fall. At Tungurahua in Ecuador, the Instituto Geofísico of the Escuela Politécnica Nacional maintains maps showing how far lava flows and lahars could reach along river valleys toward the city of Baños. At Ruapehu in New Zealand, lahar warning sirens are installed along the Whangaehu River valley, and tourists skiing on the volcano during winter can hear them in real-time tests. Hazard maps do not predict what will happen, but they show what could happen under plausible scenarios and which roads and towns lie within flow paths.

## How to Stay Informed in Real Time

The most reliable sources for volcano status are the national volcano observatory websites. USGS Volcano Hazards Program covers all US volcanoes. INGV covers Italy (Etna, Stromboli, Vesuvius, Campi Flegrei). PVMBG covers all Indonesian volcanoes. GeoNet covers New Zealand. The Volcanic Ash Advisory Centers (VAACs) in London, Tokyo, Anchorage, and other cities issue airspace warnings. The Global Volcanism Program at the Smithsonian Institution publishes weekly volcanic activity reports in English covering the whole world. Many observatories now have social media accounts and mobile apps providing alerts in multiple languages.
