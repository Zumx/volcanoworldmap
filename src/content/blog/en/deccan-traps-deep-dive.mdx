Across the Deccan Plateau of western and central India, a traveller notices something subtle but persistent: the land has a stepped quality, gentle terraces stacked one above another as if the earth itself were a great staircase. These steps are the Deccan Traps — vast sheets of basalt lava that erupted in colossal pulses over a million years, beginning roughly 66 million years ago. They cover more than 500,000 square kilometres, extending from Mumbai inland across Maharashtra, Madhya Pradesh, and Karnataka. Their name derives from the Dutch word for stairs, a tribute to the layered topography they create.

## A Flood Basalt on a Planetary Scale

The Deccan Traps are classified as a Large Igneous Province (LIP) — a category of volcanic event that dwarfs anything witnessed in historical time. Over their eruptive lifetime, they extruded an estimated 500,000 cubic kilometres of basalt, in some places stacking lava flows to depths of more than 3,000 metres. Individual flows can be traced for hundreds of kilometres across the plateau. The magma rose from a mantle plume — a column of abnormally hot rock ascending from deep within the Earth — thought to have been associated with what is now the Réunion hotspot in the Indian Ocean. As the Indian Plate migrated northward, the plume's track shifted, eventually producing the Lakshadweep islands and the Maldivian ridge.

## Timing the Catastrophe: 66 Million Years Ago

Precise radiometric dating using argon-argon methods has established that the bulk of the Deccan eruptions occurred within a geologically tight window centred on 66 million years ago — the same moment as the end-Cretaceous mass extinction that wiped out the non-avian dinosaurs, roughly three-quarters of all species, and countless marine organisms. This coincidence has made the Deccan Traps one of geology's most contentious battlegrounds.

## The Chicxulub Debate

For decades, the asteroid impact hypothesis — championed after Walter and Luis Alvarez discovered the iridium anomaly at the Cretaceous-Paleogene (K-Pg) boundary in 1980 — dominated scientific thinking about the extinction. The Chicxulub impactor, a body at least 10 kilometres in diameter that struck the Yucatán Peninsula, released energy equivalent to billions of nuclear bombs, triggering wildfires, a tsunami across the Gulf of Mexico, and a winter of debris that blocked sunlight for months. But the Deccan eruptions had also been releasing carbon dioxide and sulphur dioxide into the atmosphere for hundreds of thousands of years, and some researchers argue the volcanic gases drove long-term climate warming before the asteroid delivered the final blow. Proponents of a volcanic contribution point out that several extinction pulses occurred before the K-Pg boundary, corresponding to major eruptive phases. Those in the asteroid camp note that the sharpest extinction horizon coincides precisely with the Chicxulub event.

## Did the Impact Intensify the Eruptions?

A provocative hypothesis, developed by Princeton geophysicist Mark Richards and colleagues, proposes that the seismic energy from the Chicxulub impact — which sent powerful seismic waves around the globe — may have triggered a dramatic intensification of the Deccan eruptions. Geological evidence suggests that the most voluminous eruptive pulses occurred in the immediately post-impact interval. If this interpretation holds, the two events were not independent but interacting catastrophes, with the asteroid effectively squeezing more magma out of an already active system. The debate remains unresolved.

## Carbon, Sulphur, and Climate Forcing

Large Igneous Provinces kill primarily through their atmospheric emissions, not their lava. Basaltic magma rising from the mantle carries dissolved carbon dioxide and sulphur dioxide. Carbon dioxide is a long-lived greenhouse gas; sustained Deccan outgassing over hundreds of thousands of years would have driven gradual warming. Sulphur dioxide, by contrast, reacts with water vapour to form sulphate aerosols that cool the atmosphere — but only temporarily, because sulphate aerosols rain out within a few years. The combination of long-term CO2 warming punctuated by short, sharp volcanic winters created the kind of ecological whiplash that stresses ecosystems severely.

## The Basalt Under India's Cities

Today, the Deccan Trap basalts underlie some of India's most densely populated regions. Mumbai sits on them; so do Pune, Nashik, and Aurangabad. The black cotton soils — known as regur — that developed on the weathered basalt are among the most fertile in India, supporting vast cotton, sorghum, and soybean cultivation across Maharashtra. The same rocks that delivered climate chaos 66 million years ago now sustain the livelihoods of millions of farmers on the Vidarbha plateau.

## Dhosi Hill and India's Other Volcanic Heritage

India's volcanic history extends beyond the Deccan. Dhosi Hill in Haryana is a plug of ancient volcanic rock, more than 750 million years old, rising abruptly from the surrounding plain. In parts of Gujarat, mud volcanoes have been documented in the Rann of Kutch, fed by pressurised gases and water from deep sedimentary layers rather than true magma — but dramatic reminders of ongoing geological activity nonetheless. The Barren Island volcano in the Andaman Sea is India's only confirmed active volcano, erupting intermittently since the eighteenth century.

## Reading the Lava Stratigraphy

Geologists studying the Deccan Traps have identified at least thirty distinct lava flow sequences, each representing a separate eruptive episode. By drilling into the sequence and measuring chemical isotopes at different depths, researchers reconstruct the tempo of eruptions, the depth of their source magma, and the environmental changes each pulse caused. Palaeomagnetic data from the flows also record shifts in Earth's magnetic field, allowing correlation with the global timescale. This extraordinarily detailed volcanic stratigraphy has made the Deccan Traps one of the most intensively studied geological formations on Earth.
