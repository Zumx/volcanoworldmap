There is a superlative attached to almost every aspect of Nevado Ojos del Salado. At 6,893 m above sea level, it is the highest active volcano on Earth, the highest peak in the Western Hemisphere after Aconcagua, and the highest summit anywhere outside Asia. Its summit region holds what is generally recognised as the world's highest lake — a small, intensely cold crater pool at roughly 6,800 m, warmed fractionally by fumarolic gases that whisper evidence of the dormant volcanic system beneath. The mountain straddles the Chile-Argentina border in the Atacama zone, and its flanks plunge into one of the driest deserts on Earth.

## Geography and Setting

Ojos del Salado stands in the Puna de Atacama, a high-altitude plateau of extreme aridity shared between northern Chile and northwestern Argentina. The nearest major town is Copiapó in Chile's Atacama Region, roughly 200 km west, from which most expeditions depart. The landscape approaching the volcano from the Chilean side feels almost Martian: reddish-brown volcanic sand, salt flats blinding white under the equatorial sun, and stratovolcanoes rising sharply from the plateau with no transitional foothills. The summit is permanently glaciated on its south face, with ice fields that receive virtually no precipitation — only wind-blown snow accumulation of just a few centimetres per year.

## The Fumarolic Crater Lake

Near the summit — reached by a steep scramble up the final 400 m of loose scree — lies the crater lake that defines the mountain's volcanic status. The lake is typically less than 100 m across, and for much of the year it is frozen solid. In the austral summer, when temperatures at the summit occasionally climb above zero, the ice partially melts to reveal a dark, brine-rich pool. Fumarolic activity has been documented here: small vents emit sulfurous gases, and the ground temperature near the vents measurably exceeds ambient air. This activity was more pronounced in the 1990s, when minor phreatic explosions were reported. The volcano is classified as potentially active, not dormant in the absolute sense.

## A Mountaineering Objective

Ojos del Salado is one of the great high-altitude mountaineering objectives of the Southern Hemisphere, attracting a few hundred climbers per year. The standard route from Chile involves an approach to the Atacama base camp near Laguna Verde, a brilliant turquoise lake coloured by dissolved minerals at around 4,400 m, followed by higher camps at roughly 5,200 m and 5,700 m before the final summit push. Acclimatisation is critical: altitude sickness has turned back many experienced climbers who misjudged the pace. Most successful ascents require three to four weeks in the field. The main technical challenge is altitude itself — the standard route is non-technical but brutally exposed to wind, and storms can form with terrifying speed.

## Chile's Volcanic Chain

Ojos del Salado is the highest point of a volcanic arc that runs along Chile's length. The country's volcanic diversity is remarkable: from the northern altiplano to Tierra del Fuego, active and potentially active volcanoes number well over a hundred. Many are more recently and violently active than Ojos del Salado: Volcán Villarrica, Volcán Llaima, and Volcán Calbuco have all produced major eruptions within living memory. Further north along the Chilean chain, Volcán Chaitén's catastrophic 2008 rhyolitic eruption buried the town of Chaitén under deep ash and pyroclastic material. Volcán Chillán Nuevo and Nevado de Chillán have been in near-continuous activity, and Volcán Llaima holds the distinction of being the most historically active volcano in South America. Volcán Osorno, reflected in the waters of Lago Llanquihue, is frequently compared to Mount Fuji for its symmetry.

## The Atacama Desert Environment

The desert environment surrounding Ojos del Salado is itself a natural wonder. The Atacama is the driest non-polar desert on Earth, and the altiplano sector where the volcano stands receives barely 50 mm of precipitation annually — most of it as brief summer thundershowers driven north by the Bolivian Low. Despite this, life persists: vicuñas graze on ichu grass at 4,000 m, flamingos congregate on salt lakes, and Andean foxes hunt at night. The soil chemistry resembles Martian regolith so closely that NASA has used this landscape to test Mars rover instruments and microbial-detection protocols.

## Access and Permits

Entry to Ojos del Salado from Chile requires a permit from CONAF (Corporación Nacional Forestal) and coordination with the relevant regional office in Copiapó. Numbers are not formally capped, but the permit process filters casual visitors. The Argentine approach from Fiambalá in Catamarca Province is less developed and sees fewer climbers, but it avoids the border crossing at altitude. All expeditions should carry high-altitude rescue protocols: helicopter extraction is possible only from intermediate camps due to thin air at the summit.

## Other Peaks of the Border Zone

The high-altitude border between Chile and Argentina in this sector is lined with extraordinary volcanic summits. Among them, Volcán Maipo at 5,264 m is technically straightforward and glaciated. Volcán Tinguiririca at 4,299 m releases persistent steam. Volcán Azufre, Volcán Antuco near the Biobío River headwaters, and Cerro Marmolejo at 6,108 m round out a sub-region of exceptional vertical range. To the south, Volcán Planchón and Volcán Peteroa form an interconnected system with intermittent explosive episodes. Volcán Quetrupillán and Volcán Carrán represent more explosive, less-visited members of the southern chain.
