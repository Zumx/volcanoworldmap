On a clear day in Portland, Oregon, the view east along the Columbia River corridor terminates in the symmetrical white cone of Mount Hood, rising to 3,429 metres above sea level. The mountain is the highest point in Oregon and the fourth highest in the Cascade Range, a chain of mostly dormant but potentially volatile stratovolcanoes stretching from northern California through Oregon and Washington into southern British Columbia. Mount Hood last experienced significant eruptive activity in the 1780s and 1800s, and while its current alert level is normal (green), the hydrothermal system near its summit, the ongoing degradation of its glaciers, and the growing population in the Sandy River and Zigzag valleys below make it a subject of sustained scientific monitoring by the United States Geological Survey (USGS).

## Cascade Arc Geology

Mount Hood is the product of the Cascadia Subduction Zone, where the Juan de Fuca oceanic plate dives beneath the North American continent. This is the same tectonic boundary responsible for the catastrophic eruption of Mount St. Helens in 1980, the active Crater Lake volcano system (Mazama), and — further south — California's Lassen Peak, which last erupted in 1914-1917. The Cascade Arc contains more than a dozen potentially active stratovolcanoes, including Mounts Baker, Rainier, St. Helens, Adams, Jefferson, and the Three Sisters. Hood sits at the southern end of the Washington-Oregon volcanic cluster, separated from the Three Sisters complex to the south by a broad gap in the arc.

## Glaciers and Hydrothermal Activity

Eleven named glaciers cover the upper flanks of Mount Hood: the Sandy, Zigzag, Reid, Lilly, Coe, Newton Clark, White River, Eliot, Langille, Ladd, and Cathedral glaciers. The Eliot Glacier is the largest in Oregon. These glaciers are the source of the Sandy and Zigzag rivers, which drain the western side of the mountain through communities including Sandy and Brightwood. The glaciers have retreated significantly over the past century; the Eliot Glacier lost roughly 40 percent of its area between 1901 and 2015. Near the summit, fumarolic activity at Crater Rock — a lava dome formed during the most recent eruptions — vents sulphurous gases that melt snow and ice in summer, creating unstable mixed zones of ice and loose volcanic rock called the Devil's Kitchen.

## The Timberline Lodge and Year-Round Skiing

Mount Hood is unusual among North American volcanoes in hosting year-round skiing. The Palmer Snowfield above the Timberline Lodge — a historic 1930s New Deal construction at about 1,800 m — remains snow-covered throughout summer and attracts skiers, snowboarders, and national alpine training programmes from across the United States. Timberline Lodge itself, built by the Works Progress Administration between 1936 and 1938, is one of the finest examples of Cascadian Rustic architecture and was designated a National Historic Landmark in 1977. The lodge is built directly on the flanks of the volcano and faces the risk of burial or damage from future lahars flowing down the Sandy or Zigzag drainages.

## Eruptive History: The Old Maid and Timberline Eruptions

Geologists have divided Hood's recent eruptive history into two main episodes: the Timberline eruptive period, which ended around 1781, and the Old Maid eruptive period, which produced lahars and pyroclastic deposits in the late 18th and early 19th centuries. During the Old Maid period, lahars travelled down the Sandy River valley and deposited sediment that can still be traced downstream for many kilometres. Lewis and Clark, crossing the Columbia River in 1805-1806, noted that Mount Hood appeared to be active, with one journal entry describing what may have been fumarolic steam. The eruptive deposits from these periods cover the lower slopes and are visible in road cuts along Highway 26 near Government Camp.

## Lahar and Debris Avalanche Risk

The primary hazard from a future Hood eruption is not lava or ash but lahars — volcanic mudflows generated when eruption melts snow and ice rapidly. A large lahar from Hood could travel down the Sandy River valley and reach the Columbia River near Portland within a few hours. The USGS estimates that lahar deposits from a major Hood eruption could bury roads, bridges, and communities in the Sandy valley. Debris avalanches — non-volcanic mass movements of the hydrothermally weakened summit cone — pose an additional risk independent of an eruption. The summit area is permeated by weak, altered volcanic rock softened by decades of hydrothermal activity at temperatures above 90°C in some vents.

## Climbing Mount Hood

Mount Hood is the second most climbed glaciated peak in the world after Mount Fuji, with an estimated 10,000 mountaineers attempting the summit each year. The standard south side route from Timberline Lodge follows the Palmer Snowfield to Crater Rock, then traverses a steep headwall above the bergschrund to the summit. The route is technically moderate but requires crampons, ice axe, and rope on all sections above the snowline. The primary hazard is the Pearly Gates — a narrow couloir between two pillars of rock near the summit — which is prone to rockfall and ice fragments dislodging from the deteriorating rim. Two fumarolic zones at the top of the headwall, the Hot Rocks and the Devil's Kitchen, are dangerous in summer due to collapsing snow bridges over steam vents.

## Monitoring and Future Risk

The USGS Cascade Volcano Observatory monitors Hood with seismometers, GPS stations, and webcams. The mountain has experienced occasional small earthquake swarms in recent years, consistent with background tectonic and geothermal activity rather than magmatic unrest. The growing exurban population in the Sandy River valley and along Highway 26 — including communities such as Sandy, Brightwood, Zigzag, and Rhododendron — increases the societal exposure to volcanic and lahar risk. The National Forest Service and Oregon Office of Emergency Management maintain hazard zone maps and have developed communication protocols for the various scenarios of future volcanic unrest.
