In June 1912, residents of Kodiak Island, 160 kilometres from the eruption site, choked on ash so thick they could not see a lantern held at arm's length. Ships at sea reported ash falls that lasted days. Yet the source of this catastrophic eruption — Novarupta vent and the adjacent Mount Katmai — lay in such remote wilderness on the Alaska Peninsula that no human witnessed the event at close range. The eruption expelled roughly 13 cubic kilometres of magma, making it the largest volcanic eruption in the 20th century in North America and one of the largest worldwide since Krakatau in 1883. The following year, geologist Robert Griggs explored the valley downwind of the blast and found a landscape billowing with tens of thousands of fumaroles — a sight so astonishing he named it the Valley of Ten Thousand Smokes. That valley, and the caldera lake atop the collapsed Mount Katmai, remain among the most dramatic volcanic landscapes on Earth.

## The Novarupta Eruption: Mechanics of a Surprise

The eruption lasted approximately 60 hours from 6 to 9 June 1912. Novarupta, a then-unknown vent 10 kilometres west of Mount Katmai's summit, was the primary outlet for the magma. In the most violent phase, the eruption column reached an estimated 30 kilometres into the atmosphere. Pyroclastic flows raced down the Ukak River valley, depositing a layer of ash and pumice 200 metres deep across a 40-square-kilometre area. So much magma drained from the magma body beneath Katmai's summit that the mountain's cone collapsed, forming a caldera roughly 4 kilometres wide and 600 metres deep. The caldera has since filled with a lake approximately 250 metres deep, its water a striking turquoise blue from dissolved volcanic minerals.

## The Valley of Ten Thousand Smokes Today

When Griggs visited in 1917, the Valley of Ten Thousand Smokes was alive with fumaroles venting steam and volcanic gases at temperatures up to 645 °C. By the mid-20th century most fumaroles had cooled and gone quiet, but the valley floor remains a stark, Mars-like landscape of grey and ochre ash, cut by deeply eroded river canyons where the Ukak River has carved its way through the 1912 deposit. Today the valley is accessible by an 18-kilometre dirt road from Brooks Camp, the main visitor hub, followed by a 4-kilometre hike to the Valley Overlook. The National Park Service operates a bus service from Brooks Camp to the road's end during summer.

## Katmai National Park and Preserve

The volcanic landscape is protected within Katmai National Park and Preserve, established as a national monument in 1918 and expanded to park status in 1980. The park covers 16,564 square kilometres — larger than the state of Connecticut — and is one of the least visited national parks in the United States due to its extreme remoteness. There are no roads connecting Katmai to the outside world; all access is by floatplane from King Salmon, a small town on the Alaska Peninsula with a commercial airport, or by boat along the coast from Homer. The main season runs from June to September, when weather is most forgiving and bears are most visible.

## Brooks Falls: Bears and Salmon

While the volcanic landscape is the geological centrepiece, Katmai is perhaps more famous internationally for the annual gathering of brown bears at Brooks Falls. Each July and again in September, dozens of bears — some among the largest brown bears on Earth, routinely exceeding 400 kilograms — congregate at the falls to intercept sockeye salmon ascending the Brooks River on their spawning migration. The viewing platform at Brooks Falls, accessed on a 1.5-kilometre boardwalk from Brooks Camp, routinely holds 40 to 60 bears in the river below during peak season. Reservations for the viewing platform are now required and competitive, bookable through Recreation.gov.

## The Caldera Lake at Katmai's Summit

Mount Katmai's pre-eruption summit stood at approximately 2,317 metres. After the 1912 collapse it now reaches 2,047 metres, with the remaining rim encircling the caldera lake. The lake can be viewed only by floatplane charter from Brooks Camp or King Salmon, as no trail reaches the summit crater. Winter ice covers the lake from October to May. Fumaroles persist along the caldera walls, venting gases that warm the immediate environment — the caldera lake is slightly warmer than ambient temperature even in the coldest months.

## Hiking and Wilderness

Katmai supports a network of maintained trails around Brooks Camp, including the Dumpling Mountain Trail (4 kilometres return, 420 m elevation gain) with views across Naknek Lake and distant volcanic peaks. The surrounding wilderness is bear country, and all hikers must carry bear spray and attend the mandatory bear safety talk at the Brooks Camp visitor centre before entering the backcountry. Backcountry camping requires a permit and a canister. The Savonoski Loop, a 6-day paddling and portaging route through the park's interior lake system, is one of Alaska's great wilderness canoe journeys.

## Scientific Legacy

The 1912 eruption remains one of the most scientifically studied volcanic events in history, largely because it occurred in the same year that systematic scientific monitoring of volcanoes was beginning to be institutionalised. The pyroclastic deposit in the Valley of Ten Thousand Smokes has been used as a standard reference for pyroclastic flow dynamics and welded tuff formation. More recently, ice cores from Greenland and Antarctic have confirmed the 1912 event's global atmospheric signature — a clear sulphate spike that helps scientists calibrate ancient eruption records worldwide.
