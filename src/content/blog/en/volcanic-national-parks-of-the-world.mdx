A volcanic national park is never merely a protected piece of scenery. It is an ongoing geological experiment, a record of eruptions spanning millions of years, and often one of the most biologically improbable environments on Earth. The 250 or so national parks and equivalently protected areas centred on volcanic landscapes — from the steaming floor of Kilauea's summit caldera to the dormant trachyte domes of Auvergne — represent a global network of living laboratories where geology, ecology, and culture intersect in ways found nowhere else.

## Hawaii Volcanoes National Park, USA

Established in 1916, Hawaii Volcanoes National Park on the Big Island encompasses two of the world's most active volcanoes: Kilauea and Mauna Loa. Kilauea's East Rift Zone has produced nearly continuous eruptions since 1983, and the 2018 Lower East Rift Zone eruption added roughly 3.5 km² of new land to the island while destroying entire neighbourhoods in Leilani Estates. The park's Chain of Craters Road descends 1,100 m from the summit plateau to the coast through a landscape of historic lava flows, pit craters, and lava tube skylights. The Thurston Lava Tube — also called Nāhuku — is a 500-year-old lava tube accessible on a short loop walk. The park is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and a hotspot for volcanology research, with the USGS Hawaiian Volcano Observatory situated inside its boundaries.

## Tongariro National Park, New Zealand

Tongariro in the central North Island was New Zealand's first national park and the fourth-oldest in the world. It contains three active stratovolcanoes — Tongariro, Ngauruhoe, and Ruapehu — and is also a UNESCO World Heritage Site recognised for both its natural and cultural values. The Tongariro Alpine Crossing, widely regarded as the best one-day hike in New Zealand, traverses lava fields, sulphur-steaming vents, and the vivid Emerald and Blue Lakes formed in explosion craters. Ruapehu (2,797 m) last erupted significantly in 2007 and hosts New Zealand's largest skifields at Whakapapa and Turoa. The park's Maori spiritual significance — as the ancestral homeland of Ngati Tuwharetoa — is inseparable from its geological identity.

## Parc Naturel Régional des Volcans d'Auvergne, France

The Auvergne volcanic chain in south-central France is the largest volcanic ensemble in western Europe, encompassing roughly 80 dormant volcanic structures along the Chaîne des Puys UNESCO World Heritage Site corridor. The youngest eruptions here occurred around 6,000 years ago at the Puy de Dôme and related vents. The Puy de Dôme itself (1,465 m) is accessible by a rack railway and affords panoramic views over the Limagne plain. The Massif du Sancy (1,886 m) is a heavily eroded stratovolcano forming the highest point in the Massif Central. The regional natural park designation covers over 395,000 hectares, protecting a landscape of lava plateaus, volcanic lakes (the Lac Pavin and Lac d'Aydat being the most famous), and traditional Auvergnat farming communities still inhabiting volcanic basalt villages.

## Kamchatka Volcanoes, Russia

The Kamchatka Peninsula in the Russian Far East harbours one of the world's most spectacular concentrations of active and dormant volcanoes, with 30 currently active centres in a peninsula 1,200 km long. Kronotsky Nature Reserve protects the Valley of Geysers, second only to Yellowstone as the world's largest geyser field, as well as the Kronotsky stratovolcano itself (3,528 m). The six separate protected areas covering Kamchatka's volcanoes are collectively a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Klyuchevskaya Sopka (4,750 m), outside the strict reserve boundaries but within the broader protected zone, is the highest active volcano in Eurasia and has erupted dozens of times since records began, including major eruptions in 2021 and 2022.

## Galápagos National Park, Ecuador

The Galápagos archipelago, straddling the equator 1,000 km west of the Ecuadorian mainland, sits over the Galápagos hotspot and is still volcanically active. Isabela Island hosts six overlapping shield volcano calderas, including Volcán Wolf (1,707 m), which erupted in 2015. Fernandina Island's La Cumbre caldera erupted in 2024, producing lava flows that reached the coast. The extraordinary biodiversity that famously shaped Darwin's theory of natural selection exists precisely because these remote, recently volcanically generated islands were colonised by chance dispersal, making the geological and biological histories inseparable.

## Mount Rainier National Park, USA

Mount Rainier (4,392 m) in Washington State is the highest volcano in the Cascade Range and one of the most potentially hazardous in North America, partly because its flanks are heavily mantled by glaciers and debris-avalanche deposits. The national park established in 1899 draws over two million visitors annually to its wildflower meadows, 93 glaciers, and summit crater. The USGS Cascades Volcano Observatory monitors Rainier continuously and ranks it as one of the 18 Decade Volcanoes identified by the International Association of Volcanology and Chemistry of the Earth's Interior (IAVCEI) as warranting intensive study because of their proximity to populated areas.

## Erta Ale and Simien Mountains, Ethiopia

Ethiopia's Afar region hosts Erta Ale, an active basaltic shield volcano on the Danakil Depression — one of the lowest and hottest places on Earth — that has maintained a persistent lava lake in its summit caldera for over a century. The surrounding Afar Triangle is where the African and Arabian plates are rifting apart, and where the earliest hominid fossils, including Australopithecus afarensis, were found in the Awash Valley. This unique conjunction of active rifting, extreme geology, and palaeontological heritage makes the broader Afar protected landscape among the most scientifically extraordinary in the world.
