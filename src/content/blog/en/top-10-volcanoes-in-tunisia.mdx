Tunisia is not a country that appears on most volcanic itineraries, and for good reason: its mainland is geologically dominated by sedimentary basins, fold belts, and the ancient crystalline basement of the Saharan Shield. But the story changes dramatically in the Sicilian Channel, the shallow sea between Tunisia and Sicily. Here, the convergence of the African and Eurasian plates has produced a scattered archipelago of volcanic islands, the most important of which — Pantelleria — sits just 70 kilometres from the Tunisian coast and has been erupting periodically for hundreds of thousands of years. The geological and cultural connections between Tunisia and these volcanic islands are deep, and the channel floor itself conceals submarine volcanic features that remain poorly mapped.

## Monte Gibéle — The Summit of Pantelleria

Monte Gibéle rises to 836 metres as the highest point on Pantelleria, the volcanic island that anchors the geology of this part of the Sicilian Channel. The mountain is a composite of trachytic lava flows and domes erupted from the central complex of the island, built up over multiple eruptive cycles in the past 300,000 years. From the summit, on clear days, both the Tunisian coast and the distant outline of Sicily are visible simultaneously, underscoring the island's position as a geological and geographical bridge between two continents.

## Kúddia Mída — The Central Caldera Rim

Pantelleria's volcanic heart is a nested caldera system, and Kúddia Mída marks one of the most prominent sections of the caldera rim. The term kúddia — from the Arabic word for hill — reflects the island's long history of Arabic-speaking settlement following the Arab conquest of 700 CE. The caldera interior is lowered relative to the rim and is now filled by Lake Specchio di Venere, whose warm, alkaline waters are heated by hydrothermal activity directly below. Fumaroles and boiling mud pools along the lake margin remain active.

## Kúddie Patíte — Tuff Rings of the Southern Shore

The southern coast of Pantelleria is ringed by a series of tuff cone remnants, of which Kúddie Patíte is among the most intact. These features formed in shallow marine or coastal phreatomagmatic eruptions, where seawater mixing with rising magma generated explosions that built low, broad rings of cemented tuff. The basaltic and trachytic tuff is heavily eroded by the Mediterranean sea and wind, but the crater forms remain clearly defined when viewed from above, their dark rings contrasting with the blue sea.

## Fossa del Russo — Hot Ground and Steaming Vents

The Fossa del Russo — Rus­sian's pit — is one of Pantelleria's most accessible hydrothermal features, a low depression near the village of Scauri where the ground temperature is elevated and fumarolic gases escape from multiple vents. The surrounding basalt is stained yellow with sulphur and white with hydrothermal minerals. Despite its modest visual drama, the Fossa del Russo is volcanologically important because it lies along a fault zone that connects the central caldera with the deeper Sicilian Channel rifts.

## Gelhamár — The Sentinel Cone

Gelhamár is a trachytic dome on the eastern flank of Pantelleria whose form has been smoothed by millennia of erosion but retains the characteristic bulging profile of a viscous-lava extrusion. Domes like Gelhamár form when highly viscous magma piles up at the vent rather than flowing freely; the resulting structure is mechanically strong but prone to collapse, and the surrounding terrain preserves the debris of past partial failures. The dome is now terraced with vineyards producing the island's famous Zibibbo grapes, the same variety used in Passito di Pantelleria wine.

## Gelfisér — Fumarole Field

Gelfisér is a hydrothermal field on Pantelleria's north-west coast where submarine and coastal hot springs are accompanied by gas venting and discoloured water. The hot springs — called bagni — have been used since antiquity; Roman-period structures for bathing still survive in partially submerged form along this coastline. The Gelfisér field is evidence that the volcano's hydrothermal system extends well below sea level and that the Sicilian Channel seafloor here is thermally anomalous.

## Kúddia Bianca — White Tuff Plateau

Kúddia Bianca (the white hill) takes its name from the pale pumice and altered tuff that gives this part of Pantelleria's interior an almost lunar pallor. The pale material is hydrothermally altered basalt in which iron minerals have been bleached and replaced by silica and alunite. The plateau sits at around 300 metres elevation and is crossed by ancient stone tracks lined with the dark basalt walls that characterise the island's traditional agricultural landscape.

## Kúddie Rosse — Red Scoria Hills

The Kúddie Rosse — red hills — are a cluster of scoria cone remnants on Pantelleria's south-east, their vivid iron-oxide colouration the product of high-temperature oxidation of basaltic tephra during eruption. The deep red and orange tones contrast sharply with the surrounding dark basalt and blue sea, creating one of the most visually striking colour contrasts in any Mediterranean volcanic landscape. The Kúddie Rosse are also among the youngest volcanic features on the island, dating to eruptions within the past 10,000 years.

## Kúddia Randázzo — A Lava Flow Cone

Kúddia Randázzo is a well-preserved scoria cone from whose base a basaltic lava flow extends south-west towards the sea. The flow is young enough that its surface has not yet been deeply weathered, and the ropy pahoehoe texture is locally preserved beneath a thin soil. Walking the flow surface reveals pressure ridges, lava tubes with collapsed roofs, and the occasional intact skylights through which the molten core once moved. The cone summit offers a clear view over the southern lava fields that characterise this part of the island.

## Kúddia del Gallo and the Submarine Backdrop

Kúddia del Gallo — the rooster's hill — is a coastal cone on Pantelleria's western shore whose eruptions contributed material to both the land surface and the adjacent shallow seafloor. Submarine surveys of the Sicilian Channel have identified numerous bathymetric anomalies — seamounts, scarps, and crater rims — that together suggest the volcanic province extends far beyond the island shores into the channel floor. These submarine features, which include structures in Tunisian territorial waters, represent the most underexplored part of the regional volcanic system and are likely to yield new eruption sites as mapping technology improves.
