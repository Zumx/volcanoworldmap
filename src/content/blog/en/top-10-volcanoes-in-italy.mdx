No country combines history and active volcanism the way Italy does. The same
peninsula that buried Pompeii now lets you drive partway up Etna for dinner and
ride a boat to Stromboli to watch fountains of lava after sunset.

## 1. Mount Etna, Sicily

Europe's largest and most consistently active volcano dominates eastern Sicily.
Cable cars climb to 2,500 m, jeep tours push higher, and the summit craters are
almost always doing something visible. Etna is a UNESCO site and a working
mountain — vineyards, pistachio orchards, and ski lifts all share its slopes.

## 2. Stromboli, Aeolian Islands

The "Lighthouse of the Mediterranean" has erupted in small bursts every ten or
twenty minutes for thousands of years. Boats from Lipari and Salina deliver
visitors for sunset viewings of the Sciara del Fuoco, where glowing fragments
roll into the sea.

## 3. Mount Vesuvius, Campania

The most famous volcano in the world. The crater is reachable by a short walk
from the bus stop above Ercolano, and the view across the Bay of Naples
explains exactly why Romans were prepared to live in its shadow.

## 4. Campi Flegrei, Naples

The supervolcano next door — a sprawling caldera west of Naples where the
ground itself rises and falls in slow-motion. Solfatara's fumaroles and
Pozzuoli's harbour are the dramatic, accessible parts of a system far larger
than Vesuvius.

## 5. Vulcano, Aeolian Islands

The island that gave volcanoes their name. A short, sulphur-scented climb to
the Gran Cratere rim is one of the most rewarding easy volcano hikes anywhere
in the Mediterranean.

## 6. Mount Epomeo, Ischia

The high point of Ischia is a volcanic dome whose slow uplift drives the
island's famous hot springs and spa culture. Walks across the green tuff slopes
end at a 360-degree summit panorama.

## 7. Lipari, Aeolian Islands

A volcanic island built from successive rhyolitic domes; the obsidian and
pumice flows above Canneto are an open-air lesson in Mediterranean volcanism.

## 8. Pantelleria

A volcanic island closer to Tunisia than Sicily, with the lake-filled Specchio
di Venere caldera and natural saunas in lava grottoes. Pantelleria's wines and
capers grow on volcanic soils that taste of the place.

## 9. Monte Albano (Colli Albani), Lazio

Rome itself sits beside a quiet volcanic field. The Albano and Nemi lakes fill
craters in the Alban Hills, and the area is officially classed as a dormant
rather than extinct system.

## 10. Marsili Seamount, Tyrrhenian Sea

Italy's largest volcano is underwater. The Marsili Seamount rises 3,000 m from
the seabed between Sicily and Calabria — invisible from the surface but a
significant feature in regional hazard studies.

## How to plan an Italian volcano trip

The classic route is a week between Naples and Sicily: Vesuvius and Campi
Flegrei first, then a ferry south to the Aeolians for Stromboli and Vulcano,
finishing with Etna. Boat schedules dictate the pacing; check INGV's hazard
pages before each segment, especially for Stromboli and Etna where access
zones change with activity.
