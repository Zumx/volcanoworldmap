El Salvador is the smallest country in Central America, and one of the
most volcanic. Twenty-three stratovolcanoes line up along the Central
American volcanic arc, a few of them frequently active, all of them
within an easy day's drive of San Salvador. The coffee that flows out
of the country is grown on soil from these eruptions.

## 1. Santa Ana (Ilamatepec)

The country's tallest volcano at 2,381 m, with an active sulphur-yellow
crater lake. A 2005 eruption killed two people and forced evacuations.
Climbed in a guided 3–4 hour round trip from the foot.

## 2. San Salvador (Quezaltepec)

The volcano overlooking the capital, last erupting in 1917. A summit
crater is reached on a short hike from the El Boquerón National Park
gate.

## 3. Izalco

A near-perfect cone that grew from a flat plain in the early 18th
century. Continuously erupting for around 200 years, it earned the
nickname "Lighthouse of the Pacific" before it stopped in 1966. Still
climbable from Cerro Verde.

## 4. Cerro Verde

The forested older stratovolcano next to Izalco, with cloud-forest
hiking and parking for both Izalco and Santa Ana climbs. The Cerro
Verde National Park is the centrepiece of the western volcano
triangle.

## 5. San Miguel (Chaparrastique)

Eastern El Salvador's main active volcano, erupting in 2013 after a
long period of quiet. Coffee plantations cover the lower slopes.

## 6. Tecapa

A small stratovolcano with a turquoise crater lake (Laguna de Alegría)
inside its summit crater. Visited from the town of Alegría — one of
the country's coolest tourist drives.

## 7. Conchagua

Overlooking the Gulf of Fonseca in the east, this 1,243-m stratovolcano
gives a panoramic view across to Honduras and Nicaragua. A short drive
plus walk from La Unión.

## 8. Ilopango

A vast caldera lake east of San Salvador, formed by a 535 CE
"Tierra Blanca Joven" eruption that may have darkened the global
climate for years. Today: a swimming and weekend lake.

## 9. Boquerón (San Salvador summit crater)

The dramatic summit crater of the San Salvador volcano, 1.5 km across,
500 m deep. The inner crater contains a smaller cinder cone from a
1917 eruption.

## 10. Coatepeque

A caldera lake west of San Salvador, formed by major Pleistocene
eruptions. A weekend escape with lakeside restaurants and boating.
Hydrothermal vents along the shore.

## Why El Salvador is so volcanic

The Cocos Plate is being subducted north-eastward beneath the
Caribbean Plate at about 7 cm/year — one of the fastest convergence
rates in Latin America. The Central American volcanic arc runs the
length of the country, and the dense population sits directly on top.

## Safety and access

Major volcanoes are visited under guide arrangements via the national
park system (MARN). Some sites have had security issues in the past;
check current advisories before remote trips. Coffee farm tours combine
naturally with volcano-side stays.
