Stand on a fresh lava flow in Hawaii or the Galápagos and you are standing on a blank page. The rock is barren, sharp, and still warm in places. Within months, pioneer lichens begin colonising the surface. Within years, ferns arrive, then shrubs, then trees, each wave modifying conditions for the next. Within thousands of years, species found nowhere else on Earth can evolve in isolation on that very substrate. This succession — from bare volcanic rock to complex, endemic-rich ecosystem — is one of the most powerful engines of biological diversity the planet has produced, and it is driven directly by volcanic activity.

## The Galápagos: Evolution in Real Time

The Galápagos Archipelago, sitting astride the Nazca and Cocos tectonic plates some 900 kilometres west of Ecuador, is still volcanically active. Fernandina and Isabela, the westernmost islands, sit over the Galápagos hotspot and experience eruptions every few years — Fernandina erupted in 2009 and again in 2018. The older eastern islands, like San Cristóbal and Española, have drifted away from the hotspot, their volcanism extinct. This age gradient across the archipelago has been critical to its biodiversity: species colonised older islands, evolved in isolation, dispersed to younger islands, and diversified further. Charles Darwin's finches — actually a group of related tanagers — diversified into 15 or more species across the islands partly because volcanic activity created and destroyed barriers to movement on a geological timescale.

## Hawaiian Silverswords and Adaptive Radiation

Hawaii is the world's most isolated archipelago, sitting over a mantle plume that has been generating new volcanic islands for roughly 70 million years. The current Big Island has five shield volcanoes, two of which — Mauna Loa and Kīlauea — are among the most continuously active on Earth. The Hawaiian silversword alliance — a group of about 50 plant species including the iconic ʻāhinahina (Argyroxiphium sandwicense) that grows only on the cinder cones above 2,000 metres on Haleakalā and Mauna Kea — evolved from a single tarweed ancestor that colonised the islands roughly 5 million years ago. From that one colonist, plants radiated into bogs, forests, dry shrublands, and volcanic deserts, exploiting habitats created and refreshed by ongoing eruptions.

## Primary Succession: The First Wave of Life

When lava cools and solidifies, it presents one of the most inhospitable environments on land: no soil, no organic matter, extreme temperatures, and often high acidity from volcanic gases. Yet life colonises quickly. In Hawaii, the native ʻōhiʻa lehua tree (Metrosideros polymorpha) is often among the first woody plants to take root in fresh lava fields, sometimes appearing within a decade of an eruption. Its secret is extreme genetic plasticity — the same species grows as a shrub on a two-year-old flow and as a 30-metre canopy tree in a 400-year-old forest. Mosses and lichens prepare the substrate, bacteria fix nitrogen from the atmosphere, and gradually a soil layer accumulates. Each stage of succession provides different niches, and species that evolve to exploit early-succession lava environments exist nowhere else.

## Isolation and Speciation

For evolution, isolation is everything. When a lava flow cuts through a forest, it can sever populations of animals or plants, creating two separate breeding groups. Over thousands of generations, these groups diverge. On the island of Isabela in the Galápagos, four distinct subspecies of giant tortoise evolved on separate volcanic shields that were once isolated by lava barriers and are now connected — Alcedo, Sierra Negra, Cerro Azul, and Volcán Wolf each support morphologically distinct tortoise populations. On Hawaii, the same principle explains the extraordinary diversity of honeycreepers (Drepanidae): a single finch-like ancestor colonised the archipelago and diversified into over 50 species exploiting different food sources and habitats on different islands and lava-age zones.

## Lava Tube Ecosystems

Underground, volcanic activity creates a different kind of biodiversity laboratory. Lava tubes — tunnels formed when the outer crust of a lava flow solidifies while the molten interior drains away — can persist for millions of years and develop their own cave ecosystems entirely isolated from the surface. On the Canary Islands, Tenerife's Cueva del Viento is one of the largest lava tube systems in the world, and it hosts entirely endemic invertebrates: blind beetles, spiders, and millipedes that evolved over millions of years in complete darkness and never leave the cave. Similar cave fauna have been described from the lava tubes of the Galápagos, Hawaii, and Iceland. These ecosystems depend on organic material washing in from the surface and on chemolithotrophic bacteria that derive energy from minerals rather than sunlight.

## Volcanic Soils and Tropical Forest Productivity

Beyond islands, volcanic soils on continents drive exceptional above-ground biodiversity. The volcanic highlands of Sumatra and Java in Indonesia support some of the highest tropical forest species richness on Earth, partly because Andosols — soils derived from volcanic ash — are extraordinarily fertile: highly porous, rich in phosphorus and trace elements, and with excellent water-retention properties. The flanks of Mount Kinabalu in Borneo, though not purely volcanic, incorporate volcanic substrates that support an astonishing 6,000 plant species. The Albertine Rift in central Africa, where the Virunga Volcanic Chain including Nyiragongo and Nyamuragira has erupted repeatedly, supports the mountain gorilla (Gorilla beringei beringei) — a species confined almost entirely to the high-altitude forests on the flanks of ancient volcanic mountains.

## Eruptions as Reset Events

Not all volcanic effects on biodiversity are constructive. Major eruptions can cause local extinctions. The 1980 Mount St. Helens eruption in Washington State destroyed 600 square kilometres of forest, killed vast numbers of animals, and stripped the landscape to bare rock and ash. Yet within five years, colonisation began from surviving refugia in pockets that the blast had missed, and from adjacent unaffected areas. Within 40 years, the landscape hosts more than 300 plant species, and the elk, wolf, and black bear populations have rebounded. The eruption, catastrophic in the short term, drove a mini-evolutionary experiment in primary succession and recolonisation that ecologists have monitored continuously ever since.
