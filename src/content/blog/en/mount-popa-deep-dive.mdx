Rising abruptly from the flat dusty plains south of Bagan in central Myanmar, Mount Popa is one of those places where geology and devotion have become inseparable. At 1,518 metres above sea level, the main volcanic massif — known locally as Taung Ma-gyi or "mother mountain" — is an extinct stratovolcano that last erupted around 442 CE, though hydrothermal activity continues. Beside it stands Popa Taung Kalat, a sheer volcanic plug rising 737 metres from its base, its summit crowned with a gilded monastery complex that draws pilgrims from across Myanmar. The two formations together create one of the most visually arresting and spiritually charged landscapes in Southeast Asia.

## Geological Origins and Structure

Mount Popa is the product of repeated eruptions through the Irrawaddy sedimentary basin, pushing alkaline basaltic and trachytic magmas to the surface across a period spanning several million years. The main cone is a classic eroded stratovolcano with concentric ridges of solidified lava and pyroclastic deposits. Popa Taung Kalat is a volcanic neck — the hardened magma plug that filled the central vent of an older, now-eroded volcano. As the surrounding softer rock eroded away over millions of years, the harder plug was left standing, creating the extraordinary spire visible from dozens of kilometres across the central dry zone. The rock is a dark andesite-basalt, heavily fractured and colonised by dense vegetation except on the near-vertical faces.

## The Spiritual Significance of Nat Mountain

Popa means "flower" in Sanskrit, and the mountain is considered the home of the most powerful nat spirits in the Burmese animist tradition. Myanmar's official pantheon of 37 nats includes several whose legends are tied directly to Mount Popa. The Mahagiri nats — two sibling spirits whose tragic story involves betrayal and fire — are among the most important, and their shrines dominate both the mountain and Popa Taung Kalat. Each year two major festivals at Mount Popa draw thousands of pilgrims: one in the Burmese month of Nayon (around June) and a larger one in Tazaungmon (around November). During these festivals the air is thick with incense, the sound of cymbals, and the movements of nat kadaws — spirit mediums — who channel the nats in elaborate ritual performance.

## Climbing Taung Kalat

The pilgrimage route to the monastery atop Taung Kalat involves ascending a covered stairway of 777 steps clinging to the plug's flanks, flanked by statues of nats and the occasional troupe of macaque monkeys who regard the pilgrims with studied indifference. The views from the top are extraordinary: on clear mornings the great plain of Bagan is visible to the northwest, its ancient pagoda spires rising above the haze, while to the east the Irrawaddy River glints in the distance. The summit monastery contains layered shrines, gilded Buddha images, and the resident nat figurines dressed in elaborate costumes. Footwear must be removed at the base, and the stone steps — polished by centuries of bare feet — can be slippery after rain.

## Mount Popa as an Ecological Island

Beyond its spiritual role, Mount Popa is a critical ecological refuge in an otherwise heavily degraded agricultural landscape. The volcanic soil supports a forest of remarkable density and diversity rising through tropical dry forest into montane evergreen woodland near the summit. This forest acts as a sky island — an isolated patch of habitat surrounded by arid cultivation — and supports species found nowhere else in the region of Bagan's dry zone. Leopards, wild boar, and the endemic Popa langur (Trachypithecus popa), a critically endangered primate discovered as a distinct species only in 2020, all shelter in the forest. The Popa langur, with its distinctive dark face and pale grey body, is found only on this volcano; global population estimates suggest fewer than 250 individuals remain.

## The Popa Langur: A New Species on the Edge

The formal description of Trachypithecus popa in the journal Zoological Research in 2020 — based on museum specimens and field surveys — drew international attention to the mountain's biodiversity. The langur was previously lumped with closely related colobine monkeys in the region, but genetic and morphological analysis confirmed its distinctiveness. Its population is confined to Mount Popa National Park and a handful of nearby forest patches. Threats include the loss of surrounding forest to agriculture and charcoal production, and direct disturbance from an expanding pilgrim infrastructure. Conservation programmes led by Fauna and Flora International and local partners have begun community monitoring and habitat corridor work around the mountain.

## Mount Popa National Park

The Myanmar government designated the area as Mount Popa National Park in 2001, covering approximately 250 square kilometres of volcanic massif and surrounding dry-zone forest. The park boundaries encompass the main volcanic cone, Taung Kalat, and several smaller volcanic features including Twin Taung and the hydrothermal springs in the lower flanks. Management has historically been challenged by the overlap of religious pilgrimage infrastructure with conservation objectives. The pilgrimage economy brings significant income to local communities but also drives construction, waste, and the macaque population problem — hundreds of semi-habituated macaques line the stairways and can be aggressive toward visitors carrying food.

## Hydrothermal Activity and Residual Volcanism

Though Mount Popa's last major eruption was roughly 1,600 years ago, the volcanic system is not entirely dormant. Hydrothermal springs emerge from the lower flanks of the massif, particularly to the southwest near the Lower Chindwin drainage. Water temperatures in the springs can reach 60 to 70°C, and the surrounding soil is stained with sulphur deposits. These thermal features indicate that residual heat and volcanic gases still percolate through the system. Periodic earthquake swarms have been recorded in the broader Bagan region — Myanmar sits atop the Sagaing Fault, one of the most seismically active strike-slip systems in Asia — and these may reactivate hydrothermal circulation periodically.
