In the hierarchy of volcanic hazards, airborne ash is perhaps the most deceptive: invisible on radar, capable of travelling thousands of kilometres from the source, and lethal to jet engines without warning. The eruption of Galunggung in West Java, Indonesia in 1982 provided one of the most dramatic demonstrations of this hazard in aviation history. On the night of 24 June 1982, a British Airways Boeing 747 — flight BA009 — flew into the ash cloud generated by Galunggung and lost all four engines at cruising altitude over the Indian Ocean. The crew nursed the powerless aircraft into a controlled glide for 13 minutes before the engines restarted below the ash layer, allowing an emergency landing in Jakarta. Three months later, a Singapore Airlines 747 experienced nearly identical engine failures in the same airspace for the same reason.

## The Volcano's Setting

Galunggung (also spelled Galunggong in older sources) rises to 2,168 m in the Tasikmalaya Regency of West Java Province, about 80 km south of Bandung. It is a stratovolcano with a distinctive horseshoe-shaped caldera open to the south, the result of a prehistoric sector collapse that sent a massive debris avalanche toward the Indian Ocean coast. The modern cone has been built inside this caldera over thousands of years. The slopes are deeply dissected by rivers, and the surrounding lowlands are some of the most fertile agricultural land in Java, watered by mineral-rich volcanic soils. Paddy fields and tea plantations extend almost to the base of the cone.

## The 1982 Eruption

The eruption began in April 1982 and continued intermittently until January 1983. Over this period, Galunggung produced more than 30 eruption episodes, alternating between explosive bursts generating ash columns up to 20 km high and lava effusion events that built new material inside the caldera. The eruption killed more than 60 people directly and displaced around 35,000, destroying villages in the lahar-prone valleys on the volcano's flanks. The ash fell on Bandung and reached as far as Australia. What made this eruption globally significant beyond Indonesia was the aviation dimension: it forced the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) to establish systematic protocols for detecting and avoiding volcanic ash clouds, protocols that remain in force today and that were critical during subsequent events like the 2010 Eyjafjallajökull eruption over Europe.

## How Volcanic Ash Destroys Jet Engines

Understanding what happened on BA009 requires understanding the mechanics of ash ingestion. Volcanic ash differs from ordinary particulate matter: its particles are hard, abrasive glass and mineral shards that sandblast the compressor blades of a jet engine, stripping protective coatings and disrupting the aerodynamic profiles of the blades. More critically, ash melts inside the turbine section at temperatures above around 1,100°C, coating the turbine nozzles and blocking cooling passages. All four engines on BA009 glowed with a ghostly blue light — St Elmo's Fire produced by ash particles — before flaming out one by one. Captain Eric Moody's radio call remains one of aviation's most understated emergency messages: "Ladies and gentlemen, this is your captain speaking. We have a small problem. All four engines have stopped."

## Post-Eruption: Volcanic Ash Advisory Centres

The Galunggung incidents triggered a global restructuring of aviation safety planning around volcanic hazards. Nine Volcanic Ash Advisory Centres (VAACs) were established under ICAO coordination, each responsible for tracking ash clouds in a defined global sector. The system has since been tested by Mount Pinatubo in 1991, Montserrat in 1995-1997, and most notably Eyjafjallajökull in 2010, which closed European airspace for six days at enormous economic cost. Dispersion modelling, satellite detection, and aircraft sensor systems have all advanced dramatically as a result of the Galunggung lessons.

## Galunggung's Wider Eruption History

Before 1982, Galunggung's most catastrophic eruption occurred in 1822, when a large sector collapse and pyroclastic flows killed an estimated 4,000 people in surrounding villages. The volcano has a long history of explosive behaviour interspersed with quieter periods. After the 1982-83 eruption, the volcano returned to a state of relative quiescence, but monitoring by the Indonesian Centre for Volcanology and Geological Hazard Mitigation (CVGHM) continues uninterrupted. Minor seismicity has been recorded at intervals, and a small warm lake has formed in the caldera since the eruption.

## Java's Volcanic Landscape

Galunggung sits within one of the most densely volcanic regions on Earth. Java alone has approximately 120 volcanoes, of which 35 are currently considered active. Among its immediate neighbours in West Java, Gunung Salak (2,211 m) has had historical eruptions, and Gunung Gede (2,958 m) forms the core of Gede Pangrango National Park — one of the most visited natural areas in Indonesia. Gunung Papandayan near Garut is a popular destination for its accessible crater fumaroles. Further east on Java, Gunung Semeru (3,676 m) is the island's highest point and one of the most consistently active volcanoes in the archipelago. Gunung Bromo in the Tengger Caldera is iconic for its sunrise views, while Gunung Ijen on Java's eastern tip holds an electric-blue acid lake and a sulfur-mining operation that continues in conditions of extreme toxicity. Arjuno-Welirang, Gunung Kelud, and Gunung Raung complete a dense ring of active volcanic centres.

## Visiting Galunggung Today

The volcano is accessible as a day trip from Tasikmalaya or Garut. A paved road leads partway up the northern flank, and a marked trail continues to the caldera rim, from which the warm inner lake is visible below. Fumarolic activity is minor but detectable, particularly during the rainy season when steam from heated groundwater can be dramatic. The surrounding landscape — deep gorges, waterfalls, and terraced rice fields — is beautiful by any standard. The site has become a modest ecotourism destination, with local guides available for the two-to-three-hour hike to the rim.
