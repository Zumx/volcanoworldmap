Rising to 3,726 metres, Mount Rinjani is the second highest volcano in Indonesia and the undisputed centrepiece of the island of Lombok. What makes Rinjani unique among Southeast Asia's great volcanoes is not merely its height but the dramatic architecture of its caldera interior: a sacred lake called Segara Anak, roughly 6 by 8 kilometres across, and within that lake a young, steaming cone called Gunung Barujari that last erupted in 2015 and 2016. The caldera rim stands at around 2,900 metres, and the view from its lip across the turquoise water to Barujari's summit, with Gunung Semeru visible on the Java horizon on clear days, is one of the most memorable in all of Indonesia.

## Geological Origins

Rinjani is a classic stratovolcano, formed at the convergence of the same Indo-Australian and Eurasian plates responsible for the explosive geology across the entire archipelago. Geologists estimate that a catastrophic caldera-forming eruption around 1257 CE — possibly the largest in the past millennium globally — created the present depression. Evidence of this event, known as the Samalas eruption, has been found in ice cores from both poles and linked to a severe global cooling event in the 13th century. The blast ejected an estimated 40 cubic kilometres of material, dwarfing the 1815 Tambora eruption in terms of climatic impact.

## Segara Anak: The Sacred Lake

Segara Anak translates loosely from Sasak as "child of the sea." The lake sits at about 2,000 metres inside the caldera and reaches depths of over 200 metres in places. For the Sasak and Balinese Hindu communities of Lombok and Bali, the lake has deep spiritual significance. Pilgrims undertake the demanding trek to its shores to make offerings and conduct rituals, particularly during the Pekelem ceremony in which offerings are cast into the waters. Hot springs at the lakeside — reachable by a steep descent from the caldera rim — provide a thermal soak after the long walk, and freshwater fish have colonised the lake despite its volcanic chemistry.

## Gunung Barujari: The Inner Cone

Gunung Barujari — sometimes called Gunung Baru — is the young parasitic cone that has grown within Segara Anak since the caldera stabilised. It stands around 2,376 metres, emerging dramatically from the lake. Its eruptions in 2009, 2015, and 2016 produced ash columns that disrupted flights across the Lombok Strait and caused temporary evacuations in villages on the caldera rim. Watching smoke drift from Barujari's vent while sitting on the caldera rim 900 metres above is an experience that telescopes geological time into a single, visceral moment.

## The Trek: Routes and Logistics

Three main routes lead to the caldera rim: the Senaru approach from the north, the Sembalun route from the east, and the Torean route from the northwest. The most popular circuit combines Senaru and Sembalun, allowing trekkers to camp on the caldera rim, descend to the lake, and exit via the wide, open Sembalun valley. A permit system managed by the Rinjani Trek Management Board limits daily trekker numbers; guides are compulsory, and porters carry the loads at fixed-rate agreements designed to support local livelihoods. The trek typically takes 2-4 days depending on whether the summit push to the true peak is attempted.

## Summit Attempt: The Final Push

The summit of Rinjani is not the caldera rim but the highest point of the volcano's original edifice, reached by continuing north and east from the main campsite at Pelawangan II (2,639 m). The summit push leaves before midnight, with climbers picking their way up a ridge of crumbling volcanic rock and loose scree, using head torches, before dawn breaks to reveal the full panorama of the caldera below and the Java volcanoes on the horizon. Conditions can change rapidly; cold, wind, and occasional rain make the summit attempt genuinely testing. The reward — watching sunrise from Indonesia's second highest volcano — is extraordinary.

## Ecology of the National Park

Mount Rinjani National Park covers 41,330 hectares of tropical forest, sub-montane scrub, and alpine grassland. The forests below 2,000 m shelter the endemic Lombok myna, black monkeys, wild boar, and the rare Lombok tree-frog. Above the treeline, deer graze the caldera slopes. The edelweiss Anaphalis longifolia covers the exposed ridges, and the descent toward Sembalun passes through meadows of wildflowers in the wet season between November and March.

## Responsible Trekking and Conservation Challenges

Rinjani's popularity has brought substantial waste and erosion challenges. The Rinjani Trek Eco Tour programme, established in the 2000s, trained local guides and introduced zero-waste protocols on the mountain. Trekkers are now required to carry all rubbish out of the park. Porters' welfare has also become a major focus: the Porter Welfare Scheme sets minimum load standards at 25 kg per porter and mandates rain gear, appropriate footwear, and fair wages. Despite these efforts, the sheer volume of visitors — tens of thousands per year — demands ongoing vigilance.
