Ukraine's volcanic story is written in deep time. Millions of years ago, a subduction zone
along the margin of the ancient Paratethys Sea drove the formation of a volcanic arc through
what are now the Eastern Carpathians. Stratovolcanoes and calderas erupted through the Miocene
and into the Pliocene, leaving behind chains of eroded volcanic edifices, caldera lakes, and
rich mineral-spring systems that still bubble to the surface today. The mud volcanoes of
Starunia — including the site that yielded one of the world's most extraordinary Ice Age
preservations — are the most dramatic surface expression of this deep heat that lingers
beneath the Ukrainian Carpathians.

## The Carpathian Volcanic Arc

The Carpathian volcanic arc stretches roughly from Slovakia through Hungary and into western
Ukraine and Romania, formed by the subduction of oceanic lithosphere beneath the Eurasian
plate during the Neogene. In Ukraine, the arc's remnants are concentrated in the Transcarpathian
lowland and the innermost ranges of the Carpathians. Volcanic activity peaked between about
12 and 6 million years ago, producing classic stratovolcanoes built from andesitic and
rhyolitic lavas. These edifices have since been heavily eroded by glacial and fluvial
processes, but their cores — solidified magma chambers and lava necks — remain as prominent
hills and ridges across the Transcarpathian region.

## Shaianska Hora and the Transcarpathian Hills

Among the most recognizable volcanic relics in Ukraine is Shaianska Hora near the town of
Khust in Zakarpattia Oblast. This isolated hill is the eroded core of an ancient Miocene
volcano, its andesitic rocks stripped of softer pyroclastic materials by millions of years
of erosion. Shaianska Hora overlooks the Tisza River valley and is surrounded by vineyards
that benefit from the mineral-rich volcanic soils — soils that give Transcarpathian wines a
distinctive mineral character. Several similar volcanic hills dot the lowlands between
Uzhhorod and Mukacheve, each a ghost of a once-active stratovolcano.

## Starunia: The Mud Volcano That Froze Megafauna

Few volcanic sites anywhere in the world can rival the biological significance of Starunia
in the Ivano-Frankivsk region of western Ukraine. The site is a cluster of mud volcanoes
seeping hydrocarbon-laden brine and wax — a product of natural petroleum seeps beneath
the Carpathian foothills. These cold, oxygen-poor, mineral-rich muds proved extraordinary
preservation environments. Excavations in the early twentieth century and again in 2007
yielded complete woolly rhinoceros specimens, their skin, hair, and internal organs
preserved by the wax-impregnated mud. One rhinoceros discovered in 1929 is now housed in
the Natural History Museum in Kraków. The mud volcano Grjazovyi Vulkan Starunia remains
active, slowly building low cones and releasing methane gas, a reminder that the Carpathian
subsurface is still geologically restless.

## Mineral Springs and Geothermal Resources

The volcanic heritage of the Ukrainian Carpathians has a practical face: some of the most
celebrated mineral springs in Central Europe emerge from this region. The Transcarpathian
lowland hosts over 300 documented mineral spring sources, ranging from carbon-dioxide-rich
sparkling waters to sulphurous thermal springs at temperatures approaching 42 degrees Celsius.
The resort town of Svalyava has built an entire spa economy on its sodium-bicarbonate springs,
while Berehove, near the Hungarian border, offers thermal pools fed by deep geothermal water
reaching 55 degrees Celsius at the borehole. These springs are direct heirs of the Miocene
volcanic system — groundwater circulating through fractured volcanic rocks and deep fault
zones picks up minerals and heat before rising to the surface.

## Ostra and the Vyhorlat Range

The Vyhorlat Range in eastern Zakarpattia represents one of the largest surviving chunks of
the Carpathian volcanic arc on Ukrainian territory. Its highest peak, Velykyi Dil at about
1,012 metres, is formed from andesitic lava flows and agglomerates dating from the Miocene.
Beneath the Vyhorlat plateau lies Sinevirske Lake — a crater lake of outstanding beauty at
989 metres elevation, its dark waters reflecting dense beech and fir forest. Geologists
debate whether the lake occupies a true volcanic caldera or a maar depression; current
consensus leans toward a maar origin, formed when rising magma encountered groundwater and
exploded rather than erupted effusively.

## The Makovytsia Tuff Plateau

In the Chernivtsi Oblast and neighbouring parts of Lviv Oblast, outcrops of volcanic tuff
mark the distal ash deposits of the Carpathian volcanic arc. These pale, soft rocks were
historically quarried as a lightweight building material and can be seen in traditional
architecture across the foothills. The Makovytsia area exposes particularly clear tuff
sequences, and the layering of ash beds of different compositions tells volcanologists
about the sequence and character of individual eruptions within the arc system.

## Caldera Lakes of the Inner Carpathians

Several lake-filled depressions in the Ukrainian Carpathians have long been proposed as
volcanic calderas. Lake Synevyr, already mentioned in the context of the Vyhorlat maar
theory, is the most celebrated. Smaller circular depressions in the Gorgany and Chornohora
ranges have also been proposed as volcanic structures, though many geomorphologists attribute
these to large-scale landslides or glacial erosion. Resolving these interpretations requires
detailed subsurface geophysical surveys that have not yet been systematically carried out.

## Andesite Quarrying and Volcanic Heritage

The volcanic rocks of Transcarpathia have been quarried for centuries as construction stone
and road aggregate. The andesites of the inner Carpathian arc are particularly valued for
their hardness and durability. Several quarries around Berehove and Vynohradiv expose
excellent cross-sections through former lava flows and volcanic breccias, inadvertently
creating geological windows that help scientists reconstruct the architecture of the ancient
arc system. Some disused quarries have been proposed as geopark sites.

## Hot Springs near Velykyi Bereznyi

In the far western corner of Zakarpattia Oblast, the small town of Velykyi Bereznyi sits
near a cluster of sulphurous springs that emerge from faulted zones within the Carpathian
flysch. While these springs are not geothermal in the strictest sense — their temperatures
hover near ambient — their high mineral loads and sulphur content reflect the chemical
imprint of deep volcanic rocks through which the groundwater travels. They represent the
outermost reach of the Carpathian volcanic geochemical system.

## Geological Conservation Efforts

Ukraine has begun to recognise the geoheritage value of its Carpathian volcanic sites.
The Synevyr National Nature Park in Zakarpattia protects Lake Synevyr and the surrounding
volcanic landscape. The Zakarpatska Mineral Waters Regional Landscape Park covers key spring
sites. Academic institutions in Uzhhorod and Lviv have published detailed geological maps
of the Carpathian arc remnants, and there is growing interest in developing a Carpathian
volcanic geopark comparable to those already established across the border in Slovakia
and Hungary.
