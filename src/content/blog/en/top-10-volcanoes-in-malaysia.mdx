Malaysia sits at the confluence of complex tectonic forces. The Sundaland block — the shallow continental shelf underlying the South China Sea, Malay Peninsula and the western half of Borneo — was assembled from volcanic island arcs and continental fragments over hundreds of millions of years. Today the Malaysian mainland and Sabah experience little seismicity, but Sabah's Semporna Peninsula hosts a remarkable concentration of geologically young volcanic features including domes, cones and the country's most significant active mud volcano fields. Understanding Malaysian volcanism means tracing the ancient arcs that built the land and the modern hydrothermal systems that still exhale through it.

## Sundaland Tectonics and the Borneo Arc

Borneo's geological history is dominated by the collision and accretion of island arc terranes with the stable Sundaland margin. Between 100 and 40 million years ago, the Rajang accretionary prism — now the Crocker Range spine of Sabah — formed when oceanic sediments were scraped off the descending oceanic crust and plastered onto the continental margin. Volcanic arc magmatism accompanied this process, producing the granite and volcanic rocks now exposed in Sabah's interior highlands. The Mulu karst and Kinabalu pluton are both remnants of this arc-related igneous activity, though Kinabalu's granite is younger, emplaced about 7 to 10 million years ago.

## Bukit Bombalai: Malaysia's Most Recently Active Volcano

Bukit Bombalai, located on the Semporna Peninsula in southeastern Sabah, is considered the most recent volcanic centre in Malaysia. The hill and its surrounding lava field consist of alkali basalt lavas dated to less than 1 million years — geologically very young for a region considered tectonically stable. The basalts form a broad, low shield rising to about 531 m above sea level. No historical eruption has been recorded, but the geological youth of the lavas, combined with active mud volcanoes and geothermal springs in the same region, suggests the area retains residual geothermal heat from the underlying magmatic system.

## Mud Volcanoes of Sabah

Sabah hosts Malaysia's most diverse mud volcano fields. The Bekenu Mud Volcano, Lipad Mud Volcano and Bakong Mud Volcano are among the documented features in the northwestern Sabah zone, where methane and CO₂ escape through water-saturated sediments under low pressure. These mud volcanoes are not fed by magmatic heat but by the compaction of thick organic-rich sedimentary sequences deposited in the Neogene basins offshore. Gas seeps in these areas have been associated with commercial natural gas accumulations. The mud volcano surfaces are typically small — a few metres across — and erupt irregular pulses of grey mud and gas bubbles.

## Mud Volcanoes of Baratang and the Andaman Connection

Though Baratang Island's mud volcanoes are technically in India's Andaman Islands, the tectonic setting that drives them — the Andaman-Nicobar subduction system — has geological cousins in Malaysia's Sabah region. The convergence of the Australian and Asian plates has produced a similar style of sediment-sourced gas expulsion across a wide arc from the Andaman Sea through the Banda Sea. Malaysian mud volcanoes are thus part of a broader Southeast Asian mud volcano province driven by regional plate convergence and rapid sediment accumulation in back-arc basins.

## Semporna Archipelago: Volcanic Islands in the Celebes Sea

The Semporna Archipelago, off the southeastern tip of Sabah, comprises dozens of small islands whose geology is predominantly volcanic. Bohey Dulang, Pulau Mantabuan, Pulau Sebangkat and Pulau Maiga are all built on Miocene volcanic rocks — lavas, pyroclastics and volcanic breccias that erupted from the Semporna volcanic arc between 10 and 20 million years ago. These islands are now famous for diving, with coral reefs growing on the submerged flanks of the old volcanic edifices. The Tun Sakaran Marine Park encompasses many of these volcanic islands.

## Mount Kinabalu's Granitic Core

Mount Kinabalu (4,095 m) is Malaysia's highest peak and one of the highest in Southeast Asia. Though not a volcano in the traditional sense, Kinabalu is a massive granite pluton that intruded through older rocks between 7 and 10 million years ago. The granite is still rising due to isostatic rebound — the summit is estimated to rise about 5 mm per year. The mountain's rapid uplift and exposure of fresh rock makes it botanically extraordinary, hosting over 5,000 plant species including carnivorous pitcher plants and orchids found nowhere else. The summit plateau, scraped clean by Pleistocene glaciers, exposes bare grey granite.

## Ancient Volcanic Rocks of Peninsular Malaysia

The Malay Peninsula contains extensive bodies of igneous rock emplaced during Paleozoic and Mesozoic volcanic arc events. The Main Range granite that forms the backbone of the Peninsula from the Thai border to Johor is the product of Triassic arc magmatism, representing the roots of a former volcanic chain. Tin mineralisation associated with this granite made Peninsular Malaysia one of the world's major tin producers through the 19th and 20th centuries. Numerous small volcanic hills in the interior are erosional remnants of this ancient arc.

## Geothermal Springs and Hot Seeps

Despite the absence of recent volcanism on the Malay Peninsula, numerous hot springs occur throughout the country. The Sungai Klah Hot Springs near Sungkai in Perak reach temperatures of up to 120 °C at the borehole, with surface pools maintained at 40–55 °C. The Bentong hot springs in Pahang, the Ulu Slim springs in Perak and the Air Panas Gabai springs near Kuala Lumpur all reflect residual geothermal heat in the granite terrain. These springs are popular recreational destinations and some have been developed into spa resorts.

## Volcanic Hazard Outlook

Malaysia has no active volcanoes on its territory in the strictest sense, but the proximity of active volcanic zones in the Philippines, Indonesia and Vanuatu means that volcanic ash fall and tsunami from remote eruptions represent real risks. The 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami, triggered by the Sumatra-Andaman earthquake, affected Malaysia's northwestern coast significantly. Volcanic ash from major eruptions in Indonesia has affected air quality and aviation in peninsular Malaysia on several occasions.
