On 22 May 1915, Lassen Peak in northern California produced a lateral blast of superheated ash and volcanic gases that swept 30 kilometres northeast, scorching a path through dense forest and killing every tree in its way. The devastated zone — still called the Devastated Area — remained largely barren for decades. That eruption was the climax of a sequence that had begun in May 1914 and would continue intermittently until 1917, making Lassen's event the only significant volcanic eruption on the continental United States between the 19th century and Mount St Helens' catastrophic explosion in 1980. The 65 years of comparative quiet that followed embedded the 1915 blast deep into the culture of American volcanology, ultimately helping make the case for establishing a national volcanic monitoring programme.

## What Kind of Volcano Is Lassen Peak?

Lassen Peak is classified as a plug dome volcano — one of the world's largest examples of its type. Unlike the classic cone-shaped stratovolcanoes (Mount Shasta, Mount Rainier), plug domes grow by the slow, thick extrusion of viscous dacitic lava that piles up directly above the vent rather than flowing far from it. Lassen's dome is approximately 2.4 kilometres wide at the base and 610 metres tall, rising to 3,187 metres above sea level. It was extruded beginning about 27,000 years ago from the remains of an older stratovolcanic edifice called Mount Tehama, whose ancient flanks still form the ridges visible around Brokeoff Mountain and Mount Conard to the south and west.

## The 1914–1917 Eruptive Sequence

Lassen's modern eruptive period began with steam explosions in May 1914 that opened a new crater at the summit. Over the following year, roughly 150 individual steam and ash eruptions occurred, enlarging the summit crater from a few metres across to roughly 300 metres in diameter. By spring 1915 a plug of hot dacite lava had risen to fill the crater, glowing red by night. On 19 May a lava overflow sent sluggish streams down both the northwest and northeast flanks, melting the snowpack and triggering mud and debris flows that swept into the valleys below. Three days later the main event struck: the lateral blast excavated a new notch in the summit and buried the valley of Lost Creek under metres of hot pumice and ash, a deposit geologists call the Hot Rock area.

## Hydrothermal Wonderland

The scientific legacy of the 1915 eruption extends far beyond volcanology: Lassen Volcanic National Park, established in 1916 partly in response to the eruptions, protects one of the most diverse hydrothermal landscapes in the world. Bumpass Hell, reached via a 4.8-kilometre return trail from the main park highway, is the largest hydrothermal area in the park — a 6-hectare basin of boiling mud pots, fumaroles, and brilliantly coloured hot springs. Water temperatures at Bumpass Hell reach 95°C, and the pools are tinted yellow, orange, and white by sulfur, iron compounds, and silica. Sulphur Works, accessible directly from the road, sits within the old caldera of Mount Tehama and is one of the most accessible hydrothermal sites in the entire Cascade Range.

## Devil's Kitchen and the Thermal Belt

Beyond Bumpass Hell, the park contains a series of quieter but equally remarkable thermal features. Devil's Kitchen, reached after a 6-kilometre hike, presents fumaroles and hot spring pools in an eerie, largely steam-filled valley. The Boiling Springs Lake, just 1.6 kilometres from the Warner Valley trailhead, is a broad turquoise lake maintained at roughly 52°C by underlying geothermal heat. Terminal Geyser — despite its name, not a true geyser but a vigorous steam vent — makes its presence known through a constant column of steam rising above the forest canopy. Together these features trace a thermal belt across the park that reflects a magma body still present at shallow depth.

## Scientific Impact and Monitoring

The 1915 eruption caught scientists largely unprepared. Photographs by B.F. Loomis, a local rancher who systematically documented the eruption sequence, became among the first detailed visual records of a major volcanic event in North America. His images of the lateral blast, the lava overflow, and the Devastated Area proved invaluable when volcanologists interpreted the Mount St Helens eruption in 1980 — similar mechanisms, similar deposits, six decades apart. USGS now maintains seismometer networks and ground-deformation monitors at Lassen as part of the Cascade Volcano Observatory, which uses the park's well-documented 1915 event as a calibration baseline.

## Hiking in a Volcanic Landscape

The summit trail to Lassen Peak begins at a trailhead on the main park road at 2,475 metres and gains roughly 610 metres over 4 kilometres of rocky switchbacks. The route passes through alternating fields of pale dacite and dark andesite, crosses steam vents in the upper crater zone, and reaches the summit with broad views of Shasta to the north and the Sierra Nevada to the south. The trail is typically snow-free from late July to October. Beyond the summit trail, the park's 280-kilometre network includes routes to Cinder Cone, a perfectly symmetrical scoria cone near Butte Lake that last erupted in the 1650s, and the Painted Dunes — multicoloured ash beds stained by oxidised iron minerals.

## Wildlife and Wilderness

Lassen's 430 square kilometres of protected wilderness support populations of black bears, mule deer, pronghorn on the eastern plateau, and a remarkable diversity of birds including Clark's nutcracker, which is essential for dispersing whitebark pine seeds across the subalpine zone. The park's lakes — Manzanita, Reflection, Emerald, Summit — are fed by snowmelt and remain cold enough to support populations of brook and rainbow trout throughout summer.

## Planning Your Visit

The park's main scenic road, California Highway 89, crosses the park from southwest to northeast and is typically open from June to October, depending on snowpack. The nearest towns are Redding to the southwest and Chester to the southeast, both within 75 kilometres. The park headquarters at Kohm Yah-mah-nee Visitor Center, in the southwest corner, provides maps, current road conditions, and interpretive displays on the 1915 eruption geology. Camping is available at several campgrounds including Manzanita Lake, the largest, near the northwest entrance.
