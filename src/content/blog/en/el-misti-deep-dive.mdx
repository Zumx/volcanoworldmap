El Misti is the iconic volcano of southern Peru, a strikingly symmetrical cone that
rises directly above Arequipa, the country's second-largest city. Its graceful form
is woven into the identity of the city below, appearing on its skyline and in its
art. But El Misti is also one of the most potentially dangerous volcanoes in Peru,
because so many people live within reach of its eruptions, making it a focus of
serious hazard planning.

## A cone above a city

El Misti reaches about 5,822 metres and stands just a short distance from the heart
of Arequipa, a city of roughly a million people. Its near-perfect conical shape,
often capped with snow, dominates the urban skyline and has made it a beloved symbol
of the region. Few major cities sit so close to such a large and active volcano.

## A typical Andean stratovolcano

El Misti is a classic Andean stratovolcano, built by the subduction of the Nazca
Plate beneath South America. Its steep cone is composed of alternating layers of
lava and explosive deposits, and it has multiple nested craters at its summit. It
forms part of a volcanic group with the neighbouring peaks of Chachani and
Pichu Pichu, which together frame the city of Arequipa.

## A history of eruptions

El Misti has erupted numerous times over the past several thousand years, including
significant explosive events. One notable eruption around the fifteenth century is
thought to have affected the region and is recorded in local history and deposits.
While the volcano has been relatively quiet in recent centuries, showing mainly
fumarolic activity, its geological record makes clear its capacity for substantial
eruptions.

## The threat to Arequipa

The proximity of El Misti to Arequipa makes it one of the highest-risk volcanoes in
Peru. A major eruption could shower the city with ash, generate pyroclastic flows,
and produce lahars that travel down the valleys into populated areas. The
combination of a large, capable volcano and a major city at its foot is precisely
the kind of situation that volcanic hazard planning seeks to address.

## Lahars and the valleys

Among the most serious hazards from El Misti are lahars. Volcanic debris on its
slopes, mobilised by heavy rain or by melting of summit ice during an eruption, can
flow down the river valleys that pass through Arequipa. Mapping these valleys and
planning for lahar evacuation is a central part of managing the risk the volcano
poses to the city.

## Monitoring and preparedness

Peruvian volcanologists monitor El Misti closely, tracking its seismicity, gas
emissions, and ground deformation. Given the enormous population at risk,
authorities have invested in hazard maps, monitoring networks, and public education,
working to ensure that the city is prepared should the volcano show signs of
reawakening. This preparedness is essential for a volcano of such potential
consequence.

## A cultural and natural landmark

Beyond its hazards, El Misti is a cherished part of Arequipa's identity and a draw
for climbers and visitors. Its slopes can be ascended on a strenuous high-altitude
trek, and archaeological finds near its summit attest to its significance to
pre-Columbian peoples. The volcano is thus woven into the cultural, natural, and
everyday life of southern Peru.
