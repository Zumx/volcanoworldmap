Driving east from the Sierra Nevada crest on US 395, travellers descend into a broad, flat-floored valley flanked by high fault scarps and scattered with steaming vents and alkaline lakes. This is Long Valley Caldera, a 32-by-17-kilometre oval depression in east-central California, and it is the product of one of the largest volcanic eruptions in North America's recorded geological history. About 760,000 years ago, a cataclysmic eruption expelled roughly 600 cubic kilometres of rhyolitic magma in what volcanologists call the Bishop Tuff eruption — named for the fine-grained ash deposit that blanketed much of the western and central United States, as far east as Kansas and Nebraska. The collapse of the magma chamber roof created the caldera; within it, the modern town of Mammoth Lakes sits atop a system that remains restless, monitored, and potentially dangerous.

## The Bishop Tuff Supereruption

The Bishop Tuff eruption 760,000 years ago was roughly 2,000 times larger than the Mount St Helens 1980 event. Pyroclastic flows raced across the landscape at hundreds of kilometres per hour, incinerating everything within hundreds of kilometres. Ash fallout several centimetres thick has been identified as far away as Nebraska. The eruption excavated a caldera 3 km deep; subsequent resurgence has pushed the central dome — now known as the Resurgent Dome — upward by about 600 metres as fresh magma reinflated the chamber. The Bishop Tuff deposit itself is visible in road cuts along US 395 near Bishop and in the walls of the Owens River Gorge — pale, welded tuff that records in stone the violence of a geologically recent catastrophe.

## Post-Caldera Volcanism: The Mono-Inyo Craters

Long Valley's volcanic story did not end 760,000 years ago. A series of younger eruptions has produced the Mono-Inyo Craters chain, a 45-kilometre line of rhyolite domes and craters stretching from the caldera's northwest margin to Mono Lake. The youngest Inyo Craters erupted as recently as 550 years ago, producing explosions that scattered pumice across the caldera floor and left three craters visible today as water-filled lakes near Mammoth Mountain. Mono Lake itself — the shallow alkaline lake famous for its tufa towers — sits at the northern end of this chain, with Paoha and Negit islands formed by eruptions within the last 350 years.

## Modern Unrest Since 1980

In May 1980, four magnitude-6 earthquakes struck Long Valley in a single day — a seismic unrest that alarmed geologists and prompted intensive monitoring. Since then the caldera floor has risen more than 80 centimetres as magma pushes upward into the resurgent dome. Earthquake swarms — clusters of hundreds of small quakes — recur regularly beneath the south moat of the caldera and beneath Mammoth Mountain. From 1989 to 2001 volcanic CO2 emissions through the soil on Mammoth Mountain's flanks killed approximately 170 acres of trees near Horseshoe Lake, a striking visible indicator of subsurface volcanic gas activity. The USGS California Volcano Observatory in Menlo Park maintains real-time monitoring of seismicity, ground deformation, and gas emissions across the Long Valley system.

## Mammoth Mountain: A Volcanic Ski Resort

The ski resort of Mammoth Mountain (3,369 m) sits directly on the western rim of Long Valley Caldera, built on the flanks of a dacite dome complex. It is one of the few ski areas in the world operating on an active volcanic system. Mammoth Mountain last erupted approximately 57,000 years ago, but the ongoing CO2 emissions from its flanks place it firmly in the category of active volcanic features. For visitors, the combination of 3,500 acres of ski terrain and views directly into a supervolcano caldera makes Mammoth a uniquely strange destination.

## Hot Creek Geological Site

Within the caldera, Hot Creek runs through a gorge where geothermal water boils up through the streambed at temperatures exceeding 100 °C in some vents. The Hot Creek Geological Site, about 10 kilometres east of Mammoth Lakes on Hot Creek Hatchery Road, allows visitors to view the roiling, milky-blue water and yellow-encrusted vent walls from a safe overlook. Swimming in Hot Creek was once permitted but is now banned after sudden temperature spikes and hydrothermal explosions. The site is managed by the Inyo National Forest and is open year-round.

## The Owens Valley Fault System

Long Valley's volcanic hazard is compounded by the Eastern Sierra fault system, one of the most seismically active in California. The 1872 Owens Valley earthquake, estimated at magnitude 7.6, ruptured the fault along the valley floor and remains the third-largest historical earthquake in California history. The combination of fault-driven seismicity and volcanic unrest makes the Long Valley region one of the most complex geohazard environments in North America.

## Visiting Mammoth Lakes and the Caldera

The town of Mammoth Lakes, with a year-round population of about 8,000, is the main base for caldera visitors and lies entirely within the caldera. The USGS and Mammoth Lakes Tourism jointly publish "Living with a Restless Caldera" pamphlets available at the visitor centre on Main Street. The Mammoth Lakes Welcome Center also operates a short geological trail through the town's volcanic features. Bodie State Historic Park, an abandoned gold-rush town about 75 kilometres north, lies just outside the Mono-Inyo volcanic chain and pairs well with caldera exploration.
