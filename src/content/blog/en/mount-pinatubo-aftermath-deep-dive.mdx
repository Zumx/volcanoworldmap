On 15 June 1991, Mount Pinatubo on the island of Luzon in the Philippines produced the second-largest volcanic eruption of the twentieth century. The eruption column reached 34 km — breaching the stratosphere — and ejected roughly 10 km³ of material, including more than 17 million tonnes of sulphur dioxide. The eruption killed around 800 people despite a remarkably successful evacuation of over 60,000 individuals. But the immediate eruption was only the beginning of a decades-long geological and human drama that continues to reshape the landscape north of Manila to this day.

## The Eruption: Sequence and Scale

Pinatubo had been dormant for approximately 600 years before unrest began in March 1991 with a series of small steam explosions on the volcano's upper slopes. By June, volcanologists from the Philippine Institute of Volcanology and Seismology (PHIVOLCS) and the United States Geological Survey had set up a network of seismic stations and issued progressively larger evacuation zones. This decision to act decisively — combined with public education campaigns and the distribution of Clark Air Base's resources — is credited with saving tens of thousands of lives. The climactic eruption on June 15 coincided with Typhoon Yunya passing directly overhead, mixing heavy rain with falling ash to produce a devastating combination that collapsed roofs across a wide area and killed most of the eventual 800 fatalities.

## Pyroclastic Deposits and the Lahar Threat

The 1991 eruption deposited between 5 and 6 km³ of pyroclastic material — a mixture of volcanic ash, pumice, and rock fragments — in the valleys radiating from Pinatubo's summit. This material, known as lahar or volcanic debris flow when saturated with water, became one of the most persistent hazards in Philippine history. Every wet season for the next decade brought renewed lahar flows as monsoon rains remobilized the unconsolidated deposits. The Pasig-Potrero, Bucao, and Sacobia rivers carried lahar flows that buried entire barangays (villages), damaged bridges, and deposited thick layers of grey volcanic sand across rice fields in Pampanga and Zambales provinces. Estimates suggest that lahars destroyed or damaged more than 100,000 homes and buried more than 300 km² of agricultural land over the period 1991–2001.

## Engineering Responses: Sabo Dams and Channel Works

The Philippine government, supported by Japanese development assistance, constructed an extensive network of sabo dams — engineered check dams specifically designed to trap and slow lahar flows. Dozens of these reinforced concrete structures were built in the river valleys around Pinatubo between 1992 and 2000. They were not universally successful: several were destroyed by particularly large flows, and the sheer volume of material meant that even the largest sabo systems were eventually filled. The dams did, however, trap significant quantities of lahar material close to the mountain, reducing the distance and severity of downstream impacts. By around 2001, when most of the easily remobilized pyroclastic material had been flushed from the upper valleys, lahar frequency began to decline significantly.

## Crater Lake Formation

One of the more remarkable geological outcomes of the 1991 eruption was the formation of a new caldera and, within it, an acid crater lake now called Lake Pinatubo. In the immediate aftermath of the eruption, the collapsed summit — which had lost about 260 m in elevation — began to fill with water trapped by the new caldera rim. By 1992 a small lake had formed; by 2000 it had grown to roughly 2 km in diameter. The lake's pH was initially very acidic — around 2 on the scale — due to dissolved volcanic gases, but has gradually risen toward neutral as the hydrothermal activity beneath the lake has declined. Today Lake Pinatubo is a striking turquoise-blue, framed by rugged caldera walls, and has become one of the more unusual volcano tourism destinations in Southeast Asia, accessible by a combination of 4WD vehicle and trekking.

## Ecosystem Recovery: Forest Succession on Lava and Ash

The recovery of vegetation on Pinatubo's devastated slopes has been slower than on many other post-eruption landscapes because the thick pyroclastic deposits are nutrient-poor and prone to erosion. Pioneer species that established earliest included various ferns, sedges, and grasses — plants capable of tolerating the harsh, unstable substrate. By the early 2000s, secondary shrubland had colonised significant areas of the lower slopes. By the 2010s, fast-growing tree species including several Macaranga and Ficus species had formed closed canopy in favoured gullies and sheltered valley flanks. However, large areas of the upper volcano remain sparsely vegetated more than 30 years after the eruption, particularly on the northern and eastern slopes where the deepest deposits accumulated.

## The Aeta People: Displacement and Resilience

The Aeta (also spelled Agta), the indigenous Negrito people who had lived on Pinatubo's forested slopes for thousands of years, suffered the most profound displacement from the eruption. Before 1991, an estimated 20,000 to 30,000 Aeta lived in the upland forest communities on the volcano's flanks, surviving through a combination of hunting, gathering, and small-scale cultivation. They were evacuated to lowland camps before the eruption, but the destruction of their forest homeland — and the subsequent lahar flows that continued to threaten the slopes — prevented most from returning for years. Many Aeta communities were relocated to government resettlement sites in Pampanga, where they faced profound cultural dislocation: lowland environments offered few of the traditional resources on which Aeta skills and knowledge were based.

## Aeta Cultural Survival and Land Rights

Over the following decades, some Aeta communities managed to return to the mountains, re-establishing smaller communities in areas where the pyroclastic deposits had thinned and vegetation was recovering. Indigenous land rights — recognized partially under the Philippine Indigenous Peoples Rights Act of 1997 — have provided legal frameworks for some communities to pursue ancestral domain claims over portions of the mountain that were previously forest territory. Community-based tourism initiatives, particularly guiding trekkers to Lake Pinatubo, have provided economic alternatives that allow younger Aeta to remain connected to the mountain. These guides carry knowledge of pre-eruption trails, fauna, and cultural sites that exist in no written record.

## Climatic Effects of the Eruption

The sulphur dioxide released by Pinatubo's 1991 eruption converted to sulphate aerosols in the stratosphere, forming a global veil that reflected a fraction of incoming solar radiation back into space. Global average surface temperatures dropped by approximately 0.5 °C in 1992 and 1993 — the largest short-term climate signal attributable to a volcanic eruption in the twentieth century. The cooling was sufficient to temporarily offset some of the greenhouse-gas-driven warming trend and provided scientists with a natural experiment to test climate models. The ozone layer was also affected: the aerosol particles catalysed chemical reactions that accelerated ozone destruction, particularly at mid-latitudes, temporarily widening the ozone hole over Antarctica.

## Tourism and Economic Recovery

The area around Pinatubo today supports a growing volcano tourism economy, anchored by the Lake Pinatubo trek, which typically begins from barangay Sta. Juliana in Capas, Tarlac. The 4WD ride through the lahar channels — crossing rivers of grey volcanic sand — is itself an attraction, and the three-hour hike through recovering vegetation to the crater lake rim has become one of the Philippines' more popular adventure activities. The nearby Pampanga and Tarlac provinces have built visitor facilities, and local governments carefully manage daily visitor numbers to limit erosion on the still-fragile trail. Clark Freeport Zone, the former US Air Force base evacuated during the 1991 eruption, has transformed into a major economic hub with business parks, aviation schools, and a growing medical tourism industry — a dramatic reuse of land that was covered in ash just three decades ago.

## Ongoing Monitoring and Residual Hazard

Pinatubo is not considered currently active in a volcanic sense — no significant unrest has occurred since the early 1990s — but PHIVOLCS maintains permanent monitoring stations on the volcano. The primary ongoing hazard is lahars during heavy rainfall events, particularly on the Pasig-Potrero and Bucao river systems where the lahar channels remain relatively unconsolidated. Slope instability on the deeply eroded pyroclastic deposits continues to generate secondary mass movements. Scientists at PHIVOLCS also study Pinatubo as a long-term case study in post-eruption lahar dynamics, and their work has informed hazard management strategies for post-eruption recovery at other volcanoes worldwide.
