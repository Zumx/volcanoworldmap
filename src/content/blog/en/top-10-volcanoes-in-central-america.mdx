Stand at the rim of Masaya Caldera in Nicaragua on a still night and watch lava churn 200 m below — a view available to anyone who drives to the car park. That accessibility is the defining character of Central American volcanism. The volcanic front follows the subduction of the Cocos Plate beneath the Caribbean Plate across Guatemala, El Salvador, Honduras, Nicaragua, Costa Rica and Panama, concentrating some of the planet's most frequently active and most visitor-ready volcanoes into a corridor 1,500 km long.

## Santa María and Santiaguito, Guatemala

Santa María's 1902 eruption ejected roughly 8 km³ of tephra in one of the twentieth century's biggest Plinian blasts. The eruption column reached 28 km. A new lava dome, Santiaguito, began forming in 1922 on the scar left by that eruption and has been erupting continuously ever since, making it one of the world's longest ongoing eruptions. Observers at the Santa María summit (3,772 m) watch small explosions from Santiaguito happening at intervals of 30–90 minutes, puffing ash clouds above the Pacific coastal plain.

## Tajumulco, Guatemala

At 4,220 m, Tajumulco is the highest volcano in Central America and the highest peak in all of Mesoamerica. It is a dormant stratovolcano with no recorded historical eruptions, but its prominence draws mountaineers from across the region. The two-day ascent from San Marcos begins in pine-oak forest, transitions to open páramo and reaches a broad summit offering views of the Guatemalan highlands, Chiapas and the Pacific on clear days.

## Pacaya, Guatemala

Pacaya is one of Guatemala's most active volcanoes and the most visited in the country, reachable in under two hours from Antigua. Its MacKenney cone has been erupting almost continuously since 1965, producing lava flows that periodically destroy hiking trails and force route changes. Hardened lava fields around the base make for dramatic walking terrain even when the summit is quiescent. A 2010 eruption killed two people and rained ash on Guatemala City.

## Izalco, El Salvador

Izalco earned the nickname "Lighthouse of the Pacific" from 19th-century sailors who used its near-continuous eruptions as a navigational beacon. It formed as recently as 1770 and erupted more than 50 times before going quiet in 1966. Its steep symmetrical black cone, rising 1,950 m beside the older Cerro Verde, remains one of the most striking volcanic profiles in the region. Trails from Parque Nacional Cerro Verde descend to the base.

## Santa Ana (Ilamatepec), El Salvador

El Salvador's highest volcano at 2,381 m, Santa Ana has a summit caldera containing a vivid acid crater lake with pH values near 1. The lake turns turquoise-yellow and shifts colour seasonally. A 2005 eruption ejected rocks and ash, killing two hikers and temporarily closing the park. The standard hike from the ranger station climbs through coffee plantation and cloud forest to the summit, a steady four-hour return trip.

## Masaya, Nicaragua

Masaya is Central America's most accessible active volcano — the Santiago crater vent is reached by a paved road through a national park. In recent years a persistent lava lake has occupied the vent floor, making Masaya one of only about ten volcanoes in the world with a viewable lava lake. Sulphur dioxide emissions are intense; visitors are limited to 20 minutes at the crater edge. Bat colonies numbering hundreds of thousands roost in the adjacent Nindirí crater.

## Concepción, Nicaragua

Rising 1,700 m above the western shore of Lake Nicaragua on the island of Ometepe, Concepción is one of the most beautiful and most active volcanoes in Central America. Its steep symmetrical cone has erupted repeatedly since Spanish colonial records began. The ascent from Moyogalpa takes six to eight hours through dense cloud forest that gives way to bare lava above 1,200 m. The view across Lake Nicaragua to the Costa Rican border is outstanding on clear days.

## Arenal, Costa Rica

Arenal slept for 400 years before a catastrophic flank eruption on 29 July 1968 destroyed three villages and killed 87 people. It then erupted almost continuously for four decades, making it Costa Rica's most watched volcano through the 1990s. Activity decreased sharply around 2010 and Arenal is now in a resting phase, though seismic monitoring remains intensive. At 1,670 m it dominates the western shore of Lake Arenal, and the national park around it receives over 400,000 visitors a year.

## Poás, Costa Rica

Poás holds one of the world's largest active volcanic craters — roughly 1.5 km across and 300 m deep — with an ultra-acidic crater lake, Laguna Caliente, whose pH has been measured as low as minus 1. Mist and rain can obscure the view, but morning visits often reward clear conditions. Frequent phreatic eruptions have closed the park repeatedly; a 2017 eruption ejected blocks and mud to the car park rim. The 2,708 m summit is reached by road.

## Barú, Panama

Barú is the southernmost and highest (3,475 m) peak in Central America's volcanic chain and Panama's only active volcano, though it has not erupted since around 1550. Its crater rim offers the remarkable distinction of being one of few points on Earth from which both the Pacific and Atlantic oceans are visible simultaneously on a clear day. Trails from Boquete and Volcán ascend through montane forest rich in resplendent quetzals and rare high-altitude plant communities.
