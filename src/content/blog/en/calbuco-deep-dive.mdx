In April 2015, after more than four decades of quiet, the Chilean volcano Calbuco
erupted with almost no warning, sending colossal columns of ash soaring into the sky
above the Lake District. The dramatic eruption, captured in spectacular photographs
and video, became one of the most striking volcanic images of the decade. Calbuco is
one of the most dangerous volcanoes in southern Chile, and its sudden 2015 awakening
underscored how quickly a long-quiet volcano can spring to life.

## A rugged Lake District volcano

Calbuco reaches about 2,003 metres and rises in Chile's southern Lake District, near
the city of Puerto Montt and the lakeside town of Puerto Varas. Unlike the elegant,
symmetrical cone of its neighbour Osorno, Calbuco is a more rugged, compact
edifice. It is considered one of the most hazardous volcanoes in the southern
Andes, owing to its explosive history and proximity to populated areas.

## A history of explosive eruptions

Calbuco has erupted numerous times over the past few centuries, often explosively.
Its eruptions have produced ash, pyroclastic flows, and lahars, making it a serious
hazard to the surrounding region. Before 2015, its most recent significant activity
had been in the early 1970s, after which it entered a long period of quiet that
lulled many into thinking of it as dormant.

## The sudden 2015 eruption

On 22 April 2015, Calbuco erupted suddenly, with only a few hours of detectable
precursors. It blasted an enormous column of ash and gas more than ten kilometres
into the sky, followed by a second powerful explosion. The eruption forced the
evacuation of thousands of people, disrupted air travel across the region, and
blanketed towns in ash. The images of the towering, lightning-laced eruption column
circulated around the world.

## The challenge of short warning

One of the most striking aspects of the 2015 eruption was how little warning it
gave. Calbuco escalated from quiet to a major explosive eruption in a very short
time, posing a serious challenge for monitoring and evacuation. The event became a
case study in the difficulty of forecasting eruptions at volcanoes that have been
quiet for decades, even with modern monitoring.

## Ashfall and its impacts

The 2015 eruption spread ash across a wide area, affecting communities in Chile and
neighbouring Argentina. Ashfall disrupted agriculture, water supplies, and daily
life, and the cleanup took considerable effort. The eruption demonstrated the
far-reaching impact that even a relatively short-lived explosive event can have on a
populated region.

## Lahars and lingering hazards

Beyond the immediate eruption, Calbuco's deposits posed continuing hazards. Heavy
rain on fresh ash and debris generated lahars in the following weeks and months,
threatening valleys and infrastructure. This secondary hazard is a common feature of
explosive eruptions and a key concern in the aftermath, requiring ongoing vigilance
even after the main eruption subsides.

## Monitoring Calbuco today

Following the 2015 eruption, monitoring of Calbuco was reinforced as part of Chile's
broader volcano surveillance. Seismic networks, deformation sensors, and gas
measurements track its behaviour, with the goal of detecting any future unrest as
early as possible. The 2015 event remains a powerful reminder of the importance of
watching even seemingly quiet volcanoes.
