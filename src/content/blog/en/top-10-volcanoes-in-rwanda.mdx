Stand on the red-clay hills west of Ruhengeri and the Virunga volcanoes announce themselves with theatrical authority. Five cone-shaped peaks march along the horizon, their summits frequently hidden in cloud, their flanks dropping through Hagenia woodland into the cultivated terraces of Rwanda's most densely populated region. These volcanoes are the stage for one of conservation's greatest stories — the recovery of the mountain gorilla — and they remain geologically active, with lava erupting from the Congolese side of the chain as recently as 2021. Rwanda's piece of the Virunga chain holds five of the eight volcanoes: Karisimbi, Bisoke, Sabinyo, Gahinga, and Muhabura.

## The Virunga Volcanic Chain

The Virunga chain extends about 80 km along the borders of Rwanda, Uganda, and the Democratic Republic of Congo, straddling the western branch of the East African Rift System. The rift is pulling apart the African continent along a north–south axis, creating the conditions for both the great lakes of the western rift — Lake Kivu immediately south of the Virungaes — and the unusual potassic volcanism that characterises the chain. The Virunga lavas are rich in potassium and nepheline, reflecting the deep mantle source of the magmas that feed them, and the chain includes the world's largest lava lake: Nyiragongo in the DRC, approximately 20 km west of the Rwandan border.

## Mount Karisimbi

Karisimbi is the highest of the Virunga volcanoes at 4,507 m and the highest point of Rwanda (though legally the summit sits on the DRC border). Its name derives from the Kinyarwanda word for "white shell," reflecting the small ice cap and frequent hailstorms that cap the summit. Karisimbi is a complex stratovolcano with an elongated ridge rather than a single perfect cone; its most recent eruptions deposited lava flows that are still relatively unweathered, though the exact date of the last eruption is debated in geological literature, with estimates ranging from 8,000 to 200,000 years ago depending on the flow. The standard two-day ascent departs from the Bisate trailhead inside Volcanoes National Park; climbers camp at approximately 3,700 m and summit in the early morning for the best visibility before clouds form.

## Mount Bisoke

Bisoke (sometimes Visoke) rises to 3,711 m and straddles the Rwanda–DRC border. Its summit is occupied by a perfectly circular crater lake about 300 m in diameter, filled with pale blue-green water that shifts colour with the weather. Bisoke is one of the most rewarding day hikes in Rwanda: the trail from Kinigi gains about 1,100 m in elevation, passing through dense bamboo forest, then Hagenia-Hypericum woodland, before emerging onto the open caldera rim above 3,500 m. The last eruption of Bisoke occurred around 1957, making it the most recently active of Rwanda's five Virunga volcanoes and the only one with confirmed 20th-century activity.

## Mount Sabinyo

Mount Sabinyo at 3,645 m is the oldest and most eroded of the Virunga volcanoes. Its summit ridge forms a dramatic saw-toothed outline of lava pinnacles from which the name is thought to derive — from a Kinyarwanda expression related to "old man's teeth." Unusually, the summit of Sabinyo sits at the junction of three countries: Rwanda, Uganda, and DRC, and it is theoretically possible to stand in all three at once from the highest pinnacle. The hike from Mgahinga in Uganda or from Kinigi in Rwanda is strenuous and involves some scrambling on fixed ropes near the top; it is typically completed in six to eight hours.

## Mount Muhabura

Muhabura, at 4,127 m, is Rwanda's second highest Virunga volcano and the most prominent peak on the Ugandan side of the chain. Its name means "the guide" in Kinyarwanda, reflecting the mountain's visibility as a landmark from great distances across the rift valley. The summit hosts a small crater lake and offers panoramic views across the DRC, Uganda, and Rwanda on clear days. Dian Fossey described Muhabura as one of the most beautiful mountains in the Virungaes in her diaries at the Karisoke Research Centre, which she founded in 1967 in the saddle between Karisimbi and Bisoke at about 3,000 m.

## Mount Gahinga

Gahinga, at 3,474 m, is the smallest and lowest of Rwanda's Virunga volcanoes and is almost always climbed as part of a traverse with Sabinyo from the Ugandan side. The name means "small heap of stones" or "pile of rubble" in local languages, perhaps referring to the scoria cone's modest stature. Despite its relative modesty, Gahinga's summit holds a small swamp-filled crater and the slopes support important Afromontane forest, including giant lobelias and groundsels that give the upper zone a genuinely otherworldly appearance — plants that grow 5–7 m tall on stems adapted to the thin highland air.

## Mountain Gorillas

The five Rwandan Virunga volcanoes are the core of Volcanoes National Park (Parc National des Volcans), established in 1925 as one of Africa's first national parks. The park is home to approximately 12 gorilla family groups habituated to human presence, representing roughly a third of the global mountain gorilla population (the total population is estimated at around 1,000 individuals as of 2025). Gorilla trekking permits for Rwanda are some of the most expensive wildlife experiences on earth — approximately 1,500 USD per person per visit — but this pricing directly finances conservation and community development.

## Dian Fossey and the Karisoke Research Centre

No account of the Rwandan Virungaes is complete without reference to the American primatologist Dian Fossey, whose 18 years of fieldwork beginning in 1967 fundamentally changed human understanding of mountain gorilla behaviour and catalysed the global conservation response that reversed the species' decline. Fossey was murdered at Karisoke in December 1985, likely in connection with her fierce opposition to poaching. Her grave and the ruins of the research centre remain in the forest at 3,000 m; a dedicated museum in Musanze (Ruhengeri) tells the story. The Karisoke Research Centre continues operating under the Dian Fossey Gorilla Fund.

## Climate and Best Visiting Season

The Virunga volcanoes experience two wet seasons (March–May and October–November) and two drier periods (June–September and December–February). The drier months offer better hiking conditions and clearer summit views, though cloud is common year-round above 2,500 m. Night temperatures on Karisimbi's summit can drop below -5°C even in the dry season, and hypothermia is a genuine risk for unprepared climbers. All hiking in Volcanoes National Park must be done with licensed guides from the Rwanda Development Board.
