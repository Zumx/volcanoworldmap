Beneath the young volcanic landscapes of Hawaii, the Canary Islands, and Iceland, a hidden world of tunnels winds for kilometres through solid basalt. These are lava tubes — natural conduits through which fluid basaltic lava once flowed, and which were left as empty caverns when the eruption ceased and the lava drained away. They are among the most accessible geological wonders in the world, frequently open to public exploration, and they preserve remarkable records of past volcanic activity. Understanding how they form reveals a fundamental but elegant process of volcanic self-insulation.

## How Lava Tubes Form

When basaltic lava erupts from a vent and flows downslope, its outer surface cools and solidifies relatively quickly, forming a crust of dark basalt. This crust acts as an insulating layer — like the skin on a bowl of cooling soup — that slows the loss of heat from the interior. The molten lava continues to flow beneath this crust, effectively using the hardened shell as a pipe. As long as the eruption continues to supply lava, the tube maintains a steady flow of molten rock at temperatures around 1,100 to 1,200°C. When the eruption stops or the supply of lava is diverted, the remaining molten rock drains out through the lower end of the tube, leaving behind a hollow tunnel.

## The World's Longest Lava Tubes

The longest continuously mapped lava tube in the world is Kazumura Cave on the flanks of Kilauea, Big Island of Hawaii. Kazumura runs for more than 65 kilometres and reaches depths of over 1,100 metres — making it not only the longest but also the deepest lava tube ever documented. It formed during a massive eruption on Kilauea's east rift zone centuries ago and preserves multiple lava levels, lava stalactites and stalagmites (called lavacicles), and extensive sections of original lava tube walls coated in shimmering mineral deposits. Much of Kazumura is on private property and requires permission and a guide to enter.

On Lanzarote in the Canary Islands, the Jameos del Agua and Cueva de los Verdes form part of the Jameos-Túnel de la Atlántida lava tube system, which extends beneath the sea for about 1.5 kilometres, making it one of the world's longest submarine lava tubes. Cueva de los Verdes is open for guided tours and offers one of the most dramatic public lava tube experiences available anywhere, with cathedral-like chambers, dramatic lighting, and rock walls displaying the full palette of colours produced by mineral oxidation.

## Thurston Lava Tube (Nāhuku), Hawaii Volcanoes National Park

The most visited publicly accessible lava tube in the world is Thurston Lava Tube, also known as Nāhuku, in Hawaii Volcanoes National Park on the Big Island. It formed during a Kilauea eruption roughly 500 years ago. A short loop trail descends through tree fern forest into a section of the tube about 130 metres long and 9 metres wide, with a ceiling height of 6 to 8 metres. The walls glisten with moisture and are lined with green ferns and liverworts that colonise the humid interior. Visiting is free with the national park entry fee, and the main section is fully lit and paved.

## Lava Tubes of the Canary Islands

Beyond Lanzarote, Tenerife and La Palma host notable lava tube systems. La Palma's Cueva del Viento is one of the longest volcanic tunnels in the world at over 17 kilometres and the most complex in Europe, with five levels of superimposed tubes formed during different eruptions of Teide's volcanic system. It is accessible only by guided tour on specific days, with numbers strictly limited to protect the cave's fragile formations. The island chain itself is a hotspot volcanic system sitting above a plume in the eastern Atlantic, making the Canary Islands one of the most volcanic archipelagos outside the Pacific.

## Lava Tubes in Iceland

Iceland's active volcanic landscape produces new lava tubes with each major rift eruption. The Vatnshellir cave, accessible on the Snæfellsnes Peninsula, is a 200-metre section of an 8,000-year-old tube formed during Snæfellsjökull's last major eruption. Guided tours descend through two levels of tube formation, with spectacular stalactites of dark basalt and lava benches — ledges left behind as the lava level dropped in stages. The most recently formed tubes are found in the Fagradalsfjall eruption area on the Reykjanes Peninsula, where lava fields from 2021 to 2023 created new tube systems now accessible by specialist guided expeditions.

## What You Find Inside

Lava tube interiors preserve a fascinating array of secondary formations and features. Lavacicles — the volcanic equivalent of stalactites and stalagmites — form where viscous lava drips from the ceiling or squeezes up through cracks in the floor. Lava benches mark former flow levels. Lava shelves and ledges trace the successive lowering of the lava surface. Tube walls often display a characteristic striped pattern in ochre, brown, and black from iron and manganese oxidation at different temperatures. Some tubes support unique ecosystems: the blind, depigmented lava cave crab Munida elso lives nowhere else in the world but in the flooded section of the Jameos del Agua tube in Lanzarote.

## Lava Tubes Beyond Earth

Lava tubes are not unique to Earth. NASA scientists believe that Mars and the Moon both possess lava tube systems formed during ancient volcanic episodes. Lunar lava tubes, imaged by the SELENE and Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter missions, may be orders of magnitude larger than those on Earth — potentially hundreds of metres wide — because the Moon's lower gravity allows larger tubes to remain structurally stable. These tubes are of intense interest as potential shelters for future human bases, providing natural protection from cosmic radiation, micrometeorites, and extreme temperature swings.

## Planning Your Visit

For most visitors, guided tours at well-managed sites — Nāhuku in Hawaii, Cueva de los Verdes on Lanzarote, Vatnshellir on Snæfellsnes — offer the best and safest introduction to lava tubes. Temperatures inside active lava tube environments hover between 12 and 18°C year-round, so bring a layer regardless of outside weather. Wear sturdy shoes with closed toes; lava cave floors are uneven and slippery. Flash photography may be restricted at sensitive sites. Always stay on marked routes and never enter unmarked tubes without a qualified guide.
