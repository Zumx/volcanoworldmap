Vietnam is not a country that announces its volcanic past loudly. The red laterite soils that characterise the central highlands — Da Lat, Buon Ma Thuot, Pleiku — are the weathered remnants of basaltic lava flows erupted millions of years ago, and the rolling hills that seem so gentle from a motorbike window are actually ancient shield volcanoes worn smooth by tropical erosion. But the volcanic story is more recent than that: scattered across the central highlands and along the coast of the South China Sea, Vietnam has dormant cones, crater lakes, and lava tube systems whose last eruptions occurred within the past few hundred thousand years.

## Chư Đang Ya — The Wild Garlic Volcano

Chư Đang Ya (Núi lửa Chư Đang Ya) in Gia Lai Province is Vietnam's most visually striking dormant volcano, a classic conical hill rising about 600 metres above the surrounding plateau. The Jarai people call it the wild garlic mountain, and the name fits: the rich volcanic soil on its flanks produces unusually potent vegetables. Every spring, wildflowers — including a rare species of sunflower found only here — cover the upper slopes in yellow. The crater is shallow and accessible by trail, offering a 360-degree panorama over the Pleiku basalt plateau.

## Chư B'luk — The Active Crater of Dak Nong

Chư B'luk (Núi lửa Chư B'luk) stands near the town of Ea Sup in Dak Nong Province and is the site of one of the largest lava tube systems in Southeast Asia. The Dray Nur–Chư B'luk UNESCO Global Geopark was established in 2020 partly in recognition of these tubes, which extend for tens of kilometres beneath the plateau. Inside the main tubes, stalactites formed from calcium-rich drip water hang above lava surface features — a rare combination of speleothem and volcanic morphology. The volcano itself is a low shield with multiple pit craters.

## Núi Lửa Chư Hdrông — The Urban Crater

Chư Hdrông (Núi lửa Chư Hdrông) is remarkable for its location: the crater sits almost entirely within the city limits of Pleiku, capital of Gia Lai Province. What would elsewhere be a protected geological monument is here surrounded by houses, market stalls, and a small temple. The crater lake — T'Nưng Lake, also called Bien Ho (Sea Lake) — is 24 metres deep and sits in a caldera-like depression. Residents fish from its shores and use it as a recreational park, apparently unbothered by the fact that they live on the rim of a volcano.

## Núi Thới Lới — Lý Sơn Island

Off the coast of Quảng Ngãi Province, Lý Sơn Island is the eroded remnant of a volcanic island complex whose most prominent feature is Núi Thới Lới, the highest point at 169 metres. From its summit, the sea stretches blue in every direction and the basaltic cliffs that ring the island are clearly volcanic in origin. The island also preserves Núi Hòn Sỏi and Núi Giếng Tiền — a well and coin mountain — both smaller volcanic cones now farmed with garlic, a Lý Sơn specialty that grows nowhere else in Vietnam with such flavour, attributed to the island's volcanic soils.

## Băng Mo — Hidden Crater in the Highlands

Núi lửa Băng Mo sits in remote Dak Lak Province, a small shield volcano whose existence was barely documented until recent geological surveys mapped the highlands in detail. The area around the cone is still largely forested and the approach trails pass through stands of ironwood that haven't been cleared for coffee cultivation. The crater is draped in mosses and ferns fed by year-round highland moisture, a greenhouse-like interior that contrasts sharply with the dry-season landscape outside.

## Nam Dong — Southern Highlands Cone

Núi lửa Nam Dong in Kon Tum Province is one of the more isolated volcanic features in Vietnam's central highlands, sitting within a protected forest corridor that limits access. The lava flows from Nam Dong are relatively fresh by geological standards and created a distinctive landscape of angular basalt boulders now smothered by dipterocarp forest. Indigenous Bahnar communities in the surrounding villages have used the volcanic rock for centuries as a building material and for rituals associated with the earth.

## Nâm Gleh R'luh — Basalt Plateau Vent

Núi lửa Nâm Gleh R'luh marks one of the northernmost volcanic vents on Vietnam's central basalt plateau, in a zone where the plateau begins to fragment into isolated mesas and deep river gorges. The vent is surrounded by coffee and pepper estates whose dark red soil immediately betrays a basaltic parent material. During the rainy season, the approach road washes out regularly — a reminder that volcanic soils, however fertile, are highly erodible in intense tropical downpours.

## Nâm Kar — Twin Lava Fields

Núi lửa Nâm Kar produced two distinct lava flows in its eruptive history, creating fields of slightly different ages that can be distinguished by the degree of weathering and soil development. The younger flow, darker and less colonised by vegetation, shows pressure ridges and inflation structures typical of pahoehoe lava. The older flow has been fully incorporated into the agricultural landscape, its surface indistinguishable from the red earth of the surrounding plateau except to a trained geological eye.

## Núi Bà Đen — The Black Lady Mountain

Núi Bà Đen — the Black Lady Mountain — in Tay Ninh Province rises to 986 metres, an isolated volcanic plug that towers over the flat alluvial plain surrounding it. The mountain is a sacred site for Cao Dai pilgrims, who make their way to a series of temples and grottoes cut into the volcanic rock. The cable car to the summit was the longest in Southeast Asia at the time of its opening. Although not strictly a volcano in the eruptive sense, the mountain's igneous origin is fundamental to its character, both geological and cultural.

## Cư M'gar and the Dak Lak Plateau Vents

The volcanic features around Cư M'gar — including Đồi Cư M'gar and Đồi Cư Suê — are among the smallest and most agriculturally transformed volcanic landforms in Vietnam. Their flanks are planted densely with coffee — Dak Lak is Vietnam's coffee capital — and during harvest season the red cherries against the green plants against the dark basalt rocks create a colour palette unique to Vietnam's volcanic landscapes. Though modest in scale, these low cones are essential to understanding the dense, low-elevation volcanic field that underlies much of the province.
