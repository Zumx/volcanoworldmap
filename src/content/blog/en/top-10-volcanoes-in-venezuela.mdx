Venezuela is not a country that appears in most lists of volcanic nations, yet beneath the
South American plate and along the complex fault systems of the Caribbean–South America
plate boundary, enough heat and tectonic stress accumulates to produce geothermal systems,
volcanic landforms, and igneous rocks that tell a story spanning hundreds of millions of
years. The Venezuelan Andes — the Mérida range specifically — host the most concentrated
evidence of relatively recent volcanic and geothermal activity, while the offshore islands
of the Venezuelan Caribbean reveal a history of oceanic volcanism entirely separate from
the Andean story.

## The Venezuelan Andes and Geothermal Heat

The Mérida Andes, the northernmost arm of the Andean chain as it swings north into Venezuela,
owe their topographic height not primarily to recent volcanism but to the collision and
compression of the South American and Caribbean plates. Yet this same tectonic vigour has
generated substantial geothermal heat within the crust. The city of Mérida — Venezuela's
highest city at about 1,630 metres — sits surrounded by a landscape where faulting has
exposed thermally altered rocks and hot spring systems. Fumaroles and warm springs occur
in several localities within the Venezuelan Andes, most notably in the Mucuchíes area of
Mérida state and near the town of Bailadores further south.

## Volcán de Yagrumito: Venezuela's Most Contested Volcanic Feature

In the southern reaches of Mérida state, near the border with Barinas, a poorly documented
volcanic or volcanic-related structure known as Volcán de Yagrumito has attracted periodic
attention from Venezuelan geologists. Its status is genuinely ambiguous: some sources
describe it as a small maar volcano or explosion crater; others interpret the feature as
a hydrothermal alteration zone with no true volcanic edifice. Access is difficult —
the area is reached by rough tracks from the small town of Yagrumito, and the dense
cloud forest surrounding the feature makes remote sensing difficult. What is not in dispute
is that the area shows anomalous surface temperatures, gas emissions, and hydrothermally
altered clays consistent with heat flow from depth. Whether this represents a dormant
volcano, a fossil geothermal field, or an active system awaiting eruption remains an open
scientific question.

## La Orchila: Volcanic Island of the Venezuelan Caribbean

Roughly 200 kilometres north of the mainland, La Orchila is a Venezuelan federal dependency
and naval base in the Caribbean Sea. It is of volcanic origin, formed as part of the
Lesser Antilles-related arc system that extends from the Windward Islands toward Venezuela.
La Orchila's rocks are predominantly basaltic and andesitic, similar to those of Curaçao
and the ABC islands, though unlike those islands it lacks prominent volcanic edifices today.
Access is restricted to military personnel, making geological study extremely limited. The
island chain that includes La Orchila, La Blanquilla, and Los Roques represents the
submerged and eroded remnants of a former arc system, the geologic heritage of a time
when the Caribbean–South America collision was actively generating volcanism in the
offshore region.

## The Guiana Shield and Intraplate Basalts

South of the Orinoco River, the ancient Guiana Shield is one of the oldest stable
continental cratons on Earth, with Precambrian rocks dating back nearly 4 billion years.
Despite its great age and stability, the shield was intruded by dolerite sills and dykes
during the Mesozoic Pangea breakup, and these dark igneous intrusions are exposed in the
walls of Venezuela's famous tepui tabletop mountains — Roraima, Autana, and Auyán-tepui
among them. The black dolerite columns that stripe the flanks of these sandstone plateaus
are not volcanic in the eruptive sense but represent magma that intruded laterally into
existing rocks without reaching the surface.

## Hot Springs and Geothermal Springs of the Mérida Region

Beyond Yagrumito, Venezuela's most accessible geothermal expressions are its hot springs.
In Mérida state, the Aguas Termales de Bailadores is a popular resort drawing visitors to
waters at around 38 degrees Celsius. Further north, in Trujillo state, the Aguas Calientes
de Motatán emerge along faulted volcanic rock contacts. These springs are fed by meteoric
water that descends along major fault systems into the crust, picks up heat from the
geothermal gradient — somewhat elevated beneath the Andes — and rises back to the surface.
They are the most viscerally accessible reminder that Venezuela's crust, like all crust, is
hot at depth.

## The Falcón Basin: Ancient Volcanic Rocks

The Falcón Basin in northwestern Venezuela, between the Andes and the Caribbean coast,
preserves sequences of Eocene and Oligocene volcanic and volcanoclastic rocks that record
a period of arc-related magmatism some 35 to 45 million years ago. These rocks — exposed
in road cuts and riverbank outcrops across Falcón state — are the eroded remnants of an
ancient volcanic arc system that has since been largely buried or removed by erosion.
The town of Coro, a UNESCO World Heritage city, is built on these ancient volcanoclastic
sediments.

## Caribbean Plate Tectonics and Venezuela's Volcanic Future

Venezuela's limited present-day volcanism is a direct consequence of its tectonic setting.
The Caribbean plate moves eastward relative to South America along a complex zone of
right-lateral strike-slip faults running along the northern coast of Venezuela — the
El Pilar fault system — rather than subducting beneath it. Without active subduction along
Venezuela's Pacific or Atlantic margins, there is no mechanism to generate arc volcanism
of the sort seen in the Colombian Andes to the west or in the Eastern Caribbean arc to the
east. The geothermal heat in the Mérida Andes reflects crustal thickening and collision
rather than mantle melting above a subducting slab.

## Mud Volcanoes of the Orinoco Delta

In the Orinoco Delta region of eastern Venezuela, natural gas seeps produce mud volcanoes
analogous to those found in Azerbaijan or Iran. These features — small cones of bubbling
mud driven by methane gas rising from petroleum reservoirs at depth — are associated with
the Warao indigenous communities who inhabit the delta and who have longstanding
cosmological relationships with the seeping gas and mud. Though not magmatic in origin,
they are striking geological phenomena in a landscape otherwise defined by river channels
and mangrove swamps.

## Tepuis as Volcanic Analogues

Venezuela's tepuis — the flat-topped sandstone mountains of the Gran Sabana — have no
volcanic origin but occupy a conceptual space in the public imagination similar to volcanoes.
Their dramatic cliffs of red and brown sandstone, often capped by cloud, their isolation and
sense of ancient mystery, and the black intrusive rock of the dolerite dykes that cut
through them all contribute to a landscape that feels primordial and volcanic even when it
is not. Angel Falls, the world's highest uninterrupted waterfall at 979 metres, plunges
from the face of Auyán-tepui over rocks that include both ancient sandstone and the dark
Mesozoic dolerite intrusions.
