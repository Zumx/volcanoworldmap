The ground beneath the coastal town of Pozzuoli, a western suburb of Naples, has been moving for centuries. Roman columns at the ancient market of the Macellum of Pozzuoli bear the unmistakable bore marks of marine molluscs at a height of about 7 metres above their current base — evidence that the ground has risen and fallen by several metres at least once in historical times, submersing the columns underwater before lifting them back above the sea surface. This phenomenon, called bradyseism, is the surface expression of one of Europe's most dangerous volcanic systems: the Campi Flegrei caldera, a vast volcanic depression stretching 12 kilometres across beneath the western suburbs of Naples and the Pozzuoli Bay.

## The Scale of the Caldera

Campi Flegrei — meaning "Burning Fields" in Italian — is a supervolcano. Its caldera was formed by a colossal eruption approximately 39,000 years ago known as the Campanian Ignimbrite event, which deposited thick layers of pyroclastic material across a vast swath of southern Italy and beyond, and is thought to have contributed to the decline of Neanderthal populations in Europe. A second major caldera-forming event occurred around 15,000 years ago. The current active volcanic field contains at least 24 craters and volcanic vents, including Monte Nuovo, which was the site of the caldera's most recent eruption in 1538 and emerged from the sea floor in just a few days. The caldera rim is largely submerged beneath the Pozzuoli Bay and the Tyrrhenian Sea.

## Bradyseism: The Slow Pulse of a Restless Caldera

Bradyseism is the gradual uplift or subsidence of the ground surface caused by movements of magma and hydrothermal fluids within the volcanic system. At Campi Flegrei, it has operated for millennia, with documented episodes of rapid uplift in 1969-1972 and 1982-1984 that raised the town of Pozzuoli by approximately 1.7 metres in total and prompted the evacuation of 40,000 residents from the oldest parts of the town. Since 2005, a new episode of sustained uplift has been underway, with the ground in some areas rising by more than 1 metre by the early 2020s. This prolonged episode has raised scientific alarm, with INGV monitoring teams observing increasing seismicity, ground deformation, and changes in fumarolic gas chemistry at the Solfatara crater.

## The Solfatara and Pisciarelli

The most visible surface expressions of Campi Flegrei's volcanic activity are the Solfatara crater and the adjacent Pisciarelli fumarolic field. The Solfatara is a shallow maar-like crater in the municipality of Pozzuoli where sulphurous gases, steam, and bubbling mud pools have been active for at least 2,000 years. The Romans called it Forum Vulcani — the Forum of Vulcan — and considered it sacred. The Pisciarelli area has intensified dramatically since 2012, with new fumarolic vents opening and gas temperatures increasing. The site was partially closed to the public following a tragic accident in 2017, when three members of a family fell through the thin crust near a boiling mud pool and died.

## Monte Nuovo: The 1538 Eruption

Monte Nuovo — "New Mountain" — is a small cinder cone that grew to about 120 metres in just a week in September and October 1538, following a period of intense bradyseism that had raised the local ground by several metres and emptied the ancient harbour of Pozzuoli of its water as the seafloor rose. The eruption was relatively small but caused significant local casualties and destroyed parts of the town of Tripergole. It was the first eruption witnessed by modern scientific observers in the region and marked the end of a repose period that had lasted since Roman times. Monte Nuovo is now a protected nature reserve and provides a dramatic illustration of how quickly a new volcanic edifice can form within the Campi Flegrei field.

## The Rione Terra and Roman Heritage

The Rione Terra, the ancient acropolis of Pozzuoli built on volcanic rock above the town, was evacuated in 1970 due to bradyseism damage. Its underground archaeological layer, buried beneath centuries of construction, was rediscovered during restoration work and found to contain an extraordinary Roman city preserved beneath the mediaeval structures — streets, shops, and an ancient temple converted into the first cathedral. The site is now partially accessible to visitors and illustrates the remarkable geological instability that has shaped human settlement in the region for more than 2,500 years.

## Risk and Evacuation Planning

Campi Flegrei sits beneath a population of approximately 360,000 people in the innermost Red Zone, with millions more in the broader hazard area including Naples itself. Italy's National Emergency Plan for Campi Flegrei divides the risk zone into sectors and assigns evacuation routes and receiving regions, mirroring the structure of the Vesuvius plan. However, the geography of Campi Flegrei is more complex than Vesuvius — the hazard zone covers multiple municipalities on both sides of Pozzuoli Bay, and the nature of a potential eruption is highly uncertain. It could range from a small phreatic event at an existing fumarolic vent to a much larger magmatic eruption at an entirely new location within the caldera.

## Scientific Debate and Alert Levels

The INGV raised the alert level for Campi Flegrei from green (background) to yellow (attention) in 2012, where it has remained. In 2023, the alert was raised further to orange (warning) — the first time orange had been declared for Campi Flegrei in four decades — following an intensification of seismicity and ground uplift. Debate within the scientific community centres on whether the current unrest represents a gradual approach toward a future eruption or a long-term process of magmatic intrusion that could continue for decades without erupting. The Stromboli volcano in the Aeolian Islands, Vulcano, and Etna — all part of the broader Italian volcanic arc — are also closely monitored, but none present the population-at-risk challenge posed by Campi Flegrei.
