On the morning of 14 November 1963, the cook aboard the fishing vessel Ísleifur II noticed something odd off the south coast of Iceland: what looked like a vessel on fire. The dark column rising from the sea was not smoke from a ship but the opening phase of a volcanic eruption from a submarine vent 130 metres below the ocean surface. Over the following four years the eruption built an island from nothing — first tephra cones battered and reshaped by Atlantic waves, then protected by flowing lava that hardened into solid basalt resistant to erosion. By the time activity ceased in 1967, Surtsey covered 2.7 square kilometres and rose 174 metres above sea level, named for Surtr, the Norse fire giant. Today, eroded to about 1.3 square kilometres, it is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the most carefully monitored ecological laboratories in the world.

## The Eruption That Built an Island

The Surtsey eruption began with classic Surtseyan activity — the style of eruption named after it, characterised by explosive interaction of magma and seawater producing tall jets of steam and dark tephra. When water could reach the vent freely, the explosions were violent, building the island as fragmented, unconsolidated scoria. But as the island grew and the vent was eventually sealed above sea level, the eruption style shifted to quieter lava fountaining and effusive flows that hardened into pillow lavas and solid lava shields. It was this lava hardening that preserved the island against the relentless erosion of North Atlantic seas, which had destroyed earlier fragile tephra cones in a matter of weeks.

## Iceland's Volcanic Context

Surtsey belongs to the Vestmannaeyjar (Westman Islands) archipelago, a cluster of volcanic islands and seamounts off Iceland's southern coast created by the same hotspot and mid-ocean ridge system that drives all of Iceland's volcanism. Eldfell on Heimaey island, the archipelago's main inhabited island, erupted in 1973 and nearly buried the fishing town of Vestmannaeyjar before a heroic effort using seawater pumping slowed and eventually halted the lava advance. Helgafell, Eldfell's older sibling on the same island, last erupted approximately 5,000 years ago and forms the backdrop to the modern town. Surtsey itself is part of an underwater ridge system that includes the seamount Syrtlingur, which briefly appeared above sea level in 1965 before being erased by erosion, and Jólnir, which similarly emerged and disappeared.

## Life on New Lava: The Ecological Experiment

From the moment Surtsey's lava cooled, scientists established strict access restrictions to protect the ecological value of the island as a pristine colonisation laboratory. Only authorised researchers may land on Surtsey; no tourist access is permitted. The results have been extraordinary. Within three years of the eruption ending, mosses and the first vascular plant — sea rocket — had established themselves in hollows where wind-blown soil and bird droppings had accumulated. By the 1980s a self-sustaining plant community was developing. Today approximately 89 plant species have been recorded, along with 335 invertebrate species, seabirds including puffins and gulls nesting in burrows and cliff ledges, and harbour seals using the shoreline.

## The Role of Birds in Colonisation

Birds proved to be the primary vector for life on Surtsey. Seabirds roosting on the island deposited seeds from distant plants in their droppings, introduced soil bacteria via their guano, and attracted soil invertebrates as their nesting burrows created habitat. The first great black-backed gull bred on Surtsey in 1974, and their breeding colony — along with Atlantic puffins arriving later — dramatically accelerated the pace of soil formation and plant colonisation in the areas beneath nesting colonies. Research teams visiting annually to census the breeding populations have documented the process of island ecosystem assembly in real time, producing data of global significance for understanding how life recovers after volcanic events.

## Geology of an Oceanic Island

Surtsey's geology provides a near-perfect natural experiment in oceanic island formation. The basaltic lava flows are typical of mid-ocean ridge basalt, similar in composition to the lavas of the Reykjanes Peninsula on Iceland's mainland. The transition from explosive Surtseyan activity to effusive shield-building mirrors the process that originally built Iceland itself — and indeed the process that created Hawaii's main islands millions of years before. Geophysical surveys have mapped the subsurface structure of the island, revealing a plumbing system of dykes and sills connecting to the broader volcanic system beneath the Vestmannaeyjar.

## Erosion and the Island's Future

Despite the protective lava cap, Surtsey has lost nearly half its original area to ocean erosion since 1967. Wave action cuts into the tephra flanks not protected by lava, and the island gradually subsides as the ocean floor beneath it cools and contracts. Scientists estimate that Surtsey may disappear below sea level within a few centuries — making the ongoing ecological research all the more urgent. Annual monitoring expeditions, typically lasting one to two weeks in summer, measure shoreline retreat, update plant and animal censuses, and collect soil samples to track biogeochemical change.

## Visiting the Westman Islands

While landing on Surtsey is restricted to scientists, the Westman Islands are readily accessible by ferry from Þorlákshöfn (about 30 minutes crossing) or by a short flight from Reykjavík. Heimaey is a rewarding destination in its own right: the 1973 lava field from Eldfell's eruption is accessible on foot, and the Eldheimar museum — built around a house excavated from the 1973 ash — offers a gripping account of the eruption. Boat tours from Heimaey's harbour circle Surtsey at a respectful distance, offering views of the eroded cliffs and nesting seabird colonies from the sea.
