Volcano monitoring agencies around the world use alert level systems to communicate the state of volcanic activity — not just to scientists and emergency managers, but to residents, tourists, and pilots. These systems vary in their exact terminology, the number of levels they use, and the agencies that administer them. But their underlying logic is remarkably consistent: they range from background or normal activity through elevated unrest to eruption in progress, and they trigger specific emergency responses at each stage. Knowing how to read these alerts — and understanding what they do and do not tell you — is essential for anyone travelling near active volcanoes.

## The Aviation Colour Code: Green, Yellow, Orange, Red

The most internationally standardised alert system is the aviation colour code, used to inform pilots about ash hazards. It is administered in the United States by the US Geological Survey (USGS) Volcano Hazards Program and by equivalent agencies in Iceland, New Zealand, Japan, and elsewhere. The four levels are:

Green: The volcano is at a normal background level. No eruption is occurring, and no unrest beyond typical background is detected. No aviation hazard.

Yellow: The volcano is exhibiting signs of elevated unrest above known background levels. This might include increased earthquake frequency, ground deformation, or changes in gas emissions. No eruption is anticipated in the immediate future, but monitoring is intensified. Aviation: Be aware.

Orange: An eruption is expected or imminent, or an eruption is occurring but is producing limited or no ash. For pilots, this means significant ash emissions may occur soon and flight planning should avoid the volcano. For residents and tourists, this is typically the level at which evacuation orders begin to be prepared or issued for communities closest to the volcano.

Red: A major eruption is in progress or imminent, with significant ash emissions that pose a hazard to aviation. This is the highest aviation alert level. Ground hazards — pyroclastic flows, lava, lahars — are also typically most severe at this stage.

## The USGS Volcano Alert Levels: A Parallel System

The USGS also operates a parallel ground-based alert level system using the terms Normal, Advisory, Watch, and Warning. These correspond roughly to Green, Yellow, Orange, and Red, but are specifically oriented toward ground hazards rather than aviation. A Watch means elevated unrest with the potential for eruption. A Warning means an eruption is imminent or occurring with significant ground hazards. During the 2018 eruption of Kilauea's Lower East Rift Zone, the USGS maintained Red/Warning status for months as lava inundated communities in the Leilani Estates area of the Big Island, destroying more than 700 homes.

## Indonesia: The Four-Level PVMBG System

Indonesia's Centre for Volcanology and Geological Hazard Mitigation (PVMBG) manages alert levels for more than 120 active volcanoes. Its system uses four levels: Normal (Level 1, green), Waspada meaning Alert (Level 2, yellow), Siaga meaning Standby (Level 3, orange), and Awas meaning Danger (Level 4, red). At Level 4, exclusion zones of 5 to 10 kilometres or more around the summit are typically enforced, all tourism to the volcano is suspended, and residents within the hazard zone are evacuated. Merapi, Java's most dangerous volcano, regularly oscillates between Levels 2 and 3 and has reached Level 4 during major eruptions in 2010 and 2014. The 2010 eruption killed 353 people, many of whom refused to evacuate despite Awas status.

## Japan: The Japan Meteorological Agency System

Japan's JMA uses a five-level volcanic alert system. Level 1 (Active volcano): Normal climbing and residential activity permitted. Level 2 (Do not approach crater): Access to within a few hundred metres of the crater is prohibited. Level 3 (Restrict mountain entry): Climbing and access to the mountain area is prohibited, typically within 1 to 4 kilometres of the summit. Level 4 (Prepare to evacuate): Residents near the volcano should prepare to leave. Level 5 (Evacuate): Immediate evacuation of residents in danger zones. Mount Ontake was at Level 1 when it erupted without warning in September 2014, killing 63 people in one of Japan's deadliest post-war volcanic disasters. This event prompted a review of the JMA system and its reliance on seismic precursors.

## New Zealand: The Volcanic Alert Bulletin

GeoNet, New Zealand's hazard monitoring agency, uses a six-level Volcanic Alert Level (VAL) from 0 to 5. Level 0 is no volcanic unrest; Level 1 is minor unrest; Level 2 is moderate unrest; Level 3 is minor eruptive activity; Level 4 is moderate eruption; Level 5 is major eruption. Whakaari (White Island), a frequently active marine volcano in the Bay of Plenty, was at Level 2 when it erupted on 9 December 2019, killing 22 people including tourists who had been on the island minutes before. The disaster revealed a critical ambiguity in alert systems: Level 2 means unrest is occurring, but it does not specify when or if an eruption will follow — and some visitors interpreted it as safe.

## What Alert Levels Cannot Tell You

Alert levels are snapshots of current conditions based on observed data. They cannot predict the exact timing of eruptions. A volcano can move from Level 1 to Level 4 in hours (as Ontake demonstrated) or remain at Level 3 for months without a major eruption. They also cannot communicate the full range of hazards: a volcano at Level 1 may still emit toxic gas concentrations near the crater, produce small explosive blasts, or generate lahars during heavy rain without any increase in volcanic unrest.

## Hazard Zone Maps: The Spatial Dimension

Beyond alert levels, volcano observatories publish hazard zone maps showing the likely spatial extent of different hazards — pyroclastic flows, ashfall, lava inundation, lahars — for different eruption scenarios. These maps are the tool that emergency managers use to plan evacuation routes and designate exclusion zones. They are based on modelling of past eruptions and the topography around the volcano. Before visiting any active volcano, checking the relevant observatory's current hazard zone map is as important as checking the alert level.
