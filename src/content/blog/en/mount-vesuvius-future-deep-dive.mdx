The last time Vesuvius erupted was in March 1944. Allied soldiers camped on its lower slopes watched streams of lava destroy the town of San Sebastiano al Vesuvio and marvelled at ash columns rising above the front lines. Since then, the volcano has been quiet — deceptively, worryingly quiet. For volcanologists at the Osservatorio Vesuviano, part of the Italian National Institute of Geophysics and Volcanology (INGV), that silence is not reassuring. Vesuvius stands above the most densely populated volcanic risk zone in the world, and every year that passes without an eruption is a year in which the pressure in the magmatic system continues its slow, uncertain build.

## The Geography of Danger

The Zona Rossa — the Red Zone — around Vesuvius encompasses 18 municipalities and approximately 700,000 residents. This is the area that emergency planners believe cannot be protected in the event of a major eruption; the only strategy is evacuation before the event. The zone includes towns like Ercolano (ancient Herculaneum), Torre del Greco, Portici, and Boscoreale — all within 10 kilometres of the summit, all historically devastated by Vesuvian eruptions in the centuries before and after the catastrophic AD 79 event that buried Pompeii and Herculaneum. The question of how to evacuate 700,000 people in the narrow window between the first volcanic signals and the eruption itself is one of the most complex emergency planning problems in Europe.

## Vesuvius as a Geological System

Vesuvius is a stratovolcano that sits within the caldera of an older volcano, Monte Somma, whose remnant ridge is visible to the north and northeast. This nested structure — called a somma-vesuvio type — is found at several volcanoes worldwide, including Gunung Semeru within the Tengger caldera in Java. The magmatic system beneath Vesuvius is thought to be connected to a much larger and more complex system beneath the Campi Flegrei caldera to the west of Naples. Eruptions from Vesuvius have historically ranged from relatively minor lava flows — such as the 1944 event — to Plinian catastrophes like AD 79, which produced a column of ash and pumice estimated at 33 kilometres in height.

## The 1944 Eruption in Context

The 1944 eruption was the last of a series of relatively moderate events that characterised Vesuvius through the 19th and early 20th centuries. Between 1631 and 1944, the volcano erupted dozens of times. The 1631 eruption was itself a major catastrophe, killing several thousand people. After 1944, the volcano entered its current quiescent phase. Volcanologists note that in Vesuvius's history, longer repose periods between eruptions tend to correlate with larger and more explosive events when eruptions eventually occur. The 79 AD eruption followed a period of repose of roughly 1,000 years — a sobering historical parallel.

## Risk Assessment and Monitoring

The INGV's Osservatorio Vesuviano operates a dense network of seismometers, GPS receivers, and gravimeters around the volcano and beneath the Bay of Naples. In the event of an awakening, scientists would look for shallow seismicity beneath the summit, accelerating ground uplift, changes in fumarolic gas chemistry — particularly an increase in SO2 relative to CO2 — and changes in gravity indicating magma movement. The current alert level is green (background), the lowest on Italy's four-level volcanic alert scale. Moderate seismic swarms occasionally occur beneath the volcano but have not yet signalled magmatic unrest.

## Evacuation Planning: A Political and Logistical Nightmare

Italy's Civil Protection Department first published a comprehensive Vesuvius evacuation plan in 1995 and has updated it periodically since. The plan divides the Red Zone into sectors, each assigned to a specific receiving region in other parts of Italy that has pledged to host evacuees. The logistical challenges are enormous: the narrow coastal strip between the volcano and the sea is served by limited roads, and the density of buildings and population means that the transport network would saturate almost immediately. A 2014 revision of the plan introduced voluntary pre-evacuation of at-risk residents as a preparedness measure, offering financial incentives to those who relocate permanently outside the Red Zone. The uptake has been limited.

## The Monte Somma Ridge and Strabo's Descriptions

Before the AD 79 eruption, Vesuvius had a broad, flat summit — a caldera that Roman writers described as covered in vines and inhabited by wildlife. Strabo noted that the mountain showed signs of having once burned violently, with ashy, blackened soil, but had long been dormant. The 79 AD eruption destroyed the existing cone and rebuilt it in a single catastrophic event. Monte Somma's ridge, preserved on the northern flank, is a remnant of the pre-79 caldera rim, standing at about 1,132 metres compared to Vesuvius's current summit of 1,281 metres.

## Campi Flegrei and the Regional Volcanic System

Vesuvius does not stand alone in the volcanic threat to Naples. Twenty kilometres to the west, the Campi Flegrei caldera has been experiencing bradyseism — slow ground uplift caused by magmatic and hydrothermal activity — since 2005. The interaction between the two systems, Vesuvius and Campi Flegrei, is an active area of research: some geologists propose that they share deep plumbing, others maintain they are independent. The broader Campanian volcanic system also includes the islands of Ischia and Procida, both volcanically active in recent centuries.
