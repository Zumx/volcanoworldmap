At the northern end of Kamchatka's central volcanic belt stands Shiveluch, one of
the largest and most active volcanoes on the peninsula. Its long history of massive
eruptions, dramatic flank collapses, and a persistently growing lava dome has made
it a focus of intense study and a recurring threat to North Pacific aviation.
Shiveluch is a volcano of immense scale and restless energy, embodying the raw
power of the Kamchatka volcanic region.

## A giant of the north

Shiveluch is one of the largest volcanoes in Kamchatka, a massive edifice whose
summit region reaches well over 3,000 metres. It is also among the most active,
with a long record of powerful eruptions. The volcano consists of an older, larger
edifice and a younger active part, where the present-day eruptions and dome growth
are concentrated, making it a complex and evolving system.

## A persistent lava dome

Much of Shiveluch's recent activity has centred on the growth of a lava dome within
its active crater. Viscous, silica-rich magma slowly extrudes to build the dome,
which periodically becomes unstable. As it grows and collapses, it generates
pyroclastic flows and powerful explosions, a cycle that has continued for years and
makes Shiveluch one of the most closely watched dome-building volcanoes in the
world.

## Powerful explosive eruptions

Shiveluch is capable of major explosive eruptions that send ash plumes soaring tens
of kilometres into the atmosphere. A significant eruption in 2023 produced an
enormous ash cloud and substantial ashfall across the surrounding region, blanketing
nearby villages. Such events demonstrate the volcano's capacity for large, sudden
escalations beyond its more routine dome activity.

## Flank collapse and debris avalanches

Like other large dome-building volcanoes, Shiveluch has a history of dramatic flank
collapses. When part of the steep, unstable edifice gives way, it can generate
enormous debris avalanches that sweep across the surrounding terrain. These
collapses are among the most powerful landscape-altering events a volcano can
produce, and Shiveluch's deposits record several such episodes.

## A hazard to aviation

Shiveluch's frequent ash emissions pose a serious threat to aircraft crossing the
busy North Pacific air routes. Its ash plumes can rise rapidly to cruising
altitudes, and monitoring agencies issue aviation ash advisories whenever the
volcano erupts. This makes Shiveluch's activity a matter of international concern,
not just a local hazard in a sparsely populated region.

## A remote wilderness setting

Shiveluch rises in the remote wilderness of northern Kamchatka, far from major
settlements, in a landscape of forests, rivers, and tundra. Its isolation means
that, while ashfall affects nearby villages, the volcano's main hazards play out
largely in uninhabited terrain. This remoteness also makes ground-based study
challenging, and much monitoring relies on satellites and remote sensing.

## Monitoring a restless giant

Russian volcanologists keep a close watch on Shiveluch, tracking its dome growth,
seismicity, and ash emissions. Because the volcano can shift quickly from steady
dome extrusion to violent explosions, continuous monitoring is essential both for
protecting nearby communities and for warning aircraft. Shiveluch is one of the key
volcanoes in Kamchatka's extensive surveillance network.
