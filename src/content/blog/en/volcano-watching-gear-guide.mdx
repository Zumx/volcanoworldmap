Standing close enough to see lava fountains light up the night sky at Kilauea, or watching a lava delta collapse into the Pacific in a shower of steam, is among the most visceral experiences nature offers. But volcanoes are indifferent to human safety. The gas plumes, falling tephra, sudden lava bench collapses, and — in the rarest and worst cases — explosive outbursts that kill without warning mean that proper preparation is not optional. This guide covers the equipment volcanologists, journalists, and experienced volcano enthusiasts use when approaching active eruptions, organised by hazard category.

## Respiratory Protection: Gas Masks and Filters

Volcanic gases kill more quietly than lava. Sulphur dioxide (SO₂), hydrogen sulphide (H₂S), carbon dioxide (CO₂), and hydrogen fluoride (HF) are the primary respiratory hazards at most basaltic and andesitic volcanoes. At Kilauea, the USGS Hawaiian Volcano Observatory regularly records SO₂ concentrations exceeding safe workplace limits downwind of active lava. A simple nuisance dust mask (N95 or P2) offers no protection against gases — it filters only particulates. For volcanic gas hazards, you need a full-face or half-face respirator fitted with combination cartridges rated for acid gases and particulates. The 3M 6500 series with 60926 combination cartridges is a practical and widely available option. For CO₂-rich environments such as the floor of Mammoth Mountain's horseshoe lake area in California — where CO₂ seeping from a dormant magma body has killed trees — even a respirator is insufficient; CO₂ is undetectable by smell and can asphyxiate silently. In such zones, a personal CO₂ detector is essential.

## Eye and Face Protection

Volcanic ash and Pele's hair — fine glass filaments spun from lava by the wind — are abrasive and can permanently damage corneas. Standard sunglasses provide no adequate seal. Sealed chemical-splash goggles or ski goggles with full foam perimeter seals are the minimum for any situation where ash is falling or blowing. A hard hat with integrated face shield adds protection from lapilli falls within 1–2 km of active vents.

## Footwear and Lower Body Protection

Lava fields — whether recent pahoehoe (smooth, ropy lava) or rough, jagged a'a — are treacherous underfoot. Pahoehoe crusts can conceal lava tubes that collapse without warning, dropping you into a hot void. A'a shreds standard hiking boots within hours. The minimum footwear for lava field walking is a leather hiking boot with a thick, rigid Vibram sole. Many experienced observers at Kilauea and Etna favour full leather gaiters to protect against radiated heat and splatter from small lava spatters. Heat-resistant Kevlar gaiters, rated to withstand brief contact with material at 200–300°C, are available from specialist suppliers and worth carrying if you plan to approach active lava benches.

## Clothing and Heat Protection

For any approach within 50 metres of active lava or a volcanic vent, natural fibres (wool, cotton, leather) are strongly preferred over synthetics. Nylon and polyester melt at relatively low temperatures and can cause severe contact burns. A wool base layer and a leather or cotton outer layer provide several seconds of protection against radiated heat and contact with hot tephra — sufficient to retreat before serious injury. A fire-resistant Nomex or PBI coverall, the kind used by firefighters, provides additional margin. Aluminium-coated proximity suits used by professional volcanologists and lava boat operators reflect radiated heat and are rated for work near active lava.

## Navigation and Communication

GPS accuracy on volcanic terrain can be degraded by the presence of metallic minerals, but a modern GPS unit (Garmin GPSMAP 66i or similar) remains essential for tracking your position relative to hazard zones. Mark your approach waypoints and escape routes before entering active terrain. A satellite communicator (Garmin inReach, SPOT Gen4) allows emergency messaging when there is no cellular coverage — which is the case at most remote volcanic sites. A two-metre handheld VHF radio lets you communicate with a monitoring observatory or a base party. Never enter active volcanic terrain alone.

## Photography and Observation Equipment

Photographing active eruptions from a safe distance requires long focal lengths. For lava fountaining at night — the most photogenic scenario — a 400–600 mm telephoto lens, a sturdy tripod, and a camera capable of high ISO performance (modern full-frame mirrorless cameras like the Sony A7 IV or Nikon Z8 work well) is the professional standard. For video, a 4K camera with optical image stabilisation handles the heat shimmer better than stabilisation in post. Thermal imaging cameras (FLIR Lepton or similar) are increasingly affordable and allow you to detect invisible heat variations in lava fields — including hot spots beneath crust that might otherwise look walkable. A high-quality binocular (10×42 or 12×50) lets you assess hazard conditions from a safe standpoint before committing to approach.

## Personal Safety Monitoring Devices

Portable MultiRAE or RAEGuard multi-gas detectors monitor SO₂, H₂S, CO, CO₂, and O₂ levels simultaneously and alarm when concentrations exceed pre-set thresholds. These are the instruments carried by volcanologists in the field; models suitable for non-specialists include the Industrial Scientific Ventis MX4 and the BW Clip 4-gas detector. A handheld radiation detector is unnecessary at most volcanoes but worth carrying at sites where naturally occurring radioactive material (NORM) exposure is elevated — some volcanic fumarolic areas have elevated radon levels.

## First Aid and Emergency Supplies

Burn treatment is the first-aid priority for volcano visits: a burn gel dressing kit (Water-Jel or similar), non-stick bandages, and sufficient sterile saline for eye washing. A thermal blanket and whistle are basic emergency kit. Carry a minimum of three litres of water per person per day in volcanic terrain, as the exertion of walking on rough lava in warm conditions is significant, and dehydration accelerates the impairment of judgement. Never rely on natural water sources near volcanic zones — they may be acidic enough to damage skin.
