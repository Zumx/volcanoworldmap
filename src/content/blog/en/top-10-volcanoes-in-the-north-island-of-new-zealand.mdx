The North Island of New Zealand sits directly above a tectonic collision zone where the Pacific Plate dives beneath the Australian Plate. This subduction feeds the Taupo Volcanic Zone (TVZ), a 300 km-long rift of extraordinary geological violence that stretches from White Island in the Bay of Plenty to Ruapehu in the central plateau. The TVZ is one of the world's most productive rhyolite-generating systems, responsible for some of the largest eruptions in geological history. Alongside the famous peaks of Tongariro National Park, the North Island contains the country's most active volcanic field, a supervolcano lake, and a live marine volcano visible from shore.

## Whakaari / White Island — New Zealand's Most Active Volcano

White Island, known by its Māori name Whakaari, is a continuously active stratovolcano that emerges from the sea about 48 km offshore from Whakatāne. Roughly 70 percent of the volcano lies below sea level. The crater contains an acid lake with a pH sometimes close to zero, and sulphur deposits have been mined here since the 1880s. Visitors were able to tour the crater floor until the fatal eruption of December 2019, when a sudden phreatomagmatic explosion killed 22 people. Access is now restricted; Whakaari is monitored 24 hours a day by GNS Science via seismic networks and satellite imagery.

## Tarawera — The 1886 Night of Terror

Mount Tarawera rises to 1,111 m southeast of Rotorua and is renowned for the catastrophic eruption of 10 June 1886, which killed an estimated 120 people in about four hours. The eruption was purely basaltic — rare for the rhyolite-dominated TVZ — and tore a 17 km-long fissure across the mountain's three dome complex. The Pink and White Terraces, once called the Eighth Wonder of the World, were destroyed and buried. Recent research suggests the terraces may have survived partially under Lake Rotomahana, which expanded enormously during the eruption as a result of phreatomagmatic blasts in the lake bed.

## Taupo — The Supervolcano Beneath the Lake

Lake Taupo occupies the caldera of one of Earth's most powerful volcanic systems. The Oruanui eruption approximately 26,500 years ago was the largest eruption on Earth in the past 70,000 years, ejecting around 530 km³ of material. The subsequent Taupo eruption of about 232 CE was the most violent eruption globally in the past 5,000 years; its ash cloud turned skies red over Rome and China. Today the caldera is partially submerged under New Zealand's largest lake (616 km²), and renewed uplift of its floor — up to 6 cm per year at times — is monitored as a precursor indicator by GNS Science.

## Tongariro — The World's First Gifted National Park

Tongariro (1,978 m) is an active andesitic stratovolcano in the central North Island and the centrepiece of Tongariro National Park, the fourth oldest national park in the world. The park was gifted by Māori chief Horonuku Te Heuheu Tūkino IV to the New Zealand government in 1887 to prevent private acquisition. Tongariro has erupted more than 70 times in recorded history; recent eruptions occurred in 2012 and 2013, sending ash over Taupō and briefly closing State Highway 1. The Red Crater and Emerald Lakes along the Tongariro Alpine Crossing, one of the world's most-walked day hikes at 19.4 km, are products of this recent hydrothermal and volcanic activity.

## Ngauruhoe — The Cone That Played Mount Doom

Ngauruhoe (2,291 m) is a subsidiary vent of the Tongariro complex and is also the youngest volcano in the Tongariro National Park. It has erupted frequently since 1839, including a continuous period of activity from 1954 to 1955. Its near-perfect black cone was used as the cinematic Mount Doom in Peter Jackson's Lord of the Rings films, though no actual lava scenes were filmed on the mountain. Ngauruhoe is considered active and is monitored alongside Tongariro. The Māori regard it as a tapu (sacred) ancestor and many iwi consider it inappropriate to climb its summit cone.

## Ruapehu — New Zealand's Highest Active Volcano

Mount Ruapehu (2,797 m) is the highest peak in the North Island and has erupted more than 60 times since 1945. A warm acidic crater lake, Crater Lake (Te Wai ā-moe), sits at its summit and behaves as a key indicator of eruptive likelihood: when the lake temperature drops and becomes murkier, eruption risk increases. Ruapehu erupted substantially in 1995–1996, affecting ski fields on its flanks (Whakapapa and Tūroa ski areas) and generating lahars. A major lahar risk existed from a tephra dam blocking the crater lake outlet; the dam broke in 2007, sending a large lahar down the Whangaehu River with minimal warning, fortunately causing no deaths.

## Tauhara — Silent Sentinel of Taupo

Tauhara (1,088 m) sits northeast of Lake Taupo town and is part of the broader Taupo caldera system. It is a lava dome complex rather than a cone, built up over multiple eruptive episodes during the past 20,000 years. Tauhara's flanks are used for geothermal energy extraction; Contact Energy operates the Wairakei and Te Huka geothermal power stations nearby, together producing around 440 MW of electricity. The mountain is visible from most of Taupo town and offers a straightforward day hike with views across the lake to the Tongariro peaks.

## Mayor Island / Tūhua — Obsidian Island of the Pacific

Mayor Island (Tūhua in Māori) is a small shield volcano 35 km off the Bay of Plenty coast, notable for its high-quality obsidian — black volcanic glass — that was traded by Māori across the Pacific. The island's two crater lakes are among the most acidic naturally occurring waters in New Zealand. Tūhua's last eruption is estimated at about 7,000 years ago. The obsidian found here has been detected in archaeological sites as far away as the Chatham Islands, 800 km distant, demonstrating the reach of pre-European maritime trading networks.

## Rotorua — A City Built on Geothermal Activity

Rotorua sits within and around a volcanic caldera that last erupted catastrophically about 220,000 years ago. Today the city is defined by its geothermal surface features: boiling mud pools (pūia), steaming vents on suburban streets, and the large geothermal parks of Wai-O-Tapu (with the Lady Knox Geyser erupting daily at 10:15 am) and Wairakei Terraces. The city's geothermal resource is managed carefully after excessive drawdown in the 1980s caused geyser Pohutu — New Zealand's largest at 30 m — to temporarily lose pressure. Restored management has since stabilised the geyser field at Whakarewarewa.

## Auckland Volcanic Field — Volcanoes in the City

The Auckland metropolitan area of more than 1.6 million people sits on a monogenetic volcanic field of about 53 individual vents, ranging in age from roughly 250,000 years to just 600 years (Rangitoto Island). These are not interconnected conduit volcanoes but separate eruptions from the mantle, meaning the next eruption could occur anywhere in the field — and models suggest Auckland will experience another eruption within the next few thousand years. Rangitoto, rising 260 m from Waitemata Harbour, is the largest and youngest, formed in a single eruption around 600 years ago that was witnessed by Māori.
