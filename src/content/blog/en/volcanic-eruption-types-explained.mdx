Not all volcanic eruptions look the same. Some volcanoes ooze lava in glowing rivers that you can walk alongside at a safe distance. Others detonate with explosions heard across continents, firing clouds of ash and rock high enough to disrupt global aviation. The difference between these extremes is not random — it reflects fundamental variations in magma composition, gas content, and the geometry of the volcanic plumbing system. Understanding eruption styles is the first step to understanding volcanic hazard.

## Why Magma Chemistry Matters

The single most important control on eruption style is silica content. Silica (SiO2) determines viscosity. Basaltic magmas, with around 45 to 52 percent silica, are fluid — they flow readily, allowing dissolved gases to escape gradually. Rhyolitic magmas, with more than 70 percent silica, are sticky and viscous — gases cannot escape easily, and pressure builds until the magma literally explodes. Between these poles sit andesitic and dacitic magmas, with intermediate viscosities that produce a wide range of intermediate eruptive behaviours.

## Hawaiian: Effusive and Spectacular

Hawaiian-style eruptions occur at volcanoes like Kilauea and Mauna Loa on Hawaii, and at Iceland's shield volcanoes such as Bardarbunga and Grimsvötn. The magma is highly fluid basalt, almost completely degassed by the time it reaches the surface. Eruptions produce lava fountains — jets of molten rock hurled tens to hundreds of metres into the air — and long-flowing lava rivers that can travel at 30 kilometres per hour over gentle slopes. Pahoehoe lava, with its smooth ropy surface, and a'a lava, with its rough, clinkery crust, both form in Hawaiian eruptions depending on the flow rate and cooling speed. The hazard is primarily from lava inundation; explosive activity is minimal.

## Strombolian: Rhythmic Fire-Fountains

Named for Stromboli volcano in the Aeolian Islands of southern Italy, Strombolian eruptions are characterised by rhythmic, intermittent explosions typically occurring every few minutes to every few hours. Gas bubbles rise through a relatively fluid basaltic or basaltic-andesitic magma column and burst at the surface, throwing clots of incandescent lava called bombs or scoria to heights of 100 to 300 metres. Stromboli itself has been erupting almost continuously for 2,000 years and is known as the Lighthouse of the Mediterranean. Parícutin in Mexico produced classic Strombolian behaviour during its 1943 to 1952 eruption. The main hazards are falling ballistic bombs and localised wildfires from hot ejecta.

## Vulcanian: Short, Violent Blasts

Vulcanian eruptions are characterised by discrete, violent explosions that last seconds to minutes. They occur when more viscous andesitic or dacitic magma plugs the conduit; pressure builds in a sealed system until the plug fails catastrophically, releasing dense clouds of ash, gas, and pyroclastic material. The eruptive column can reach 5 to 20 kilometres in height. Soufrière Hills volcano on Montserrat in the Caribbean produced repeated Vulcanian explosions during its devastating eruption in the 1990s. Volcanic bombs are a significant hazard; dense ash clouds can travel tens to hundreds of kilometres downwind.

## Pelean: Pyroclastic Flows as the Primary Killer

Named for the 1902 eruption of Mont Pelée on Martinique — which killed around 29,000 people in minutes — Pelean eruptions are dominated by dome collapse events that generate pyroclastic flows. A lava dome grows in the conduit, becomes unstable, and collapses, releasing a fast-moving avalanche of hot gas, ash, and rock that hugs the ground and fills valleys. Temperatures within pyroclastic flows typically exceed 300°C; they travel at 100 to 700 kilometres per hour and are unsurvivable in their direct path. Mount Unzen in Japan and Merapi in Java have produced devastating Pelean pyroclastic flows in recent decades.

## Plinian: Column to the Stratosphere

Plinian eruptions — named for the Roman author Pliny the Younger, who described the AD 79 eruption of Vesuvius that buried Pompeii and Herculaneum — represent the most powerful class of explosive eruption. Highly viscous silica-rich magma fragments explosively as it decompresses, generating a sustained column of ash and gas that can reach 10 to 40 kilometres into the stratosphere. The 1991 eruption of Pinatubo in the Philippines produced a Plinian column of approximately 35 kilometres, injecting 20 megatons of sulphur dioxide into the stratosphere and cooling the global climate by about 0.5°C for two years. The 1883 Krakatau eruption was similarly Plinian. Pyroclastic flows, ashfall over thousands of kilometres, and lahars (volcanic mudflows) are all hazards of Plinian events.

## Sub-Plinian and Ultra-Plinian

Sub-Plinian eruptions produce columns of 10 to 20 kilometres — powerful but not quite reaching the stratosphere in their full extent. Ultra-Plinian events are those with columns exceeding 40 kilometres; the 1815 Tambora eruption achieved a column of approximately 43 kilometres, the highest reliably estimated in modern history. The distinction matters practically: stratospheric injections disperse globally and affect climate for years, while tropospheric ash stays regional and rains out within days.

## Phreatomagmatic: Water Meets Magma

When rising magma encounters groundwater, seawater, or a lake, the result is a phreatomagmatic eruption — a violent steam explosion driven by the instantaneous vaporisation of water in contact with rock at 1,000°C or more. These eruptions are highly efficient at fragmenting magma, producing very fine ash and forming broad, shallow craters called maars. Laacher See in Germany and Kilbourne Hole in New Mexico are classic maar craters. The 2022 Hunga Tonga-Hunga Ha'apai eruption in the South Pacific was one of the most powerful phreatomagmatic events in the modern record, generating a pressure wave that circled the globe multiple times.
