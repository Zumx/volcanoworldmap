Beneath the Hellisheiði plateau southwest of Reykjavík, a web of drill pipes reaches two to three kilometres into the earth, drawing up superheated steam at temperatures above 300°C. This is the Hellisheiðarvirkjun power plant, the world's third-largest geothermal facility, and it illustrates a principle that a handful of volcanically active nations have turned into a national energy strategy: the same molten-rock processes that make volcanoes hazardous also make them inexhaustible heat sources, available around the clock regardless of whether the sun shines or the wind blows.

## How Volcanic Heat Becomes Electricity

Geothermal power exploits the heat stored in and around magmatic bodies in the crust. In high-enthalpy volcanic fields — the kind found in Iceland, Kenya's East African Rift, El Salvador's volcanic arc, New Zealand's Taupo Volcanic Zone, and the Philippines — groundwater percolating downward is heated far above boiling point under pressure. When wells intersect these reservoirs, the pressure drop causes rapid flashing to steam, which drives turbines. Dry-steam plants work where reservoirs produce steam directly; flash-steam plants handle liquid-dominated reservoirs; binary-cycle systems capture lower-temperature fluid and use it to vaporise a secondary working fluid with a lower boiling point, extending the range of exploitable resources.

## Iceland: A Nation Powered by Fire and Ice

Iceland generates virtually all its electricity from hydro and geothermal sources, with geothermal contributing roughly 30 percent of the electricity supply and close to 90 percent of space heating for the country's buildings. The Svartsengi power plant near Grindavík — whose waste water created the famous Blue Lagoon — has been operating since 1976. Hellisheiðarvirkjun, opened in 2006 and expanded to 303 MW electric plus 400 MW thermal, supplies Reykjavík's district heating directly. The volcanic systems of the Western Volcanic Zone, the Northern Volcanic Zone, and the Eastern Volcanic Zone together underpin an energy system that has made Iceland one of the lowest per-capita carbon emitters in Europe. The 2021–2024 Fagradalsfjall and Sundhnúkur eruption sequences posed no direct threat to the geothermal grid, though the 2023 Grindavík crisis required contingency planning for Svartsengi.

## Kenya: East African Rift as Power Source

Kenya's Olkaria geothermal field, within Hell's Gate National Park in the Gregory Rift, has grown from a 45 MW pilot plant in 1981 to over 800 MW of installed capacity across five operational power stations as of 2024, making it the largest geothermal complex in Africa. The field sits above a young volcanic system — Olkaria Volcanic Complex — whose last eruptions date to within the past few thousand years. The Kenyan government aims for 5,000 MW of geothermal by 2030 as part of a national decarbonisation strategy. The Menengai caldera north of Nakuru is being developed by the Geothermal Development Company of Kenya and could add another 400 MW. Geothermal now provides roughly 47 percent of Kenya's installed generating capacity, making the country one of the top five geothermal producers in the world.

## El Salvador: The Smallest Country, a Major Producer

El Salvador, a nation of 21,000 square kilometres straddling the Central American Volcanic Front, derives around 23–26 percent of its electricity from geothermal energy — one of the highest proportions in the world by installed renewable fraction. The LaGeo company operates the Ahuachapán and Berlín geothermal plants, with a combined installed capacity near 200 MW. The Berlin plant sits immediately adjacent to the Santa Ana–Llamatepec volcanic complex, whose 2005 eruption deposited tephra over parts of the geothermal concession but caused no lasting damage to infrastructure.

## New Zealand and the Philippines

New Zealand's Wairakei power station, commissioned in 1958, was the world's first commercial geothermal plant to use wet steam. Today the Taupo Volcanic Zone hosts roughly 950 MW of geothermal capacity, supplying about 17–18 percent of national electricity. The Philippines generate approximately 1,900 MW from geothermal — predominantly from the Leyte Geothermal Production Field on the volcanic island of Leyte and the Tiwi and Mak-Ban fields on Luzon — making it the second-largest geothermal producer in Asia after Indonesia.

## Drilling Technology and Sustainability

Modern geothermal development employs directional drilling to reach multiple reservoir zones from a single pad, reducing surface footprint. Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS) inject water into hot dry rock formations lacking natural permeability, dramatically expanding the geographic range of exploitable resources. The Krafla IDDP-1 borehole in Iceland struck a magma pocket at 2.1 km depth in 2009, producing superheated steam at over 450°C — demonstrating that magma-enhanced geothermal could theoretically multiply energy yields tenfold. Iceland's ongoing IDDP-2 project at Reykjanes aims to harness supercritical fluid at 4–5 km depth for the same purpose.

## Environmental Trade-Offs

Geothermal is not without environmental costs. The reinjection of cooled brine, standard practice today, prevents reservoir depletion and ground subsidence — lessons learned at Wairakei, which experienced significant subsidence in its early decades without reinjection. Geothermal fluids contain dissolved hydrogen sulphide; modern plants capture it and convert it to sulphur or inject it back underground. At Hellisheiðarvirkjun, the CarbFix project captures CO2 and H2S from the plant's gas stream and mineralises them in basaltic rock underground within two years, a world-first carbon-mineralisation operation at industrial scale.
