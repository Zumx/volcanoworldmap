Few people associate Syria with volcanoes, yet the country's southern interior is dominated by one of the world's largest and geologically youngest lava plateaux. The Harrat Ash Shaam — sometimes called the Hauran plateau — extends from southern Syria across the Jordanian border and into Saudi Arabia, covering some 45,000 square kilometres with layers of basalt erupted over the past 20 million years, with the most recent activity occurring just a few thousand years ago. The landscape is dramatic in the manner of all young lava fields: black and rust-coloured rock broken by dozens of cinder cones, maar craters, and the ruins of ancient settlements whose builders quarried the readily available basalt.

## Jabal Seis — The Highest Cone

Jabal Seis (جبل سيس) is the dominant volcanic landform on the Syrian plateau, rising to approximately 1,240 metres and visible from great distances across the flat Harrat. It is a well-preserved Pleistocene scoria cone whose flanks are covered in loose red and black cinder. The cone sits near the historical caravan routes that crossed the plateau, and ancient inscriptions and rock art have been found on the weathered basalt around its base.

## Tall Abu-Safa — A Maar on the Plateau

Tall Abu-Safa is a maar crater formed when rising magma encountered groundwater and generated a phreatomagmatic explosion that blasted out a broad, shallow depression. The maar's rim is low and nearly circular, and the interior has accumulated sediment that now supports a seasonal wetland — a striking contrast against the surrounding black lava. Sediment cores from the maar's floor contain a pollen record stretching back thousands of years, providing a window into past climate on the plateau.

## Tulul Abu Qadqad — Twin Cones

The Tulul Abu Qadqad (تلول أم قضقاض) are a pair of closely spaced scoria cones that erupted along a common fissure, creating a north-south aligned ridge of dark lava. The flanks of the cones are littered with well-formed lava bombs ejected during the eruptions, ranging from spindle shapes to flattened pancake forms that solidified mid-flight. A basalt lava flow extends east from the base of the complex for several kilometres before disappearing beneath alluvial sediment.

## Tal Dakwa and the Northern Vents

At the northern edge of the Syrian lava province, Tal Dakwa (تل الدكوة) and the nearby cones represent the most recent volcanic activity in the region. The freshness of the lava surfaces here — minimal desert varnish, sharp-edged clinker — suggests eruptions considerably younger than those in the centre of the plateau. Locally, these black lava fields are known as the Leja (the refuge), and they sheltered Druze communities and rebel fighters throughout the nineteenth and twentieth centuries precisely because the terrain is nearly impassable to cavalry and wheeled vehicles.

## Tall Shayba — Isolated Cone of the Eastern Plain

Tall Shayba (تل شيبة) rises from the flat plain east of the main Harrat Ash Shaam lava field, an isolated cinder cone that marks the eastern limit of documented Quaternary volcanism in Syria. Its isolation makes it a distinctive navigation landmark visible across many kilometres of featureless plain, and it was likely used as an orientation point by ancient travellers crossing the Syrian Desert.

## Al-Khanifis — Ancient Basalt and Rock Art

The volcanic feature at Al-Khanifis (الخنيفس) combines geological interest with a remarkable concentration of ancient rock art and inscriptions. The site preserves basaltic surfaces engraved with Safaitic script — a pre-Islamic Arabian writing system used by semi-nomadic peoples who grazed their herds across the lava plateau. The connection between volcanic landscape and human history here is unusually direct: the black basalt provided both shelter from the wind and a durable writing surface.

## Tall Jafar and the Central Cluster

Tall Jafar (تل جعفر) sits within a cluster of vents near the geographical centre of the Syrian Harrat, where eruption episodes created overlapping lava flows from multiple closely spaced cones. The resulting terrain is particularly complex, with pressure ridges, lava tubes, and skylights visible at the surface. The central cluster also contains the cone of Tall al-Alaq (تل العلق), whose summit offers one of the best panoramic views over the extent of the plateau.

## Umm Qrabba — Southern Maar Fields

Towards the southern border of Syria, Umm Qrabba (أم عقربة) is one of several maar craters that mark a zone of phreatomagmatic activity where the water table intersected rising basaltic magma. The crater rim is largely intact and the maar floor, roughly 200 metres below the rim, accumulates seasonal rainwater. In the surrounding area, the features Talitatawat Shamaliyyi (تليطاوات شمالي) and Talitatawat Qibli (تليطاوات قبلي) add further interest to the southern volcanic province.

## Al-Mahfur — The Drained Lava Lake

Al-Mahfur (المحفور) — literally the excavated — takes its name from the drained lava lake that occupies its interior. During an eruption, a lava pond formed within a pit crater; as the eruption waned, the still-molten interior drained back down through the feeder conduit, leaving a shell of solidified lava clinging to the walls and a spectacular empty hollow. The resulting structure, accessible on foot, is one of the most vivid examples of a drained lava lake in the Levant.

## Jabal Mankhar al-Sharqi — The Eastern Horn

Jabal Mankhar al-Sharqi (جبل منخار الشرقي) stands at the eastern edge of Syria's volcanic province, its elongated ridge form suggesting eruption from a linear fissure rather than a central vent. The basaltic rock here shows excellent columnar jointing where post-eruption cooling cracked the lava into polygonal prisms, creating natural walls that ancient herders used to build enclosures for their livestock. The Mankhar ridge is also notable for the unusual petrology of its lavas, which show signs of mixing between two magma batches of different compositions.
