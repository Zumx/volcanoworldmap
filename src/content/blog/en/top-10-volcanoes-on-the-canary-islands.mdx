The Canary Islands form one of the Atlantic's most studied volcanic archipelagos. Each island is essentially the summit of a submarine shield volcano built over tens of millions of years on the African plate as it drifts slowly over a mantle hotspot off the northwest African coast. The resulting islands range from the ancient and heavily eroded (Fuerteventura, Lanzarote) to the geologically young and still erupting (La Palma, El Hierro). Teide on Tenerife, at 3,718 m, is the most visited national park in Spain and hardly needs introduction; here are the ten other volcanic highlights of the seven-island archipelago.

## Cumbre Vieja, La Palma

In September 2021 the Cumbre Vieja volcanic ridge on La Palma erupted after 50 years of quiet, sending lava flows westward that buried more than 3,000 buildings, destroyed the port of Todoque and added about 50 hectares of new land to the island's southwest coast before the eruption ended on 25 December 2021 — 85 days later. The eruption was La Palma's longest in recorded history. Today the lava field is cool enough to walk, and guided tours visit the new coastline and inspect lava tube entries.

## Caldera de Taburiente, La Palma

Caldera de Taburiente is not a true caldera but an enormous erosion basin 9 km across and 770 m deep, carved into the older northern shield of La Palma. The encircling walls reach up to 2,426 m (Roque de los Muchachos), home to the Roque de los Muchachos Observatory, one of the world's premier optical telescope sites chosen for its exceptional atmospheric clarity. Basalt dykes and columnar structures are exposed in the caldera walls, a cross-section through millions of years of eruptions.

## Montañas del Fuego, Lanzarote

Between 1730 and 1736 the Timanfaya eruptions remade a quarter of Lanzarote's land surface with lava flows and cinder fields. The event lasted six years and produced one of the most bizarre and beautiful volcanic landscapes in Europe. Today the Timanfaya National Park preserves this field of black lava, red cones and surreal desert that local artist César Manrique used as the backdrop for an architectural vision of human creativity in dialogue with raw geology. Geothermal heat remains intense: grills above natural vents cook meat for the park restaurant.

## Corona Lava Tube, Lanzarote

The Jameos del Agua and Cueva de los Verdes cave system on Lanzarote's northern coast is part of the Túnel de la Atlántida, a lava tube nearly 1.5 km long that extends from the summit of Corona volcano to below sea level, making it the world's longest known volcanic sea tunnel. The submerged portion hosts a blind albino crab, Munidopsis polymorpha, found nowhere else on Earth. The tunnel is a reminder that the volcanic structures of the Canaries extend deep beneath the surface and out to the seafloor.

## Caldera de los Cuervos, Lanzarote

Among the more than 300 volcanic cones on Lanzarote, the Caldera de los Cuervos in the Timanfaya area offers the best-preserved cross-section of a recent scoria cone. Its rim, about 100 m above the surrounding lava field, is intact and easy to walk. The interior of the crater is a bowl of red and black cinder, and the flanks preserve the layered tephra stratigraphy of its brief but violent eruption in the 1730s.

## Caldera de Bandama, Gran Canaria

Gran Canaria's most striking volcanic feature is the Caldera de Bandama, a collapse crater about 1 km wide and 200 m deep in the island's northeast. At its floor, a farmer maintains a smallholding sheltered from the trade winds. The encircling rim reaches 574 m at Pico de Bandama, and the rim trail gives a complete panorama of the caldera and views across to the capital Las Palmas de Gran Canaria. The caldera formed about 2,000 years ago.

## Montana Roja and Jandía, Fuerteventura

Fuerteventura is the oldest of the main Canary Islands, its original shield largely eroded, but volcanic activity continued into the Holocene. Montana Roja, a 196-m red scoria cone at the island's southern tip near Playa de Jandía, erupted perhaps 10,000 years ago and its perfectly preserved form stands in striking contrast to the white sand beaches below. Several other young cones are visible from the road across the Jandía Peninsula.

## Pico de la Zarza and La Pared, Fuerteventura

Pico de la Zarza (807 m) is Fuerteventura's highest point and the summit of the Jandía Massif. Although geologically ancient, the views from the summit on clear days stretch to Gran Canaria and La Palma. The geological boundary at La Pared, a thin isthmus between north and south Fuerteventura, marks the junction of two separate ancient shield volcanoes, now joined by blown sand.

## El Golfo and Las Montañetas, El Hierro

El Hierro experienced a submarine eruption in 2011–2012 south of La Restinga, the first confirmed eruption in historical time associated with this island. Discoloured water, dead fish and new shoal areas marked the seafloor activity. On the island's surface, El Golfo is a dramatic eroded caldera open to the north Atlantic, its floor filled with farmland 600 m below the rim. Las Montañetas to the southeast are young scoria cones rising from the Llanos de Aridane plain.

## Calderas of La Gomera

La Gomera is the only Canary Island with no eruption in human prehistory. Its volcanic activity ended roughly 4 million years ago and its landscape is shaped by massive erosion rather than recent lava. The Garajonay National Park, a UNESCO World Heritage site, covers the misty summit plateau of the island with ancient laurisilva forest — a relict ecosystem that once covered large parts of the Mediterranean. The caldera rim trails offer dramatic views of sea fog filling the valleys below.
