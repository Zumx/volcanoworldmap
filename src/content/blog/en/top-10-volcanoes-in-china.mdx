Stand on the rim of Tianchi — Heaven Lake — at 2,189 metres on Changbai Mountain, and the scale of China's volcanic heritage becomes visceral. The caldera stretches nearly 5 kilometres across, filled with some of the deepest and clearest water in East Asia. In 946 CE, the Millennium Eruption here ejected so much ash that traces were found in Greenland ice cores, making it one of the largest volcanic events of the past two thousand years. Yet Changbai is only the most famous entry in a catalogue that runs from the frozen north-east to the tropical south.

## Changbai and the Tianchi Caldera

Changbai Mountain (also written Paektu in Korean) straddles the Chinese-North Korean border in Jilin Province. The summit caldera holds Tianchi, a lake that freezes each winter and thaws into a mirror of startling blue each June. Sulphurous fumaroles still hiss near the north slope, and the mountain remains on volcanological watch lists. Visitors reach the northern Chinese rim via a paved road to 2,670 metres, then descend steep wooden steps to lake level.

## Wudalianchi — Five Connected Lakes

In northern Heilongjiang, two eruption episodes in 1719–1721 sent lava flows blocking the Bei River, creating the five interconnected lakes that give Wudalianchi its name. Fourteen shield and scoria cones dominate the landscape, their basaltic lava fields still raw and largely unweathered. The area is a UNESCO Global Geopark and the springs that seep through the lava are marketed as medicinal mineral water throughout China.

## Hainan Island Shield Fields

The island of Hainan harbours one of the largest Quaternary basaltic provinces in southern China. The volcanic cluster around Haikou and Chengmai includes the cones of Mount Fengluling, 雷虎岭 (Lei Hu Ling), and 大空山 (Da Kong Shan) as well as dozens of smaller satellitic vents including 小空山 (Xiao Kong Shan) and 眼镜岭 (Yanjing Ling). Lava tube caves thread under Shishan township; the longest tube exceeds 500 metres. Eruptions here are estimated to have occurred within the past 10,000 years, technically classifying several cones as potentially active.

## Tengchong — Yunnan's Geothermal Frontier

Tengchong sits in western Yunnan near the Myanmar border and hosts 97 volcanoes and more than 80 hot-spring groups in a 150-kilometre belt. The Dakong Shan and Xiaokong Shan cones near Maanshan rise above dense subtropical forest. Rehai — the Hot Sea geothermal park — features boiling pools, steam vents, and the Daguoquo geyser. Tengchong's volcanic soils support terraced rice paddies that glow acid-green in the wet season, making it one of China's most photogenic volcanic landscapes.

## Xinjiang's Remote High-Altitude Vents

The Ashikule volcanic field in Xinjiang occupies a high-altitude basin at roughly 4,700 metres on the Tibetan Plateau. The field contains multiple vents including 阿什库勒一号火山 and 阿什库勒三号火山, alongside the satellite peaks of Pulu. Access requires crossing difficult terrain and obtaining permits for the restricted border zone. Satellite monitoring has detected hydrothermal anomalies suggesting residual heat. The Subashi volcanic group — 苏贝希1号火山 and 苏贝希2号火山 — adds further interest to this little-visited province.

## Jingpohu and the Cascading Lava Flows of Mudanjiang

Near the city of Mudanjiang in Heilongjiang, the Jingpohu lava field preserves a remarkable landscape where Quaternary flows dammed the Mudan River and created a lake 45 kilometres long. Columnar basalt cliffs up to 30 metres high line the river gorge downstream. The area protects fossils of Pleistocene fauna caught beneath the flows and offers one of the most accessible lava-field walks in the country.

## Leiqiong and the Cross-Strait Basalts

The Leiqiong UNESCO Global Geopark spans the Leizhou Peninsula in Guangdong and the northern tip of Hainan, with more than a hundred classified volcanic features across both sides of the Qiongzhou Strait. Recent geochronological dating of lavas at 罗京盘火山口 places some flows at under 10,000 years old. The cultural landscape is equally rich: ancient lava-built houses still stand in Shishan village, using vesicular basalt blocks quarried from local cones.

## Mud Volcanoes of the Qiongdao Basin

Although hydraulic rather than magmatic, China's mud volcanoes are volcanologically significant. A cluster in the Qiongdao Basin and adjacent areas includes 一号泥火山, 二号泥火山, 三号泥火山, and a series of smaller vents numbered through to 十六号泥火山. Methane and saline sediment-charged water periodically erupt from these features, occasionally with enough force to reshape the surrounding plain. They are studied as analogues for extraterrestrial cold volcanic features.

## Da Hei Shan and Ma Ti Shan in Inner Mongolia

The volcanic cluster around 大黑山火山 (Da Hei Shan) and 马蹄山火山 (Ma Ti Shan) in Inner Mongolia forms part of the Greater Khingan Range volcanic province. Cinder cones here erupted during the late Pleistocene, creating a mosaic of lava fields now colonised by pine and birch forest. The region sees almost no international visitors and offers unmediated access to raw scoria landscapes, crater lakes, and the silence of China's vast north.

## Shandong's Ancient Volcanic Archipelago

The Shandong Peninsula and adjacent Yellow Sea preserve remnants of older volcanic activity, including the Linqu basaltic province where erosion has exhumed once-buried cones and exposed thick columnar lava stacks. While the Shandong volcanoes are too old to be considered active, they provide crucial evidence of the long-running tectonic rifting that created the North China Craton and continue to be worked by geologists seeking to understand the region's deep history.
