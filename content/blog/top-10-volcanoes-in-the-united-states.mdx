The United States straddles the Pacific Ring of Fire and a continental hotspot,
giving it active lava, glaciated stratovolcanoes, and a supervolcano. These ten
are the headline acts.

## 1. Kīlauea, Hawaii

Among the most active volcanoes in the world, where lava can sometimes be
watched safely within a national park.

## 2. Mauna Loa, Hawaii

The largest active volcano on Earth by volume — its scale is almost impossible
to perceive from its own flanks.

## 3. Mount St. Helens, Washington

The 1980 eruption reshaped volcanology; its breached crater is now an open-air
laboratory.

## 4. Mount Rainier, Washington

A glacier-armoured giant over Seattle — scenic, and among the most dangerous US
volcanoes for lahar risk.

## 5. Yellowstone, Wyoming

A caldera supervolcano whose geyser basins are the visible breath of a colossal
magma system.

## 6. Mount Shasta, California

A vast solitary Cascade cone, visible across a hundred miles of northern
California.

## 7. Lassen Peak, California

The Cascades' other 20th-century eruptor, surrounded by mudpots and fumaroles.

## 8. Crater Lake (Mazama), Oregon

A collapsed volcano cradling the deepest lake in the US — a caldera you stand
inside.

## 9. Mount Hood, Oregon

Portland's glaciated icon, climbed and skied year-round.

## 10. Novarupta / Katmai, Alaska

Site of the largest volcanic eruption of the 20th century — the Valley of
Ten Thousand Smokes.

## How to visit US volcanoes safely

Most sit within national parks or monuments with visitor centres and ranger
programmes. Always check the USGS Volcano Hazards Program status and the park
alerts before you go — Kīlauea activity, gas, and closures change rapidly.

## Timing and altitude

Cascade volcanoes are best mid-summer when lower trails clear; Hawaii is
year-round. Several summits (Rainier, Shasta, Mauna Loa) are serious
high-altitude climbs needing skills and permits — but the viewpoints below them
are spectacular without the ascent.

## Respect the hazard and the place

Stay on marked trails in thermal areas (Yellowstone's crust is literally thin
rock over boiling water), never approach active lava off designated areas, and
treat Hawaiian and tribal sites as sacred — take nothing, including rocks.
