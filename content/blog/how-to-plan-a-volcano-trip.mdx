A volcano trip is unique: the destination can change its own access overnight.
Good planning builds flexibility around hazard status, season, and permits from
the start.

## Step 1: Be honest about the level you want

Three very different trips: viewpoint/park (a walk), crater-rim hike (a hard
day), and summit ascent (mountaineering, guides, permits). Most travellers want
the first two but assume they need the third. Choose deliberately — it changes
everything.

## Step 2: Map the targets

Open the [map](/), filter to your region, and see how volcanoes cluster — the
Cascades, the Andes, the Indonesian arc, Iceland, the Canaries. A cluster
enables a multi-volcano route; an isolated giant is a single objective. The
elevation on each marker tells you walk versus climb.

## Step 3: Build around alert status

This is what makes volcano planning different. Identify the monitoring agency
(USGS, PVMBG, the local observatory), bookmark its alert page, and check before
booking and again before you go. Always carry a non-volcanic backup day —
eruptions and closures ignore itineraries.

## Step 4: Get the season right

Stratovolcanoes need their snow-free window; tropical volcanoes have dry-season
sunrise windows; cloud is the hidden enemy everywhere. Research the clearest
months, not just the warmest, and time sunrise hikes to the driest period.

## Step 5: Sort permits and guides

Many active volcanoes require permits, mandatory licensed guides, or organised
treks, and some cap daily numbers (Rinjani, Andean and Indonesian peaks).
Arrange this months ahead — it is the usual bottleneck.

## Step 6: Plan the timing of day

The best volcano experiences are at dawn — clear air, glowing vents, crowds not
yet arrived. Build pre-sunrise starts into the plan and treat midday as travel
and rest. Headtorches are essential.

## Step 7: Pack for the conditions

Sturdy boots for abrasive ground, sun and wind protection, ample water, warm
layers (summits are cold at any latitude), a dust mask or buff for ash, and gas
awareness — know what sulphur in a hollow means and act on it.

## Step 8: Respect culture and conservation

Volcanoes are frequently sacred and almost always protected. Take no rocks,
build no cairns, stay on trails, follow Indigenous and park guidance, and obey
alert levels without exception.

## Put it together

Decide the level, map the cluster, anchor to alert status and season, lock the
permits, keep a backup. Open the [map](/), draw the route, and a volcano trip
becomes one that bends gracefully when the mountain does.
